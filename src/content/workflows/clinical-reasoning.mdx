## The Problem

Diagnostic error contributes to an estimated **10-15% of clinical encounters** and remains a leading cause of malpractice claims and preventable harm. The root cause is rarely missing knowledge; it's cognitive. Anchoring, premature closure, availability bias, and base-rate neglect all narrow the differential before you've fully considered it.

Unlike note-writing workflows where AI primarily saves time, clinical reasoning is where AI can **improve diagnostic accuracy**. LLMs don't anchor on the first abnormal lab. They don't get fatigued at 2 AM. They consider diagnoses you may have already unconsciously dismissed.

This workflow walks you through a 4-step process for using AI as a diagnostic reasoning partner, from case presentation through workup planning and synthesis.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  Never paste identifiable patient information (name, DOB, MRN, dates of service) into a consumer AI tool. De-identify all data or confirm your organization has a BAA in place with the vendor before entering any clinical details. See our [tool reviews](/tools) for BAA availability by vendor.
</Callout>

<Callout type="evidence">
  A 2023 JAMA Internal Medicine study found that LLMs generated differential diagnoses that included the correct final diagnosis more frequently than physician-generated lists in standardized case vignettes. See the [full trial summary](/trials/llm-clinical-reasoning-jama-2023) for methodology and limitations.
</Callout>

## Step 1: Present the Case

AI reasoning quality is directly proportional to input quality. Before prompting, structure your clinical data into a complete case presentation:

- **Demographics**: age, sex, relevant social context
- **Chief complaint and HPI**: timeline, character, associated symptoms, aggravating/alleviating factors
- **Past medical and surgical history**: include medications and allergies
- **Family and social history**: when relevant to the differential
- **Physical exam**: vital signs, pertinent positive and negative findings
- **Studies to date**: labs, imaging, ECG, pathology

**Recommended models:** Claude or GPT-4o both handle structured case input well. The key is your input, not the model choice at this stage.

<Callout type="tip">
  Pertinent negatives matter. Stating "no rash, no joint pain, no oral ulcers" in a fever workup meaningfully constrains the differential. Don't omit findings just because they were normal; include the ones that are diagnostically relevant.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="For Trainees">
  Practice structuring your own case presentation before relying on the AI to organize it. The discipline of creating a coherent clinical narrative is itself a diagnostic reasoning skill.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Generate the Differential

With your structured case, use the [DDx Generator template](/templates/ddx-generator) to prompt the model for a comprehensive differential diagnosis.

**Recommended model: o3.** OpenAI's o3 uses extended chain-of-thought reasoning that is particularly well-suited to differential diagnosis generation. It systematically works through pathophysiologic mechanisms and considers diagnoses that pattern-matching alone would miss.

Claude excels at synthesizing complex narrative data and tends to provide well-organized output with appropriate clinical nuance, making it a strong alternative when the case involves extensive chart review or ambiguous findings. GPT-4o is fast and capable for straightforward presentations but may generate a less exhaustive differential for atypical or multisystem cases compared to o3's deliberative process.

For a detailed head-to-head analysis of model performance across case complexity levels, see the [full comparison guide](/guides/llm-clinical-reasoning-comparison).

<Callout type="tip">
  Explicitly ask the model to include "uncommon but dangerous diagnoses that should not be missed." LLMs tend to weight common diagnoses heavily by default, and this prompt modifier reliably surfaces critical rule-outs like aortic dissection, mesenteric ischemia, or pulmonary embolism that anchoring might cause you to overlook.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="For Trainees">
  Write your own differential first. Then compare it against the AI output. The learning happens in the gap: the diagnoses the AI considered that you didn't, and understanding *why* you missed them, is where clinical reasoning improves.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Plan the Workup

Once you have a refined differential, use the [Workup Planner template](/templates/workup-planner) to generate a prioritized diagnostic workup. The template structures testing by urgency (emergent rule-outs vs. routine workup) and maps each test to the diagnoses it helps confirm or exclude.

**Recommended models:** o3 or Claude. o3 is methodical about test-to-diagnosis mapping. Claude is particularly good at explaining the rationale behind each test and flagging when a single study can address multiple diagnoses simultaneously.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  LLMs don't know your institution's test availability, turnaround times, or cost constraints. A model may recommend serum galactomannan or a tagged RBC scan that your facility doesn't offer, or suggest "stat MRI" when your scanner has a 3-day wait. Always filter the AI workup through local logistics before ordering.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip" title="For Trainees">
  Before looking at the AI workup plan, decide what you would order and why. Then use the AI output to identify tests you hadn't considered and evaluate whether they change your pre-test probability for key diagnoses.
</Callout>

---

## Step 4: Synthesize and Decide

As results return, use the [Case Synthesis template](/templates/case-synthesis) to integrate new data with your working differential. Feed the model the original case, your differential, and the new findings. Ask it to re-rank the differential, identify diagnoses that have been effectively ruled out, and highlight any new diagnoses raised by unexpected results.

**Recommended model: Claude.** Claude is the strongest model for integrating large volumes of complex clinical data with appropriate epistemic hedging. It reliably distinguishes between "this is most likely" and "this cannot be excluded," a critical distinction in clinical reasoning that other models sometimes flatten into false certainty.

This is the step where the AI is most clearly a **reasoning partner, not a decision-maker**. The synthesis should inform your clinical judgment, not replace it. You are integrating the AI's analysis with information it doesn't have: the patient's appearance, your gestalt, the family conversation, the nursing observations.

Consider using the [Cognitive Debiasing workflow](/workflows/cognitive-debiasing) at this stage to explicitly check for anchoring, confirmation bias, and premature closure before finalizing your assessment and plan.
