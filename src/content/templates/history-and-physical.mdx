## When to use this

Use this template when admitting a patient and you need a complete history and physical examination note for the medical record. Paste in your raw clinical data (HPI details, past history, exam findings, labs, imaging) and the LLM organizes it into a structured H&P with a problem-based assessment and plan. Particularly useful on high-volume admission nights when the documentation backlog starts to outpace the clinical work.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, admission/discharge dates, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="History and Physical">
You are an excellent physician writing a comprehensive admission history and physical note. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a complete H&P.

### Rules

- Write the HPI as a detailed narrative paragraph in past tense. Include time course, symptom onset, progression, associated symptoms, pertinent positives and negatives, and what brought the patient to the hospital.
- Write the ROS as a brief list of pertinent positives and negatives not already covered in the HPI. Do not repeat HPI content in the ROS.
- Write the Physical Exam by system in concise paragraph prose. Report all findings provided; do not invent findings.
- Write the Assessment as a one-line summary followed by a numbered problem list.
- Write the Plan by problem, with specific, actionable items under each problem.
- Include specific numbers (lab values, vitals, doses, dates) whenever available.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never spell out what an abbreviation stands for.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. If data is missing, write "[Obtain and document before signing]".
- All historical sections (HPI, PMHx, PSHx, etc.) must be in past tense. Physical Exam and Assessment/Plan use present tense where appropriate.
- Include all medication details: name, dose, route, frequency.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- "pshx" = past surgical history
- "fhx" = family history
- "shx" = social history
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Specialty focus | Add: "This is a [cardiology/pulmonology/GI] admission. Emphasize [specialty]-relevant history and exam findings." |
| HPI length | Add: "Keep the HPI to 8-10 sentences maximum" or "Write a comprehensive HPI with full timeline." |
| Assessment depth | Add: "Include a brief differential diagnosis for each problem." |
| Trainee documentation | Add: "Include attestation statement: 'I have personally seen and examined the patient and reviewed the resident's note. I agree with the findings and plan as documented with the following additions/modifications: [***]'" |
| Exam completeness | Add: "Include a complete 14-system ROS and 12-system physical exam for this admission." |

## Variations

### ED-to-floor admission

Use this when admitting a patient from the emergency department who has already been evaluated, treated, and dispositioned by the ED team.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="ED-to-Floor Admission H&P">
You are an excellent physician writing an admission H&P for a patient being admitted from the emergency department. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a complete H&P that incorporates the ED evaluation and your own assessment.

### Rules

- Write the HPI as a detailed narrative paragraph in past tense. Include the patient's initial presentation, ED evaluation (labs, imaging, interventions), ED diagnosis, and reason for admission.
- Clearly distinguish between the ED assessment and your own admission assessment when they differ.
- Write in past tense for historical sections; present tense for current exam and plan.
- Include specific numbers (lab values, vitals, doses, dates) whenever available.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "[Obtain and document before signing]" for missing data.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

───────────────
***

───────────────────────────

  Arrival vitals: T ***  HR ***  BP ***  RR ***  SpO2 ***

### Subspecialty admission

## Tips & pitfalls

- **HPI narrative structure.** The HPI should read as a story with a clear arc: who the patient is, what happened, and how they ended up in the hospital. If the LLM produces a bulleted list, re-run with emphasis on "narrative paragraph."
- **ROS duplication.** The model frequently repeats HPI content in the ROS. Scan for duplication and remove it; a good ROS adds information, not echoes.
- **Fabricated exam findings.** The AI may generate "normal" findings for systems you did not examine. If you did not examine it, it should say "[Deferred]," not "unremarkable."
- **Lab value accuracy.** Always cross-reference lab values against the actual chart. The model may transpose digits, round values, or infer reference ranges.
- **Assessment one-liner.** The model sometimes writes an overly generic summary. The one-liner should state the most likely diagnosis or clinical problem, not just repeat the chief complaint.
- **Medication reconciliation.** The AI may omit home medications or add medications not on the patient's list. Cross-reference the full med list before signing.
- **Prior workup gaps.** For patients with complex histories, the model may not include relevant prior studies (TTE, colonoscopy, biopsy results). Add these manually if they inform the current admission.
