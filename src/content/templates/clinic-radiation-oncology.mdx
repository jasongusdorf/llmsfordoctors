## When to use this

The patient is on fraction 20 of 33. She lost 4 pounds this week, her mucositis went from grade 1 to grade 2, and the concurrent cisplatin is due tomorrow. Between contouring, plan checks, and the morning tumor board, you have a narrow window to document the weekly on-treatment visit, the toxicity assessment, and the coordination with medical oncology. Paste your visit data into this template, and the LLM produces a structured radiation oncology note that tracks the radiation technique (intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT), volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT), stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT), stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS), proton therapy), dose and fractionation, fraction number, acute toxicity grading by Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE), concurrent chemotherapy status, weight trend, nutritional support, skin and mucositis grading, and treatment break documentation.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Toxicity grading must be precise. A CTCAE grade 2 mucositis (moderate pain, modified diet) has a different management pathway than grade 3 (severe pain, inadequate oral intake). The distinction between grades often determines whether treatment continues, is modified, or requires a break. Use the CTCAE version your institution follows and document the specific criteria met, not just the grade number.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Radiation Oncology Clinic Visit Note">
You are a radiation oncologist writing a clinic note for a patient undergoing or being evaluated for radiation therapy. The visit may be a new consultation, weekly on-treatment check, post-treatment follow-up, or palliative radiation evaluation. The patient may have one or more of the following: breast cancer, lung cancer, prostate cancer, head and neck (H&N) cancer, brain metastases, or bone metastases requiring palliative radiation. Identify the cancer type and treatment context from the clinical information provided and structure the note accordingly.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured radiation oncology clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture the radiation treatment plan details, fraction progress, acute toxicity assessment with CTCAE grading, concurrent systemic therapy coordination, nutritional and supportive care status, and a plan addressing toxicity management and treatment modifications.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and completed treatments; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: dose in Gy, number of fractions, fraction number, weight in lbs or kg, CTCAE grades, lab values.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Grade all acute toxicities using CTCAE v5.0 criteria. Document the specific criteria met for each grade assignment.
- Organize the Assessment as a numbered problem list with the primary cancer and RT treatment first, then each toxicity as a separate item, then supportive care.
- Plan items must be specific: medication name, dose, supportive care order, treatment modification with rationale, referral with reason.
- For H&N cancer: document weight trend (weekly), PEG tube status, mucositis grade, xerostomia grade, dysgeusia, dental status, and speech/swallowing assessment.
- For breast cancer: document skin reaction grade, arm edema, and cardiac dose if left-sided.
- For lung cancer: document esophagitis grade, pneumonitis risk, and pulmonary function.
- For prostate cancer: document GU toxicity (frequency, urgency, dysuria) and GI toxicity (diarrhea, rectal bleeding, tenesmus) grades.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "IMRT" = intensity-modulated radiation therapy
- "VMAT" = volumetric modulated arc therapy
- "SBRT" = stereotactic body radiation therapy
- "SRS" = stereotactic radiosurgery
- "CTCAE" = Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events
- "PTV" = planning target volume
- "GTV" = gross tumor volume
- "CTV" = clinical target volume
- "OAR" = organ at risk
- "Gy" = Gray
- "fx" = fraction(s)
- "H&N" = head and neck
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Initial consultation | Add: "This is a new radiation oncology consultation. Structure the note as a full evaluation with history, staging workup review, treatment options discussed (including non-RT alternatives), treatment recommendation with rationale, and informed consent documentation including risks, benefits, and alternatives." |
| Reirradiation | Add: "This patient has been previously irradiated. Document prior RT details (date, site, dose, technique), cumulative dose to overlapping structures, and the rationale for reirradiation including risk-benefit discussion and OAR dose constraints." |
| Brachytherapy | Add: "This patient is receiving brachytherapy. Document the technique (HDR/LDR), applicator type, implant geometry, dose rate, dose per fraction, number of fractions, image guidance, and coordination with the physics plan." |
| Pediatric radiation | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Include developmental considerations, sedation/anesthesia requirements, growth and neurocognitive late effects risk, fertility preservation discussion, and coordination with pediatric oncology." |
| Proton therapy | Add: "This patient is receiving proton therapy. Document the clinical rationale for proton vs. photon therapy, the dosimetric advantage for OAR sparing, insurance authorization status, and any logistical considerations (travel, treatment center)." |

## Variations

### Weekly on-treatment visit

Use this for the weekly check during an active radiation course. The note is shorter and focused on toxicity surveillance, weight tracking, and treatment continuity. This is the bread-and-butter radiation oncology note: brief, systematic, and oriented toward catching problems before they become treatment-limiting.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Weekly On-Treatment Visit">
You are a radiation oncologist writing a weekly on-treatment check note for a patient actively receiving radiation therapy.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document fraction progress (current/total) and cumulative dose.
- Grade all toxicities using CTCAE v5.0 with trajectory (new, stable, improving, worsening) compared to last week.
- Document weight with change from baseline and from last week.
- For H&N patients, document mucositis grade, dietary intake status, and PEG use.
- Include concurrent chemotherapy status and any coordination needed.
- Plan must address each active toxicity and state the threshold for treatment modification.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Post-RT surveillance

Use this for the follow-up visit after radiation therapy is complete. The note shifts from acute toxicity management to late effects surveillance, treatment response assessment, and cancer surveillance imaging.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Post-RT Surveillance Visit">
You are a radiation oncologist writing a post-treatment surveillance note for a patient who has completed radiation therapy.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and completed treatment; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Summarize the completed RT course: technique, total dose, fractions, dates, breaks, concurrent therapy.
- Document resolution or persistence of acute toxicities with current CTCAE grades.
- Screen for late effects based on treatment site: radiation pneumonitis (lung), cardiac toxicity (left breast, mediastinal), hypothyroidism (H&N), xerostomia (H&N), fibrosis, lymphedema, neurocognitive effects (brain), secondary malignancy risk.
- Document treatment response: clinical exam findings, imaging results (PET/CT, MRI), tumor markers.
- Include cancer surveillance schedule appropriate to the diagnosis and stage.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Palliative RT consultation

Use this for the patient referred for radiation to palliate symptoms from metastatic disease: bone pain, brain metastases causing neurological symptoms, tumor-related bleeding, airway obstruction, or spinal cord compression. The note balances symptom relief goals with treatment burden and prognosis.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Palliative Radiation Therapy Consultation">
You are a radiation oncologist evaluating a patient for palliative radiation therapy. Structure the note around symptom burden, goals of care, and a treatment recommendation that balances efficacy with treatment burden.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the specific symptom(s) radiation is intended to palliate.
- Include prognosis discussion, performance status, and goals of care.
- For bone metastases: document pain score, fracture risk (Mirels score if applicable), prior palliative RT to the same site, and fractionation options (single fraction 8 Gy vs. multi-fraction).
- For brain metastases: document number and size, symptom burden, prior whole brain RT or SRS, and treatment options (WBRT vs. SRS vs. observation).
- For spinal cord compression: document neurological deficit, time course, steroid use, and urgency.
- Treatment recommendation must include fractionation schedule, expected onset and duration of palliation, and expected side effects.
- Address concurrent systemic therapy and coordination with medical oncology.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **CTCAE grading must be precise, not estimated.** Grade 2 mucositis means painful but still eating a modified diet. Grade 3 means inadequate oral intake. The difference between these two grades often determines whether the patient stays on treatment, gets a PEG tube, or gets admitted. Use the CTCAE v5.0 definitions, not your gestalt.
- **Weight loss in H&N patients is predictive.** More than 5% weight loss during treatment correlates with worse outcomes, treatment breaks, and hospitalization. Document the weight at each visit, the cumulative percentage loss, and the weekly rate of change. A patient losing 3 lbs per week will not finish 7 weeks of treatment without nutritional intervention.
- **Treatment breaks have consequences.** Every missed fraction during a definitive course for a rapidly proliferating tumor (especially H&N SCC) can reduce local control. Document the reason for any break, the number of fractions missed, and whether the treatment plan will be modified to compensate (e.g., accelerated fractionation or weekend treatments).
- **The model may confuse dose per fraction with total dose.** Verify that the dose-fractionation scheme matches standard regimens for the diagnosis. Common errors: prescribing SBRT doses in conventional fractionation schedules, or writing "70 Gy per fraction" when it should be "2 Gy per fraction to 70 Gy total." Always check the math.
- **Late effects surveillance has specific timelines by site.** Hypothyroidism screening (TSH every 6-12 months post-H&N RT), cardiac monitoring (echo for left breast or mediastinal fields), pulmonary function testing (for lung RT), and neurocognitive assessment (for brain RT) all have evidence-based schedules. Document the surveillance plan with specific tests and timing, not just "monitor for late effects."
- **Concurrent chemotherapy coordination requires explicit communication.** If the radiation oncologist identifies a toxicity that should delay chemotherapy, document the recommendation and the communication to medical oncology. "Will communicate with med onc" is insufficient; document the specific recommendation ("recommend holding cisplatin cycle 3 given ANC 1.2; discussed with Dr. [de-identified] on [date]").
