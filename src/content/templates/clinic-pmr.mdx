## When to use this

Your patient rolled into clinic in a power wheelchair with a new pressure injury, a baclofen pump due for refill, and questions about returning to work. You have 30 minutes. Twenty of those minutes were spent examining, adjusting, and listening. The remaining ten are documentation. Paste your visit data into this template, and the LLM produces a structured physiatry note organized around the patient's injury type, functional status, equipment needs, and rehabilitation goals. The note knows to capture Functional Independence Measure (FIM) scores, American Spinal Injury Association Impairment Scale (AIS) grades, Rancho Los Amigos levels, Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS) scores, Braden scores, bowel and bladder programs, durable medical equipment (DME) specifications, therapy progress, and community reintegration status without you listing each from memory.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Rehabilitation notes serve dual purposes: clinical documentation and justification for continued therapy, DME, and disability determinations. If functional status has changed, document the direction and magnitude. Vague statements like "making progress" do not support medical necessity for ongoing services.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="PM&R / Rehabilitation Medicine Clinic Visit Note">
You are a physiatrist writing an outpatient rehabilitation medicine clinic note after seeing a patient for follow-up or new evaluation. The patient may have one or more of the following conditions: traumatic brain injury (TBI), spinal cord injury (SCI), stroke, chronic pain, limb amputation, or musculoskeletal rehabilitation. Identify all active rehabilitation diagnoses from the clinical information provided and structure the note around them.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient PM&R clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture functional status with validated scales, rehabilitation therapy progress, DME status and needs, bowel and bladder management, spasticity assessment, pain management, community reintegration, and a goal-oriented plan for each problem.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "Functional Independence Measure (FIM) score"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: FIM scores, AIS grades, Rancho levels, MAS scores, Braden scores, ROM in degrees, strength in MRC grades.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a numbered problem list with the primary rehabilitation diagnosis first, then secondary complications, then comorbidities.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: therapy type and frequency, DME orders with specifications, medication changes with doses, referrals with reason.
- For SCI: document neurological level, AIS grade, bowel program, bladder management, skin integrity, and autonomic dysreflexia (AD) history.
- For TBI: document Rancho Los Amigos level, cognitive FIM subscores, behavioral status, and post-traumatic seizure prophylaxis.
- For stroke: document modified Rankin Scale (mRS) score, NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS) if available, affected side, and motor recovery trajectory.
- For spasticity: document MAS scores by muscle group, current treatment (oral, botulinum toxin, intrathecal baclofen), and functional impact.
- For amputees: document amputation level, prosthetic type, K-level classification, socket fit, and gait analysis findings.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "FIM" = Functional Independence Measure
- "AIS" = American Spinal Injury Association Impairment Scale
- "MAS" = Modified Ashworth Scale
- "mRS" = modified Rankin Scale
- "AD" = autonomic dysreflexia
- "DME" = durable medical equipment
- "AFO" = ankle-foot orthosis
- "ROM" = range of motion
- "MRC" = Medical Research Council (muscle strength grading)
- "SCI" = spinal cord injury
- "TBI" = traumatic brain injury
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Pediatric rehabilitation | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Use WeeFIM instead of FIM. Include developmental milestones, school/IEP accommodations, growth parameters, and family/caregiver training goals." |
| Inpatient rehabilitation | Add: "This is an inpatient rehabilitation admission note or progress note. Include admission FIM scores, projected discharge FIM, length of stay estimate, team conference notes, and discharge planning." |
| Spinal cord injury annual eval | Add: "This is an annual comprehensive SCI evaluation. Include full AIS examination, updated bowel/bladder assessment, bone density screening, cardiovascular risk factors, mental health screen (PHQ-9), and 5-year equipment replacement schedule." |
| Workers' compensation case | Add: "This patient is being seen under a workers' compensation claim. Include work-related injury details, functional capacity findings, specific work restrictions with weight limits and hours, and return-to-work timeline." |
| Concussion / mild TBI | Add: "This is a concussion or mild TBI evaluation. Include SCAT5 results, vestibular/ocular motor screening (VOMS), symptom inventory with severity, cognitive testing, and graded return-to-activity protocol." |

### Post-stroke spasticity management

Use this for the stroke patient whose visit centers on spasticity assessment and treatment: botulinum toxin injections, oral agents, or intrathecal baclofen evaluation. The note captures the functional impact of spasticity (both positive and negative), prior treatments and responses, and injection planning.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Post-Stroke Spasticity Management">
You are a physiatrist writing an outpatient note for a stroke patient being evaluated or treated for post-stroke spasticity. Structure the note around spasticity assessment, functional impact, treatment history, and injection or pump management planning.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document MAS scores for each affected muscle group, both upper and lower extremity.
- Distinguish helpful spasticity (assists transfers, standing) from harmful spasticity (pain, contracture risk, hygiene difficulty, skin breakdown).
- Document prior spasticity treatments with dates, doses, and functional response.
- For botulinum toxin: include muscles targeted, total units per visit, dilution, guidance method (EMG, ultrasound, anatomic landmarks), and interval since last injection.
- For ITB pump: include current dose, concentration, refill schedule, and functional gains since implantation.
- Include passive ROM measurements for joints at risk of contracture.
- Include mRS for overall disability tracking.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **FIM scores require specificity.** The model may assign FIM levels that do not match the actual supervision or assistance the patient needs. Verify each subscore against the 7-point scale definitions: 7 is complete independence, 6 is modified independence (device), 5 is supervision, 4 is minimal assist (patient does 75%+), 3 is moderate assist, 2 is maximal assist, and 1 is total assist. A score of 5 means the patient does the task but needs someone watching; a 4 means someone physically helps.
- **AIS grading requires a complete examination.** The model cannot assign an AIS grade from incomplete exam data. If you provide only motor findings without sensory testing (light touch and pin prick by dermatome, including sacral segments S4-5), the grade may be wrong. AIS B vs. C distinction hinges on sacral sparing and motor function below the level.
- **Spasticity is not always the enemy.** Document both helpful and harmful effects. A patient whose extensor tone helps with standing transfers will lose that function if you treat spasticity too aggressively. The treatment goal is function, not a MAS score of zero.
- **Pressure injury staging matters for billing and wound care referrals.** Stage 1 (nonblanchable erythema) and stage 2 (partial thickness) have fundamentally different treatment and documentation requirements than stage 3 and 4 injuries. Unstageable and deep tissue injury are distinct categories, not "stage 5."
- **Bowel program timing and effectiveness dictate quality of life.** A program that takes 90 minutes is not working, even if the patient is technically continent. Document duration, effectiveness, and patient satisfaction. A change in oral regimen, suppository type, or timing can transform daily life.
- **DME prescriptions require medical justification linking the device to a functional goal.** "Needs a wheelchair" is not sufficient. "Requires ultralight manual wheelchair to achieve independent community mobility (FIM 6) given complete T6 paraplegia with preserved UE strength" connects the device to the clinical picture.
- **Autonomic dysreflexia documentation is critical above T6.** Even if the patient has never had an episode, document that the risk was assessed, that the patient carries an AD management card, and that the care team knows the patient's baseline blood pressure. AD is a medical emergency that non-rehab providers may not recognize.
