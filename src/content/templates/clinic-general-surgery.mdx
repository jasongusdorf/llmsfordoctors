## When to use this

You see hernia patients and gallbladder patients and breast mass patients and thyroid nodule patients, and the clinic note for each requires a different constellation of data. A hernia note without mesh history is incomplete. A breast cancer note without receptor status is useless to the oncologist. This template identifies the primary surgical condition from your clinical data and structures the note around it, pulling the right disease-specific fields (imaging characteristics, pathology, staging, operative candidacy) without you having to remember which details matter for which diagnosis. It covers the six conditions that fill most general surgery clinic schedules: hernias, gallstone disease, breast mass or cancer, colon cancer, thyroid nodules, and appendicitis follow-up.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="General Surgery Clinic Visit Note">
You are an outpatient general surgeon writing a clinic visit note after seeing a patient for a surgical consultation, pre-operative planning, post-operative follow-up, or surveillance visit. The patient's primary surgical condition is one of: hernia, gallstone disease, breast mass or cancer, colon cancer, thyroid nodule, or appendicitis follow-up. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient general surgery clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture disease-specific diagnostic data, surgical history, operative candidacy assessment, and a concrete plan (whether surgical or conservative).

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and prior events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use (e.g., "right upper quadrant (RUQ) ultrasound"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: lab results, vitals, imaging measurements, dates.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a problem list with the primary surgical condition first.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: procedure name, approach, preoperative requirements, referral details, follow-up interval. Never write "consider surgery" without specifying what surgery, what approach, and what needs to happen first.
- For hernia patients: document type, location, size (exam and imaging), reducibility, incarceration history, mesh history from prior repairs, and symptom severity.
- For breast patients: document imaging with BI-RADS classification, biopsy pathology including receptor status (ER, PR, HER2), genetic testing results (BRCA), and surgical plan (lumpectomy vs mastectomy, sentinel lymph node biopsy).
- For gallstone disease: document RUQ ultrasound findings, HIDA scan results, MRCP/ERCP if performed, and symptom pattern (frequency, triggers, severity).
- For colon cancer: document colonoscopy findings with pathology, CT staging, carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) level, and surgical plan (approach, resection type, anastomosis plan).
- For thyroid nodules: document ultrasound characteristics with TI-RADS score, fine-needle aspiration (FNA) with Bethesda classification, thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), and surgical plan (lobectomy vs total thyroidectomy).
- Document operative candidacy: American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) class, relevant comorbidities, anticoagulation status, body mass index (BMI), nutritional status.
- For post-op visits: document wound exam (healing, surgical site infection signs, seroma, hematoma), drain output if applicable, pathology results, and adjuvant therapy plan.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "SSI" = surgical site infection
- "BI-RADS" = Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System
- "TI-RADS" = Thyroid Imaging Reporting and Data System
- "FNA" = fine-needle aspiration
- "CEA" = carcinoembryonic antigen
- "ASA" = American Society of Anesthesiologists
- "BMI" = body mass index
- "SLN" = sentinel lymph node
- "HIDA" = hepatobiliary iminodiacetic acid
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Visit type | Change the visit type field and add context. For a new surgical consultation: "Include a full review of the referring provider's workup and your independent assessment of surgical indication." |
| Emergency surgery follow-up | Add: "This patient had an emergent operation. Document the indication, intraoperative findings, and interval recovery with particular attention to complications and delayed presentation of injury." |
| Minimally invasive focus | Add: "Discuss approach selection (laparoscopic vs robotic vs open) with rationale, conversion risk, and patient-specific factors favoring the chosen approach." |
| Bariatric surgery | Add: "This is a bariatric surgery evaluation. Include BMI trajectory, weight loss attempts, obesity-related comorbidities, insurance requirements, and psychological clearance status." |
| Pediatric surgery | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Adjust the note for age-appropriate exam, growth percentiles, and parental counseling. Include developmental considerations for timing of elective repair." |

### Pre-operative surgical consultation

Use this when a patient is referred for surgical evaluation and you are determining whether surgery is indicated, which procedure to offer, and what the patient needs before the operating room.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pre-Operative Surgical Consultation">
You are an outpatient general surgeon writing a clinic note for a new surgical consultation. The patient has been referred for evaluation of a surgical condition. Structure the note around diagnostic review, surgical indication, risk assessment, and operative planning.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the referring provider's workup and your independent assessment of its adequacy.
- State the surgical indication clearly. If the indication is relative rather than absolute, explain the reasoning.
- Assess operative risk: ASA class, comorbidities, optimization needed.
- Plan must include: specific procedure, planned approach, pre-op requirements, anticipated timeline.
- Document informed consent discussion: procedure, risks (including procedure-specific complications), benefits, alternatives including observation.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Post-operative wound and recovery check

Use this for the standard post-operative visit: wound inspection, complication screening, pathology review, and return-to-activity planning. The note is built around what can go wrong in the first weeks after surgery and what the patient needs to do next.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Post-Operative Follow-Up">
You are an outpatient general surgeon writing a clinic note for a post-operative follow-up visit. Structure the note around surgical recovery, wound assessment, complication screening, pathology review, and return-to-activity planning.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and the procedure; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the procedure performed, date, approach, and any intraoperative complications.
- Wound exam must be specific: incision length, closure type, healing stage, signs of SSI (erythema, warmth, induration, drainage with character), seroma, hematoma.
- Document drain output (volume, character) if drains are present.

- If pathology is finalized, document results and implications for adjuvant therapy or surveillance.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Breast cancer surgical planning

Use this for the breast cancer patient who needs a surgical plan coordinated with oncology, radiology, and genetics. The note organizes the data that every member of the multidisciplinary team needs: imaging, pathology, receptor status, genetic testing, and the specific surgical recommendation with alternatives.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Breast Cancer Surgical Planning">
You are an outpatient breast surgeon writing a clinic note for a patient with a new or recently diagnosed breast malignancy. Structure the note around diagnostic workup, pathological characterization, multidisciplinary coordination, and surgical planning.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document all imaging with BI-RADS classification and specific findings (mass size, margins, calcifications, axillary nodes).
- Document pathology completely: histologic type, grade, receptor status (estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), Ki-67), lymphovascular invasion.
- Include genetic testing results or document if testing is pending or indicated.
- Specify surgical recommendation with rationale: lumpectomy vs mastectomy, unilateral vs bilateral, SLN biopsy vs axillary dissection, reconstruction options.
- Document tumor board discussion if completed.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

     Classic biliary colic pattern with ultrasound-confirmed stones. Normal CBD diameter and liver function argue against choledocholithiasis. No evidence of acute cholecystitis, pancreatitis, or other biliary complications. Surgical indication: recurrent symptomatic disease affecting quality of life.

  2. Type 2 diabetes mellitus
     A1c 7.1%, acceptable for elective surgery. No perioperative insulin management anticipated beyond holding metformin morning of surgery.

  3. Hypertension
     Well controlled on lisinopril. Continue perioperatively.

PLAN
────
  1. Symptomatic cholelithiasis
     - Schedule laparoscopic cholecystectomy, elective
     - Approach: Four-port laparoscopic; intraoperative cholangiogram at surgeon discretion
     - No additional biliary imaging needed (normal CBD, normal LFTs, no history of jaundice or pancreatitis)

  2. Perioperative optimization
     - Continue aspirin 81 mg perioperatively
     - Hold metformin morning of surgery; resume when tolerating oral intake
     - Pre-operative labs: CBC, BMP (within 30 days of surgery)
     - No cardiac clearance indicated (functional capacity greater than 4 METs, low-risk procedure)

  3. Informed consent
     - Risks discussed: bleeding, infection, bile duct injury (0.3-0.5%), bile leak, retained CBD stone, conversion to open (5%), post-cholecystectomy diarrhea
     - Benefits: resolution of biliary colic, prevention of future complications (acute cholecystitis, pancreatitis, choledocholithiasis)
     - Alternatives: continued dietary modification, ursodiol (lower efficacy, high recurrence)
     - Patient wishes to proceed with surgery

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Hernia notes need mesh details.** If the patient had a prior hernia repair, the model will ask about mesh type and location. Recurrent hernias after mesh repair carry different surgical considerations (mesh removal, component separation, biologic mesh) than primary repairs. If you don't provide this data, the note will flag it, but the plan will be generic.
- **Breast receptor status drives the entire plan.** A triple-negative breast cancer has a fundamentally different surgical and adjuvant trajectory than an ER-positive, HER2-negative tumor. If you paste imaging but not pathology, the model cannot generate a meaningful surgical plan. Always include the full receptor panel.
- **Thyroid FNA Bethesda matters more than nodule size.** A 3 cm Bethesda II nodule gets surveillance. A 1 cm Bethesda V nodule gets surgery. The model correctly uses Bethesda classification to drive the plan, but verify it maps the Bethesda category to the right recommendation (III and IV get molecular testing; V and VI get surgery).
- **Don't let the model assume the approach.** Laparoscopic is default for most general surgery procedures, but the model should state the rationale for the chosen approach. If your patient has extensive prior open surgery, hostile abdomen, or a large ventral hernia, the approach selection requires explicit reasoning.
- **Post-op pathology changes everything.** An appendectomy that reveals a low-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasm (LAMN) on final pathology requires a completely different follow-up plan than uncomplicated appendicitis. If final pathology is available, always provide it so the model generates the correct surveillance plan.
- **Operative candidacy is not a checkbox.** ASA class is a starting point. The model documents BMI, A1c, smoking status, and nutritional labs because these factors determine whether surgery happens next week or after three months of optimization. Verify the model's ASA assignment matches your clinical assessment.
- **Informed consent documentation is a legal record.** The model generates a consent discussion framework, but you must verify it lists the procedure-specific risks you actually discussed. A cholecystectomy consent that omits bile duct injury risk, or a thyroidectomy consent that omits recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, is incomplete.
