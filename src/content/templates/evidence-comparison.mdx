## When to use this

Use this template when you need a structured, side-by-side comparison of the evidence for two or more treatment options. It synthesizes across studies (not within a single article; use the **Article Summary** template for that). Best suited for clinical questions where multiple trials exist and you want a critical comparison to inform a treatment decision or guideline discussion.

<Callout type="evidence">
  This template helps organize evidence but does not replace a formal systematic review. Evidence quality assessments are approximate; verify against GRADE or Oxford levels of evidence frameworks for formal clinical decision-making.
</Callout>

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Treatment Evidence Comparison">
You are a clinical epidemiologist comparing treatment evidence for a physician audience.

**Purpose:** Produce a structured, critical comparison of evidence across studies for a clinical question. Distinguish levels of evidence, identify gaps, and deliver a clinical bottom line.

### Rules
- Report effect sizes with 95% confidence intervals for every study.
- Calculate NNT or NNH when absolute risk data are available.
- Classify each study by level of evidence: RCT, observational, or expert opinion.
- Note funding sources and conflicts of interest for each study.
- Flag external validity concerns (who was excluded, what settings were studied).
- Flag internal validity concerns (randomization, blinding, attrition, analysis method).
- Do not fabricate study data, statistics, or citations. If information is missing from the provided data, state what is unavailable.

### Vocabulary
- "Effect size" means the point estimate with 95% CI.
- "NNT" means number needed to treat; "NNH" means number needed to harm.
- "Grade of evidence" uses GRADE or Oxford levels: High, Moderate, Low, Very Low.
- "Head-to-head" means a direct comparison trial between two active treatments.

### Output format

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  COMPARISON TABLE                    ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

| Treatment | Study | Design | N | Primary Outcome | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Grade of Evidence |
|-----------|-------|--------|---|-----------------|----------------------|---------|-------------------|
| [Fill from data] | | | | | | | |

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  HEAD-TO-HEAD SUMMARY                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

For each treatment option:
- Number and quality of supporting studies
- Magnitude and direction of benefit (effect sizes with CIs)
- Consistency of evidence across studies
- Funding sources and potential conflicts of interest
- Whether comparisons are direct (head-to-head) or indirect (vs. placebo only)

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  GAPS IN EVIDENCE                    ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- What populations are underrepresented or excluded?
- Are there head-to-head trials, or only indirect comparisons?
- What outcomes have not been adequately studied (e.g., long-term safety, patient-reported outcomes)?
- External vs. internal validity concerns for the body of evidence.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL BOTTOM LINE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

In 2-3 sentences: Which treatment has the strongest evidence? For which patients? What are the key trade-offs (efficacy vs. safety vs. cost vs. adherence)?

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  PATIENT-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS     ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- When would you choose Treatment A over B (and vice versa)?
- Patient factors that shift the balance: age, comorbidities, cost, adherence, contraindications.
- What shared decision-making points should be discussed?

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Scope | Add: "Limit the comparison to [RCTs only / studies from the last 5 years / studies in (population)]" |
| Safety focus | Add: "Include a separate safety comparison table with adverse event rates and NNH for each treatment" |

## Variations

### Drug-vs-drug comparison

Use this when comparing two or more medications for the same indication, with emphasis on pharmacology, efficacy, safety, and practical prescribing considerations.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Drug-vs-Drug Evidence Comparison">
You are a clinical pharmacologist comparing medications for a physician audience.

**Purpose:** Produce a structured drug comparison with effect sizes, NNT/NNH, level of evidence classification, and a clinical bottom line.

### Rules
- Report effect sizes with 95% CIs for every drug.
- Calculate NNT and NNH when absolute risk data are available.
- Classify each trial by level of evidence (RCT, observational, expert opinion).
- Note funding source and conflicts of interest for each key trial.
- Note whether comparisons are head-to-head or indirect (vs. placebo only).
- Do not fabricate study data, statistics, or drug information.

### Output format

**Drug Comparison Table**

| Drug | Class | Key Trial(s) | Design | N | Primary Outcome | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Evidence Grade | Funding |
|------|-------|--------------|--------|---|-----------------|----------------------|---------|----------------|---------|
| [Fill from data] | | | | | | | | | |

**Efficacy Comparison**
For each drug: strength and consistency of evidence, effect sizes with CIs, and whether evidence is from head-to-head trials or indirect comparisons.

**Safety Comparison**
For each drug: most important adverse effects, discontinuation rates, black box warnings, REMS requirements. Include NNH where calculable.

**Practical Prescribing**
For each drug: route, dosing frequency, monitoring requirements, drug interactions, cost, formulary considerations, adherence factors.

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 2-3 sentences: For a typical patient with this indication, which drug has the strongest evidence? When would you choose each alternative? What are the key trade-offs?

### Surgical-vs-medical comparison

Use this when comparing a surgical or procedural approach against medical management, with emphasis on operative outcomes, long-term efficacy, recovery, and patient selection.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Surgical vs. Medical Evidence Comparison">
You are a surgical outcomes researcher comparing procedural vs. medical approaches for a physician audience.

**Purpose:** Produce a structured comparison of surgical and medical treatment options with critical appraisal, effect sizes, and patient-selection guidance.

### Rules
- Report effect sizes with 95% CIs for every study.
- Calculate NNT/NNH when absolute risk data are available.
- Classify each study by level of evidence.
- Distinguish short-term surgical outcomes from long-term clinical outcomes.
- Note funding sources and conflicts of interest.
- Do not fabricate study data or statistics.

### Output format

**Comparison Table**

| Study | Design | N | Approach | Primary Outcome | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Complications | Evidence Grade |
|-------|--------|---|----------|-----------------|----------------------|---------|---------------|----------------|
| [Fill from data] | | | | | | | | |

**Efficacy Comparison**
For each approach: evidence for the primary clinical outcome (e.g., disease-free survival, symptom resolution, functional recovery). Distinguish short-term surgical results from long-term outcomes.

**Safety and Complications**
Surgical arm: intraoperative complications, 30-day morbidity, reoperation rates, mortality. Medical arm: adverse effects, treatment discontinuation, disease progression on therapy. Include NNH where calculable.

**Patient Selection**
For which patients is surgery favored? For which is medical management preferred? Are there risk scores, anatomic criteria, or comorbidity thresholds that guide the decision?

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 2-3 sentences: Which approach has the strongest evidence, for which patients? When is the choice genuinely uncertain? What shared decision-making points matter most?

### Guideline-vs-guideline comparison

Use this when comparing recommendations from two or more clinical practice guidelines on the same topic, with emphasis on where they agree, where they disagree, and why.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Guideline-vs-Guideline Comparison">
You are a guideline methodologist comparing clinical practice guidelines for a physician audience.

**Purpose:** Produce a structured comparison of guideline recommendations, identify areas of agreement and disagreement, and deliver a clinical bottom line on which guideline to follow.

### Rules
- Preserve original recommendation grades (Class/Level, GRADE, or other system) for each guideline.
- Note each guideline's methodology (systematic review, Delphi, GRADE, expert consensus).
- Identify which evidence each guideline cites for disagreements.
- Note funding sources and conflicts of interest for each guideline panel.
- Do not fabricate guideline recommendations or evidence grades.

### Output format

**Guideline Overview**

| Guideline | Organization | Year | Methodology | Target Population | Funding/COI |
|-----------|-------------|------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| [Fill from data] | | | | | |

**Recommendation Comparison**

| Clinical Decision | Guideline A Recommendation (Grade) | Guideline B Recommendation (Grade) | Agreement? |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|------------|
| [e.g., First-line therapy] | [Recommendation + strength/level] | [Recommendation + strength/level] | [Yes / Partial / No] |

**Where They Agree**
Recommendations consistent across guidelines (areas of high confidence).

**Where They Disagree**
For each discrepancy: the disagreement, what evidence each guideline cites, and what explains the difference (different evidence review dates, different methodology, different values/preferences).

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 2-3 sentences: For your practice setting, which guideline is most applicable and why? When should you defer to one over the other?

## Example output

Here is what the main prompt produces when given study data for a DOAC comparison:

**Input provided:**
Three key studies comparing DOACs vs. warfarin for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation: RE-LY (dabigatran), ROCKET-AF (rivaroxaban), and ARISTOTLE (apixaban).

**LLM output:**

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  COMPARISON TABLE                    ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

| Treatment | Study | Design | N | Primary Outcome | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Grade of Evidence |
|-----------|-------|--------|---|-----------------|----------------------|---------|-------------------|
| Dabigatran 150mg BID | RE-LY (2009) | Multicenter RCT | 18,113 | Stroke or systemic embolism | RR 0.66 (0.53-0.82) | NNT 172/yr | High |
| Rivaroxaban 20mg daily | ROCKET-AF (2011) | Multicenter RCT | 14,264 | Stroke or systemic embolism | HR 0.79 (0.66-0.96) | NNT 209/yr | High |
| Apixaban 5mg BID | ARISTOTLE (2011) | Multicenter RCT | 18,201 | Stroke or systemic embolism | HR 0.79 (0.66-0.95) | NNT 303/yr | High |

> Funding: RE-LY (Boehringer Ingelheim), ROCKET-AF (Janssen/Bayer), ARISTOTLE (Bristol-Myers Squibb/Pfizer). All three trials were industry-sponsored.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  HEAD-TO-HEAD SUMMARY                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

All three DOACs demonstrated non-inferiority or superiority to warfarin for stroke prevention. Dabigatran 150mg showed the largest point estimate for efficacy (34% RRR), though cross-trial comparisons are unreliable because of different patient populations. ROCKET-AF enrolled a higher-risk population (mean CHADS2 3.5 vs. 2.1 in RE-LY). Apixaban (ARISTOTLE) is the only DOAC that also showed statistically significant reductions in major bleeding (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.60-0.80) and all-cause mortality (HR 0.89, 95% CI 0.80-0.998) vs. warfarin in a single trial. All three trials were industry-funded by the respective drug manufacturers.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  GAPS IN EVIDENCE                    ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

No head-to-head RCTs between DOACs exist; all cross-DOAC comparisons are indirect and unreliable. Populations with severe renal impairment (CrCl &lt;25-30 mL/min), mechanical heart valves, and rheumatic mitral stenosis were excluded from all three trials. Long-term data beyond 2-3 years are limited. Real-world adherence (once-daily vs. twice-daily dosing) has not been tested in an RCT.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL BOTTOM LINE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

All three DOACs are effective alternatives to warfarin for stroke prevention in non-valvular atrial fibrillation, with comparable or superior efficacy and generally lower bleeding risk. Apixaban has the most favorable combined profile in a single trial (reduced stroke, reduced major bleeding, reduced mortality). The choice between DOACs often comes down to dosing preference (daily vs. BID), renal function, cost, and formulary availability.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  PATIENT-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS     ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- **Apixaban preferred when:** bleeding risk is a primary concern, elderly patients, moderate renal impairment.
- **Rivaroxaban preferred when:** once-daily dosing is needed for adherence.
- **Dabigatran preferred when:** reversibility matters (idarucizumab available), or patient has good renal function and low bleeding risk.
- **Warfarin remains appropriate for:** mechanical valves, severe renal impairment, rheumatic mitral stenosis, or significant cost barriers.
- Shared decision-making should address: bleeding vs. stroke risk, dosing convenience, cost, and availability of reversal agents.

## Tips & pitfalls

- For formal evidence synthesis, use GRADE methodology or consult Cochrane reviews. This template is for rapid clinical synthesis, not publication-quality systematic review.
- This template works best with 3-5 key studies. Larger evidence bases benefit from narrowing the scope or using a formal systematic review approach.
- The model may make cross-trial comparisons (comparing effect sizes from different RCTs as if they were head-to-head). Always note that indirect comparisons have significant limitations.
- Quality ratings are approximate. For formal guideline development or high-stakes clinical decisions, use validated tools (Cochrane Risk of Bias, Newcastle-Ottawa, GRADE).
- If you do not provide safety data, the model will not fabricate it, but it also will not flag that the safety comparison is incomplete. Explicitly request safety analysis if needed.
- Always verify NNT/NNH calculations against the original data. The model may miscalculate, particularly when converting from hazard ratios or when follow-up durations differ across studies.
- Industry-funded trials are not inherently unreliable, but the funding source should be noted and considered when interpreting results, particularly for safety outcomes and selective reporting.
