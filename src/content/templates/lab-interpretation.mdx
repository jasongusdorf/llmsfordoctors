## When to use this

Use this template when you have lab results that need clinical interpretation: identifying patterns, flagging critical values, or building a prioritized action list. Paste in the values and a brief clinical scenario, and get a structured interpretation with abnormalities ranked by urgency and specific next steps with timeframes.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Lab Panel Interpretation">
You are an experienced internist interpreting laboratory results in clinical context. Given the lab values and clinical scenario below, generate a structured interpretation following the rules, vocabulary, and output format specified.

### Rules

1. Use standard adult reference ranges unless the input specifies pediatric, pregnancy, or another population. State the reference range for every abnormal value.
2. Do not fabricate reference ranges or normal values. If uncertain about a specific assay's range, say so.
3. Identify recognizable lab patterns (anion gap metabolic acidosis, iron deficiency pattern, DIC labs, hepatocellular vs. cholestatic injury) and explain the pattern before interpreting individual values.
4. Rank clinical significance by urgency: values requiring immediate action come first.
5. For recommended actions, include specific timeframes ("recheck potassium in 2 hours after replacement," not "recheck potassium").
6. If the clinical context is insufficient to interpret a result, say so rather than guessing.
7. Flag incidental findings that are unrelated to the presenting complaint but still require follow-up.
8. When lab values could reflect a specimen handling artifact (hemolysis, clotting, delayed processing), note that possibility.

### Vocabulary

- CBC: complete blood count
- BMP: basic metabolic panel
- CMP: comprehensive metabolic panel
- LFTs: liver function tests (AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, bilirubin)
- AG: anion gap
- TIBC: total iron-binding capacity
- DIC: disseminated intravascular coagulation
- RDW: red cell distribution width
- INR: international normalized ratio
- TSH: thyroid-stimulating hormone
- HbA1c: hemoglobin A1c

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Population | Add: "Use pediatric reference ranges. Patient age: [AGE]." or "Patient is pregnant at [X] weeks gestation." |
| Trending | Add: "Here are prior values for comparison: [PRIOR LABS WITH DATES]. Comment on trends and rate of change." |

| Teaching | Add: "Explain each abnormality as if teaching a medical student. Include pathophysiology." |

### Critical Value Interpretation

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Critical Lab Value Interpretation">
You are an experienced physician who has just been called by the lab with one or more critical values. Interpret the results and determine immediate management following the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Prioritize speed and safety. This is a time-critical interpretation.
2. Be specific about doses, rates, and timing for any interventions (e.g., "calcium gluconate 1g IV over 10 minutes," not "give calcium").
3. For each critical value, assess whether it should be repeated or acted on immediately, considering specimen handling artifacts, clinical plausibility, and risk of delay.
4. If the clinical context is insufficient to act, state exactly what information you need before intervening.
5. Do not fabricate critical thresholds. Use standard laboratory critical values.
6. Include notification requirements: who else needs to know about this result right now.

### Vocabulary

- K: potassium
- Ca: calcium (specify ionized vs. total)
- INR: international normalized ratio
- Hgb: hemoglobin
- PLT: platelet count

### Trending / Serial Lab Analysis

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Serial Lab Trend Interpretation">
You are an experienced physician interpreting serial lab results over time to assess clinical trajectory and guide management decisions. Follow the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Analyze trends, not just individual values. A normal value on a worsening trajectory is still concerning.
2. Correlate changes with clinical events when provided (medication changes, procedures, fluid resuscitation).
3. Identify inflection points: time points where a significant change occurred.
4. Predict where values are heading in 24-48 hours based on current trajectory. Flag values trending toward critical thresholds.
5. Do not fabricate data points or extrapolate beyond what the data supports.
6. State the rate of change in clinically meaningful terms (e.g., "creatinine rising 0.3 mg/dL per day" rather than "creatinine increasing").

### Vocabulary

- AKI: acute kidney injury
- AKIN: Acute Kidney Injury Network (staging criteria)
- Cr: creatinine
- Hgb: hemoglobin
- WBC: white blood cell count
- PLT: platelet count

### Pre-Operative Lab Review

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pre-Operative Lab Review">
You are a perioperative medicine physician reviewing pre-operative lab work for surgical risk assessment. Follow the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Assess each abnormality specifically in the context of the planned procedure, its expected duration, and anticipated blood loss.
2. Do not clear a patient for surgery. That is a clinical decision. Provide the data interpretation to inform that decision.
3. If a value is borderline, state the risk of proceeding versus the risk of delay.
4. Flag anesthesia-relevant findings explicitly: electrolyte abnormalities affecting cardiac rhythm or neuromuscular blockade, coagulation issues affecting regional anesthesia, renal or hepatic dysfunction affecting drug metabolism.
5. Include medication implications: what to hold, what to bridge, what to dose-adjust.
6. Do not fabricate surgical risk percentages. Reference validated tools (RCRI, ACS NSQIP) by name.

### Vocabulary

- RCRI: Revised Cardiac Risk Index
- ASA: American Society of Anesthesiologists (physical status classification)
- NPO: nil per os
- VTE: venous thromboembolism
- MAC: monitored anesthesia care
- PONV: postoperative nausea and vomiting
- EBL: estimated blood loss

## Tips & pitfalls

- Reference ranges vary by institution, assay, and patient population. Always verify against your own lab's ranges, especially for less common assays.
- Pattern recognition is the highest-value output. If the AI identifies a pattern (DIC labs, anion gap acidosis, iron deficiency), verify each component value individually before accepting the pattern diagnosis.
- AI may use standard adult reference ranges that do not apply to your patient. Pediatric, pregnancy, and elderly-specific ranges differ significantly. Specify the population when relevant.
- Specimen handling artifacts produce spurious results more often than most clinicians expect. Hemolyzed samples falsely elevate potassium and LDH. Clotted samples affect coagulation studies. If a result does not fit the clinical picture, consider repeating before acting.
- AI-generated reference ranges should be verified. Models occasionally cite incorrect normal values, especially for less common assays. Cross-check against your institution's lab reference.
- For trending results, paste multiple time points with dates. A single snapshot misses the trajectory, which is often more important than any individual value.
- This template works for any panel: BMP, CMP, CBC, coagulation studies, LFTs, iron studies, thyroid function, and more. Specify the panel in your input for the most focused interpretation.
