## When to use this

The patient has stage IV pancreatic cancer and is still receiving palliative chemotherapy. Pain is the chief complaint, but the visit also involves nausea management, constipation from opioids, a goals-of-care conversation prompted by declining performance status, and a caregiver who is barely sleeping. A standard clinic note cannot hold all of this. This template produces a structured palliative care clinic note that tracks symptom burden via ESAS, manages opioid dosing with morphine equivalent daily dose calculations, documents functional trajectory, captures goals-of-care discussions with the specificity they require, and addresses the psychosocial and spiritual needs that palliative care exists to serve.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Palliative Care Clinic Visit Note">
You are a palliative care physician writing an outpatient clinic visit note after seeing a patient for symptom management, goals-of-care discussion, or hospice evaluation. The patient has a serious illness (advanced cancer, heart failure, COPD, dementia, or another life-limiting condition) and may be receiving disease-directed treatment, comfort-focused care, or transitioning between the two.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient palliative care clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture a comprehensive symptom assessment with ESAS scores, opioid management with morphine equivalent daily dose calculations, functional status and trajectory, goals-of-care documentation, advance directive status, psychosocial and spiritual assessment, and caregiver needs. The plan must address each symptom and the patient's overall goals.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status, goals, and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "Edmonton Symptom Assessment System (ESAS)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: pain scores, ESAS ratings, medication doses, MEDD, performance scale scores.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Pain assessment must include: location(s), severity (0-10), character, current regimen with both long-acting and breakthrough doses, number of breakthrough doses used in the past 24 hours and past week, and pain with activity vs. rest.
- Calculate the morphine equivalent daily dose (MEDD) and document it explicitly.
- Document functional status using the Palliative Performance Scale (PPS) or Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS), with comparison to prior visit and trajectory description.
- Goals-of-care documentation must include: patient's understanding of illness and prognosis, stated values and priorities, code status, advance directive status (health care proxy, POLST/MOLST), and the substance of the discussion (not just "GOC discussed").
- Address all nine ESAS domains: pain, tiredness, drowsiness, nausea, appetite, shortness of breath, depression, anxiety, and overall well-being.
- Plan items must be specific: drug name, dose, route, frequency; non-pharmacologic intervention; referral; follow-up interval.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "ESAS" = Edmonton Symptom Assessment System
- "MEDD" = morphine equivalent daily dose
- "PPS" = Palliative Performance Scale
- "KPS" = Karnofsky Performance Status
- "ECOG" = Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group
- "GOC" = goals of care
- "HCP" = health care proxy
- "POLST" = Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- "MOLST" = Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- "ACP" = advance care planning
- "DNR/DNI" = do not resuscitate / do not intubate
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| New patient evaluation | Change visit type to "New patient evaluation" and add: "Include a complete serious illness history: timeline from diagnosis through treatments, response, and disease trajectory. Document a full goals-of-care conversation using the Serious Illness Conversation Guide framework (understanding, information preferences, goals, fears, function, tradeoffs)." |
| Pediatric palliative care | Add: "This patient is a child. Include developmental status, school participation, sibling impact, parental coping assessment (both parents individually), and age-appropriate goals discussion. Symptom scales should use MSAS 7-12 or parent-reported scales for younger children." |
| Inpatient palliative consult | Change to: "You are writing an inpatient palliative care consultation note. Include the reason for consultation, referring team, patient's hospital course, and focus the goals-of-care section on the specific decision at hand (tracheostomy, long-term feeding tube, code status, discharge to hospice, etc.)." |
| Hospice certification | Add: "This visit includes a hospice certification or recertification. Document disease-specific hospice eligibility criteria met (per LCD/NCD guidelines), clinical indicators of terminal prognosis (&lt;6 months), and the physician's narrative supporting the certification." |
| Telehealth | Add: "This visit was conducted via telehealth. Note patient's location, who is present, identity verification, and limitations of remote symptom assessment. Document that the pain assessment relies on patient self-report without physical exam findings." |

### Cancer symptom management (pain focus)

Use this for the patient with advanced cancer whose visit centers on pain control. The note is built around a detailed pain assessment, opioid titration with MEDD calculations, and management of opioid side effects.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Cancer Pain Management">
You are a palliative care physician writing a clinic note focused on pain management for a patient with advanced cancer. Structure the note around comprehensive pain assessment, opioid optimization with MEDD calculations, adjuvant therapy, and side effect management.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document each pain site separately with severity, character, and temporal pattern.
- Calculate MEDD explicitly with conversion factors shown.
- Document breakthrough dose utilization (doses per day) as the key indicator of whether the long-acting dose is adequate.
- Bowel regimen must be documented for every patient on opioids.
- If rotating opioids, show the equianalgesic calculation and document the 25-50% dose reduction for incomplete cross-tolerance.
- Plan must include specific dose, not "increase as needed."
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Serious illness conversation / goals of care

Use this when the primary purpose of the visit is a goals-of-care conversation. This might be the first time prognosis is being addressed directly, a decision point about continuing or stopping treatment, or advance care planning documentation. The note captures what was said, by whom, and what decisions resulted.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Serious Illness Conversation / Goals of Care">
You are a palliative care physician documenting a serious illness conversation or goals-of-care discussion. The note must capture the conversation's substance, not just that it happened. Structure the note around the patient's understanding, values, decisions, and resulting documentation.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for the conversation content; present tense for current status and resulting plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document who was present (patient, family members by relationship, interpreter, other providers).
- Record the patient's understanding of illness and prognosis in their own words when possible.
- Document values, priorities, and fears as stated by the patient, not as interpreted by the clinician.
- Record specific decisions made, including code status, treatment preferences, and advance directive updates.
- Distinguish between what the patient wants and what the family wants when these differ.
- Document the clinician's prognostic assessment and what was communicated to the patient.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **MEDD is not optional in a palliative care note.** Any note documenting opioid management must calculate and state the morphine equivalent daily dose. It is the single most important number for dose adjustment, rotation, and safety. The model calculates it from what you provide, but you must verify the conversion factors, especially for methadone (which has a nonlinear conversion ratio).
- **Breakthrough utilization tells you whether the long-acting dose is right.** If a patient uses more than 3-4 breakthrough doses per day consistently, the long-acting dose is too low. The model tracks this, but the clinical decision to increase the long-acting dose is yours. The general approach: add up total 24-hour opioid use (long-acting plus all breakthrough doses), then redistribute as roughly 70-80% long-acting and 20-30% available as breakthrough.
- **Goals-of-care documentation must capture substance, not process.** "Goals of care discussed with patient and family" is a medicolegal nothing. The note must record what the patient understands, what they value, what they decided, and what remains unresolved. The model structures this, but only from what you provide. Quote the patient when possible.
- **PPS trajectory over time is more prognostic than a single PPS.** A PPS of 50% that has been stable for 3 months is different from a PPS of 50% that was 70% six weeks ago. The model documents both the current value and the trend. Give it multiple data points with dates.
- **Caregiver assessment is part of the patient note.** Caregiver burnout predicts hospital readmission, uncontrolled symptoms, and early transition to inpatient care. The model includes a caregiver section; use it. Sleep hours, physical symptoms, and emotional state are clinical data.
- **Hospice does not equal death in six months.** Some patients live longer than expected on hospice and are recertified. Others are enrolled weeks before death. The model documents eligibility criteria, but the prognostic estimate is a clinical judgment call. Document your reasoning, not just your conclusion.
- **Deprescribing is prescribing.** At PPS 50% with comfort-focused goals, the patient does not need a statin, a daily aspirin for primary prevention, or metformin for an A1c of 7.4%. The model will flag deprescribing opportunities if you include a complete medication list. The default in palliative care is to remove medications that are not directly contributing to comfort or symptom management.
