## When to use this

The patient has a procedure scheduled and their anxiety is running about three steps ahead of their understanding. This template generates a plain-language preparation guide that walks them through what the procedure is, how to prepare (day before, morning of), which medications to stop or continue, what will happen during and after, and who to call with questions. The most common source of patient confusion before a procedure is medication management: what to take, what to skip, when to stop the blood thinner. The output addresses this head-on.

Always verify the output against your institution's specific prep protocols. Fasting guidelines, medication holds, and arrival times vary.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, scheduled dates, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Procedure Preparation Guide">
You are a physician writing a preparation guide for a patient who has an upcoming medical procedure. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to make sure they know exactly how to prepare and what to expect, so they show up ready and calm.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Walk through the procedure from the patient's perspective: what they will see, hear, and feel.
- Be honest about discomfort without being alarming.
- Medication instructions must be specific: name, whether to take or skip, and when to stop.
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "blood thinner" alongside anticoagulant names
- Use "sedation" and define it: "medicine that makes you relaxed and drowsy"
- Use "general anesthesia" and define it: "medicine that puts you fully to sleep"
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Anesthesia type | Add: "This procedure uses general anesthesia" or "This is done under local anesthesia in the office" |
| Bowel prep | Add: "Include detailed bowel prep instructions (clear liquids day before, prep solution timing, what colors to avoid)" |
| Anticoagulation | Add: "Include specific bridging instructions for the patient's blood thinner" |
| Anxiety | Add: "This patient is anxious about the procedure. Include reassuring language and suggest they bring a support person." |
| Pediatric | Add: "This procedure is for a child. Write the guide for the parents." |
| Diabetes | Add: "Include specific instructions for managing diabetes medications and blood sugar on procedure day" |

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Use "your child" throughout.
- Include age-appropriate tips for talking to the child about the procedure.
- Include fasting rules specific to children (including breastmilk/formula for infants).

## Example output

```
GETTING READY FOR YOUR PROCEDURE
══════════════════════════════════════════

What the Procedure Is
─────────────────────
You are going to have a colonoscopy. The doctor
will use a thin, flexible tube with a tiny camera
to look inside your large intestine (colon). This
test checks for polyps (small growths) that could
turn into cancer over time. If the doctor finds
any polyps, they can remove them during the
procedure.

How to Prepare
──────────────
  3 Days Before
  ─────────────
  - Stop eating seeds, nuts, popcorn, and corn.
    These are hard to clean out of the colon.

  The Day Before
  ──────────────
  - Eat only clear liquids all day: water, broth,
    Jell-O, apple juice, tea. Nothing red or purple.
  - Do not eat any solid food.
  - Start drinking the bowel prep solution at the
    time your doctor told you (usually 5 or 6 PM).
  - Follow the prep kit instructions exactly.
  - You will have frequent diarrhea. This is normal
    and expected. Stay near a bathroom.
  - Drink extra clear liquids between prep doses
    to stay hydrated.

  The Morning Of
  ──────────────
  - Finish the second half of the prep (if split
    dose) at the time your doctor told you.
  - Do not eat anything.
  - You may have clear liquids until 2 hours before
    your arrival time.
  - Take your blood pressure and heart medications
    with a small sip of water.
  - Do NOT take your diabetes medicine this morning.
  - Confirm your ride home.

Medications to Stop or Continue
───────────────────────────────
| Medication         | What to Do                    |
|--------------------|-------------------------------|
| Blood thinners     | STOP per your doctor's        |
|   (Eliquis, Xarelto)|  specific instructions       |
| Aspirin            | Ask your doctor; usually OK   |
| Diabetes medicine  | STOP the morning of           |
| Insulin            | Take HALF your usual dose     |
| Blood pressure meds| CONTINUE with a small sip     |
| Iron supplements   | STOP 5 days before            |

If you are unsure about any medication, call your
doctor's office before stopping anything.

What to Expect During the Procedure
───────────────────────────────────
  Arrival and Check-In
  ────────────────────
  - Arrive at your scheduled time.
  - You will check in, change into a gown, and
    put your belongings in a locker.

  Before the Procedure
  ────────────────────
  - A nurse will place an IV in your arm.
  - You will be connected to a heart and oxygen
    monitor.
  - The nurse will ask about your medical history
    and allergies.

  During the Procedure
  ────────────────────
  - The doctor will give you sedation (medicine
    that makes you relaxed and drowsy) through
    your IV.
  - You will feel warm and sleepy within 1-2
    minutes.
  - The procedure takes about 20-30 minutes.
  - Most people do not remember it.
  - If polyps are found, the doctor will remove
    them painlessly during the procedure.

What to Expect After
────────────────────
- You will rest in a recovery area for 30-45
  minutes.
- You may feel bloated or have mild cramping from
  the air used during the procedure. This passes.
- The doctor will tell you what they found.
- You will receive written results. Biopsy results
  (if any) come in 5-7 business days.
- Someone must drive you home. You cannot drive
  for 24 hours.

Who to Call with Questions
─────────────────────────
- Before the procedure: GI office at (555) 555-0100
- Day of the procedure: the number on your
  appointment confirmation
- What to call about: prep questions, medication
  confusion, need to reschedule, new symptoms
```

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Verify prep instructions against your institution's protocols.** Fasting guidelines, medication holds, and arrival times vary by institution, procedure, and anesthesia type. The AI generates reasonable defaults, but your institution's specific protocol is the one the patient should follow.
- **Medication management is the most common source of patient confusion.** Blood thinner holds, diabetes medication adjustments, and "which pills to take with a sip of water" generate more pre-procedure phone calls than anything else. Get this section right.
- **For bowel prep, anticoagulation bridging, or complex medication management, use AI as a starting point.** Add your institution's specific prep kit name, timing, and split-dose instructions to the input.
- **Anxious patients benefit from knowing what the procedure feels like.** "You may feel pressure but not pain" or "most people say it was easier than they expected" is more useful than glossing over the experience.
- **Pair with the Post-Procedure Care template.** Hand both guides to the patient at the pre-procedure appointment so they know what happens before, during, and after. The complete set reduces uncertainty and phone calls.
- **For pediatric procedures, address the parents' anxiety alongside the child's.** Parents who are calm produce calmer children. The pediatric variation includes how to talk to the child at each developmental stage.
