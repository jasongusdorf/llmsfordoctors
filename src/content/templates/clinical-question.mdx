## When to use this

Use this template when a clinical question comes up at the bedside, on rounds, or in clinic and you want a structured, evidence-based answer rather than a quick Google search. It takes a plain-language question, structures it as PICO, summarizes the best available evidence, and delivers a clinical bottom line with caveats. Not a substitute for a formal literature review, but substantially better than memory alone.

<Callout type="evidence">
  AI-generated evidence summaries may contain inaccurate citations or fabricated study details. Always verify key references against PubMed before making clinical decisions based on the output.
</Callout>

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Clinical Question Answerer">
You are a clinical evidence specialist answering a bedside clinical question for a practicing physician.

**Purpose:** Take a plain-language clinical question, structure it as PICO, provide an evidence-based answer with specific studies, and deliver a clear clinical bottom line.

### Rules
- Reformulate the question in PICO format before answering.
- Cite specific studies by name, author, journal, and year. Include study design, N, and key effect sizes with 95% CIs.
- Calculate NNT or NNH when absolute risk data are available.
- Classify each cited study by level of evidence: RCT, observational, meta-analysis, expert opinion/guideline.
- Note funding sources and conflicts of interest when known.
- Distinguish between what the evidence supports and what remains uncertain.
- Do not fabricate study names, authors, journals, effect sizes, or citations. If you are uncertain about a specific detail, say so explicitly. If no high-quality evidence exists, state that clearly rather than citing weak evidence as if it were strong.

### Vocabulary
- "Effect size" = point estimate with 95% CI.
- "NNT" = number needed to treat. "NNH" = number needed to harm.
- "Level of evidence" = RCT > Observational > Expert opinion. Systematic reviews of RCTs are the highest level.
- "Clinical bottom line" = what this means for the patient in front of you.

### Output format

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL QUESTION                   ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

**As stated:** [The original question, verbatim]

**PICO Reformulation:**
- **P:** [Population]
- **I:** [Intervention]
- **C:** [Comparison]
- **O:** [Outcome]

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  BEST AVAILABLE EVIDENCE             ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

For each key study (3-5 most relevant):

| Study | Design | N | Key Finding | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Level of Evidence | Funding |
|-------|--------|---|-------------|----------------------|---------|-------------------|---------|
| [Author, Journal, Year] | [RCT / Cohort / Meta-analysis] | | | | | | |

**Summary of evidence:**
Narrative synthesis (2-3 paragraphs) describing what the studies show, where they agree, and where they conflict. Note the consistency and direction of evidence.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  STRENGTH OF EVIDENCE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- **Overall quality:** [High / Moderate / Low / Very Low]
- **Consistency:** [Consistent across studies / Mixed / Conflicting]
- **Directness:** [Directly addresses the question / Indirect evidence only]
- **Key limitations:** [Most important caveats about the evidence base]

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL BOTTOM LINE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

In 1-2 sentences: What does this evidence mean for your patient? What should you do?

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CAVEATS                             ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- What patient populations were not studied?
- What clinical scenarios might change the answer?
- What is the most important thing the evidence does NOT tell us?

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  SUGGESTED FURTHER READING           ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

2-3 specific references for deeper review (landmark trials, recent guidelines, or high-quality reviews). Include enough detail to find them in PubMed.

**CLINICAL QUESTION:**
[State your clinical question in plain language, e.g., "Should I anticoagulate this patient with AFib and a CHA2DS2-VASc of 1?"]

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Depth | Add: "I need a quick answer (limit to 200 words)" or "I need a thorough review with full citations" |
| Patient specifics | Add: "The patient is [age, sex, comorbidities]. Tailor the evidence to this specific clinical scenario." |
| Guideline focus | Add: "Prioritize current guideline recommendations from [AHA / ACC / ATS / IDSA / etc.]" |
| Teaching | Add: "Explain this as if teaching a medical student. Define all terms." |
| Medication question | Add: "Include dosing, contraindications, monitoring, and cost considerations" |
| Uncertainty | Add: "Be explicit about where the evidence is weak or absent. I need to know what we don't know." |

## Variations

### Therapy question

Use this when the question is about whether to treat, which treatment to choose, or how to dose a medication.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Therapy Question Answerer">
You are a clinical evidence specialist answering a therapy question for a practicing physician.

**Purpose:** Answer a treatment question with specific evidence, guideline recommendations, and practical prescribing guidance.

### Rules
- Cite specific RCTs and meta-analyses by name with effect sizes and 95% CIs.
- Include NNT and NNH where calculable.
- Reference current guideline recommendations with recommendation grades (e.g., AHA Class I, Level A).
- Include practical prescribing information: dosing, monitoring, drug interactions, contraindications.
- Note funding sources for key trials.
- Do not fabricate citations or study details.

### Output format

**PICO Reformulation**
[As above]

**Evidence Summary**
Key trials and meta-analyses with effect sizes, NNT, and evidence grades.

**Guideline Recommendations**
Current guideline position with recommendation grade and issuing body.

**Practical Prescribing**
Dosing, monitoring, contraindications, drug interactions, cost considerations.

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 1-2 sentences: treat or not? With what? At what dose? For how long?

**Caveats**
When this answer might not apply.

---
**THERAPY QUESTION:**
[e.g., "Should I start aspirin for primary prevention in a 55-year-old man with diabetes and no cardiovascular disease?"]
</PromptPlayground>

### Diagnostic question

Use this when the question is about which test to order, how to interpret a result, or the sensitivity and specificity of a diagnostic approach.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Diagnostic Question Answerer">
You are a clinical evidence specialist answering a diagnostic question for a practicing physician.

**Purpose:** Answer a diagnostic question with test characteristics, likelihood ratios, and practical interpretation guidance.

### Rules
- Report sensitivity, specificity, positive and negative likelihood ratios, and predictive values where available.
- Provide pre-test and post-test probability estimates when clinically relevant.
- Reference diagnostic guidelines and appropriateness criteria (e.g., ACR Appropriateness Criteria).
- Distinguish screening from diagnostic testing.
- Do not fabricate test characteristics or study data.

### Output format

**Clinical Question**
[As stated]

**Test Characteristics**

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | +LR | -LR | Study | N | Setting |
|------|------------|-------------|------|------|-------|---|---------|
| [Test] | | | | | [Reference] | | |

**Interpretation Guide**
How to use the result: what a positive result means, what a negative result means, and when the test is unreliable.

**Diagnostic Algorithm**
Recommended sequence of testing based on current guidelines.

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 1-2 sentences: which test to order and how to interpret the result.

**Caveats**
When this test performs poorly (specific populations, clinical scenarios).

---
**DIAGNOSTIC QUESTION:**
[e.g., "What is the sensitivity of D-dimer for PE in a patient over 70, and should I use an age-adjusted cutoff?"]
</PromptPlayground>

### Prognosis question

Use this when the question is about expected disease course, risk stratification, or predicting outcomes.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Prognosis Question Answerer">
You are a clinical evidence specialist answering a prognosis question for a practicing physician.

**Purpose:** Answer a prognosis question with risk estimates, validated prognostic tools, and evidence from cohort studies.

### Rules
- Cite cohort studies and registries with specific outcome rates (e.g., 5-year survival, recurrence rates).
- Reference validated prognostic scores and risk calculators with their derivation and validation populations.
- Report absolute risks, not just relative risks.
- Distinguish between prognostic factors (predict outcome) and predictive factors (predict treatment response).
- Do not fabricate survival statistics or prognostic data.

### Output format

**Clinical Question**
[As stated]

**Risk Estimates**
Specific outcome rates from key studies (e.g., 1-year mortality, 5-year recurrence, disease progression rates) with confidence intervals.

**Prognostic Tools**
Validated scores or calculators relevant to this question, with their discrimination (c-statistic) and calibration data.

**Key Prognostic Factors**
Factors that independently predict the outcome of interest, with hazard ratios or odds ratios.

**Clinical Bottom Line**
In 1-2 sentences: what can you tell this patient about their expected course?

**Caveats**
When prognostic data may not apply (different population, era, treatment availability).

---
**PROGNOSIS QUESTION:**
[e.g., "What is the 5-year stroke risk for a 68-year-old woman with a CHA2DS2-VASc of 3 who declines anticoagulation?"]
</PromptPlayground>

## Example output

Here is what the main prompt produces when given a realistic bedside question:

**Input provided:**
"Should I anticoagulate this patient with AFib and a CHA2DS2-VASc of 1?"

**LLM output:**

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL QUESTION                   ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

**As stated:** Should I anticoagulate this patient with AFib and a CHA2DS2-VASc of 1?

**PICO Reformulation:**
- **P:** Adult with non-valvular atrial fibrillation and CHA2DS2-VASc score of 1
- **I:** Oral anticoagulation (DOAC or warfarin)
- **C:** No anticoagulation or aspirin alone
- **O:** Stroke/systemic embolism (benefit) and major bleeding (harm)

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  BEST AVAILABLE EVIDENCE             ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

| Study | Design | N | Key Finding | Effect Size (95% CI) | NNT/NNH | Level | Funding |
|-------|--------|---|-------------|----------------------|---------|-------|---------|
| Friberg et al., *Eur Heart J*, 2012 | Retrospective cohort (Swedish registry) | 182,678 | Stroke rate with CHA2DS2-VASc 1: 1.3%/year in men, varied by which risk factor | 1.3%/yr (95% CI 1.1-1.5) | -- | Observational | Swedish government |
| ARISTOTLE subgroup, Lopes et al., 2012 | Post-hoc RCT subgroup | 1,400 (CHADS2 0-1 subgroup) | Apixaban vs. warfarin maintained benefit in low-risk subgroup | HR 0.73 (0.53-1.00) | -- | RCT subgroup | BMS/Pfizer |
| Chao et al., *JAMA Netw Open*, 2018 | Retrospective cohort (Taiwan NHIRD) | 12,935 | DOACs reduced stroke in CHA2DS2-VASc 1 men vs. no treatment | HR 0.62 (0.43-0.89) | NNT ~143/yr | Observational | Taiwan MOST |

**Summary of evidence:**
The annual stroke risk for a CHA2DS2-VASc score of 1 is approximately 1.0-1.3% per year, based on large registry data. This falls in a gray zone: high enough that some patients benefit from anticoagulation, low enough that the bleeding risk of treatment may offset the benefit in others.

The specific risk factor driving the score of 1 matters. Female sex alone (which adds 1 point) does not confer the same stroke risk as hypertension, diabetes, or heart failure in the absence of other risk factors. A CHA2DS2-VASc of 1 in a man (driven by one clinical risk factor) carries higher absolute stroke risk than a score of 1 in a woman (driven by female sex alone).

There are no RCTs specifically enrolling patients with CHA2DS2-VASc of 1 to test anticoagulation vs. no treatment. The evidence comes from subgroup analyses of larger trials and observational registries.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  STRENGTH OF EVIDENCE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- **Overall quality:** Moderate (registry data and RCT subgroups; no dedicated RCT for this population)
- **Consistency:** Generally consistent that stroke risk is ~1%/year, but inconsistent on whether treatment benefit outweighs bleeding risk
- **Directness:** Indirect; no RCT directly answers "should CHA2DS2-VASc 1 be anticoagulated?"
- **Key limitations:** The threshold at which anticoagulation benefit exceeds bleeding risk depends on individual bleeding risk (HAS-BLED score), which varies widely

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CLINICAL BOTTOM LINE                ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

For a man with AFib and CHA2DS2-VASc of 1, current AHA/ACC guidelines (2019) give anticoagulation a Class IIa recommendation ("should be considered"). The decision should weigh the specific risk factor present, the patient's bleeding risk, and patient preference. For a woman whose only point is female sex, the effective score is 0, and anticoagulation is generally not recommended.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  CAVEATS                             ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

- CHA2DS2-VASc underestimates risk in patients with left atrial enlargement, frequent paroxysmal AFib, or significant atherosclerotic disease.
- Bleeding risk must be individually assessed; a patient with CHA2DS2-VASc of 1 and HAS-BLED of 3 has a different risk-benefit calculus than one with HAS-BLED of 0.
- The ESC guidelines (2020) differ slightly from AHA/ACC: they recommend anticoagulation for CHA2DS2-VASc >=1 in men and >=2 in women (Class I), a stronger recommendation than AHA/ACC.

```
╔══════════════════════════════════════╗
║  SUGGESTED FURTHER READING           ║
╚══════════════════════════════════════╝
```

1. January CT et al. "2019 AHA/ACC/HRS Focused Update of the 2014 Guideline for the Management of Patients with Atrial Fibrillation." *Circulation*. 2019. (Current US guideline)
2. Hindricks G et al. "2020 ESC Guidelines for the Diagnosis and Management of Atrial Fibrillation." *Eur Heart J*. 2021. (European guideline, slightly different threshold)
3. Friberg L et al. "Assessment of Female Sex as a Risk Factor in Atrial Fibrillation." *Eur Heart J*. 2012. (Key study on sex-specific risk)

## Tips & pitfalls

- This template is designed for rapid clinical decision support, not for comprehensive literature reviews. For systematic evidence synthesis, use the **Evidence Comparison** or **PICO Research Question Builder** templates.
- The single most important pitfall: AI may fabricate plausible-sounding citations. Always verify at least the landmark trial citations against PubMed before acting on them.
- Specify the patient's age, sex, and comorbidities in the input. The answer to most clinical questions depends on the specific patient, not the question in the abstract.
- For medication questions, explicitly ask for dosing, contraindications, and monitoring if you need practical prescribing guidance. The default output focuses on whether to treat, not how.
- The model may underweight bleeding risk or overweight treatment benefit. Always calculate the individual patient's bleeding risk (HAS-BLED, HEMORR2HAGES, or similar) alongside the treatment benefit.
- When guidelines from different societies disagree, the model may present one guideline's recommendation without noting the disagreement. Ask explicitly: "Do any major guidelines disagree on this?"
- For questions about rare conditions or recent drug approvals, the evidence base may be thin. The model should flag this, but if the answer feels too confident for a rare condition, ask for the number and size of supporting studies.
