## When to use this

Use this template when you have an undifferentiated clinical presentation and need a structured, ranked differential diagnosis with supporting and opposing evidence for each entry.

Generates a structured, ranked differential diagnosis from a clinical vignette. Provide the chief complaint, key history, physical exam findings, and available data, and get an organized DDx with supporting and opposing evidence for each entry.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  AI-generated differentials are a cognitive aid, not a diagnostic oracle. They can over-weight common diagnoses and miss rare or atypical presentations. Always integrate your own clinical gestalt and the full patient context.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Differential Diagnosis Generator">
You are a senior internal medicine physician acting as a diagnostic reasoning consultant.

Given the clinical presentation below, generate a structured differential diagnosis. Your output should include:

**Ranked Differential Diagnoses** (top 5–7, ordered by likelihood)

For each diagnosis, provide:
1. **Diagnosis**: name and brief category (e.g., infectious, inflammatory, vascular)
2. **Likelihood**: High / Moderate / Low, with a brief rationale
3. **Supporting features**: specific elements from the case that favor this diagnosis
4. **Against**: findings that argue against it, or classic features that are absent
5. **Clinching test**: the single best next test or finding that would confirm or exclude this diagnosis

**Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses**
List any high-acuity diagnoses that must be actively ruled out even if currently less likely. For each, specify the test or evaluation required.

**Synthesis**
In 2–3 sentences, state your leading diagnosis, your key reasoning, and the initial workup priority.

Rules:
- Do not infer history, labs, or exam findings not explicitly stated below.
- If critical information is missing, state what you need and why it matters.
- Do not fabricate test results or clinical data.

---
CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
Chief Complaint: [CHIEF COMPLAINT]
Age / Sex: [AGE AND SEX]
Duration of symptoms: [DURATION AND ONSET, e.g., acute vs. subacute vs. chronic]
Key History: [RELEVANT HISTORY, ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS, PERTINENT NEGATIVES]
Past Medical History: [RELEVANT PMH, MEDICATIONS, ALLERGIES]
Exam Findings: [VITAL SIGNS AND PERTINENT PHYSICAL EXAM FINDINGS]
Available Data: [ANY LABS, IMAGING, ECG, OR OTHER RESULTS]
</PromptPlayground>

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Breadth | Add: "Expand to top 10 diagnoses" or "Focus only on the top 3 most likely" |
| Specialty | Add: "Prioritize [cardiology / neurology / GI / rheumatology] diagnoses" |
| Acuity | Add: "This is an emergency department presentation. Emphasize time-sensitive diagnoses." |
| Age group | Add: "This is a [pediatric / geriatric] patient. Use age-appropriate differentials." |
| Teaching | Add: "After the differential, provide a one-paragraph teaching point on the pathophysiology of the leading diagnosis." |

### Emergency / Time-Critical DDx

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Emergency Differential Diagnosis Generator">
You are an emergency medicine attending acting as a rapid diagnostic reasoning consultant.

Given the acute presentation below, generate a time-critical differential diagnosis. Prioritize emergent and life-threatening diagnoses that require action within minutes to hours.

**Immediately Life-Threatening Diagnoses** (must rule out first)
For each:
1. **Diagnosis**: name and mechanism of death/deterioration if missed
2. **Key supporting or concerning features** in this case
3. **Rule-out test or action**: the single fastest way to confirm or exclude — and the decision point (e.g., "If troponin negative x2 and HEART score ≤3, low risk")

**Likely Diagnoses** (top 3–5, ordered by probability)
For each:
1. **Diagnosis**: name and category
2. **Supporting features** from this case
3. **Against**: findings that argue against, or classic features absent
4. **Next step**: the single most important test or intervention right now

**Disposition-Driving Questions**
List 2–3 clinical questions whose answers will determine whether this patient needs admission, observation, or can be safely discharged.

**Synthesis**
In 2–3 sentences: What is the most dangerous possibility? What is the most likely diagnosis? What single action do you take first?

Rules:
- Time matters. Prioritize ruling out the most dangerous diagnoses before pursuing the most likely.
- Do not infer findings not explicitly stated.
- If critical information is missing, state what you need immediately.

---
CLINICAL PRESENTATION:
Chief Complaint: [CHIEF COMPLAINT]
Age / Sex: [AGE AND SEX]
Onset: [ACUTE / SUDDEN / GRADUAL, WITH TIMELINE]
Key History: [RELEVANT HISTORY, ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS, PERTINENT NEGATIVES]
Past Medical History: [RELEVANT PMH, MEDICATIONS, ALLERGIES]
Vitals: [HR, BP, RR, O2 SAT, TEMP]
Exam Findings: [PERTINENT PHYSICAL EXAM FINDINGS]
Available Data: [ANY LABS, IMAGING, ECG ALREADY OBTAINED]
</PromptPlayground>

## Example output

> **Prompt used:** DDx Generator with a 58-year-old male presenting with acute chest pain.
>
> **Clinical presentation provided:** 58M, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, 40-pack-year smoking history. Acute substernal chest pressure for 2 hours, radiating to left arm, with diaphoresis and nausea. BP 158/94, HR 102, RR 20, O2 98%. Exam: diaphoretic, no murmurs or rubs, lungs clear. ECG shows ST depression in V4-V6. Troponin pending.

**Ranked Differential Diagnoses**

1. **Acute Coronary Syndrome (NSTEMI)** — Cardiovascular
   - **Likelihood:** High — Classic presentation with multiple cardiac risk factors, ST depression, and typical symptoms.
   - **Supporting:** Substernal pressure with radiation, diaphoresis, ST depression in lateral leads, age, smoking, HTN, DM.
   - **Against:** No ST elevation (makes STEMI less likely, but NSTEMI remains high on the list). Lungs are clear (arguing against acute decompensation).
   - **Clinching test:** Serial troponins at 0 and 3 hours. A rise-and-fall pattern confirms myocardial injury.

2. **Unstable Angina** — Cardiovascular
   - **Likelihood:** High — If troponins return negative, this remains the leading diagnosis given the ECG changes and symptom profile.
   - **Supporting:** Same features as NSTEMI without biochemical confirmation yet.
   - **Against:** ST depression suggests true ischemia with likely positive troponin.
   - **Clinching test:** Negative serial troponins with resolution of symptoms and ECG changes.

3. **Aortic Dissection** — Vascular
   - **Likelihood:** Low-Moderate — Hypertension is a risk factor. Must be excluded given catastrophic consequences.
   - **Supporting:** Hypertension, acute chest pain, diaphoresis.
   - **Against:** Pain described as pressure rather than tearing, no pulse differential or back pain reported, no mediastinal widening mentioned.
   - **Clinching test:** CT angiography of chest if clinical suspicion rises. Check bilateral arm BPs.

4. **Pulmonary Embolism** — Vascular
   - **Likelihood:** Low — No dyspnea at rest, O2 sat normal, no leg swelling or immobilization history mentioned.
   - **Supporting:** Tachycardia, acute onset.
   - **Against:** Lack of dyspnea, normal O2 sat, ECG findings more consistent with ischemia than right heart strain.
   - **Clinching test:** D-dimer (if Wells score low) or CTPA.

5. **Hypertensive Emergency with End-Organ Injury** — Cardiovascular
   - **Likelihood:** Low — BP is elevated but not in the severe range (>180/120). ECG changes could represent demand ischemia from hypertension.
   - **Supporting:** Elevated BP, ST depression, symptoms.
   - **Against:** BP 158/94 is not typically in the hypertensive emergency range.
   - **Clinching test:** Troponin trend and BP response to treatment.

**Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses**
- **Aortic dissection:** Check bilateral arm blood pressures, obtain CT angiography if any asymmetry or widened mediastinum on CXR.
- **STEMI equivalent:** Repeat ECG in 15 minutes to assess for evolving ST changes. Posterior leads (V7-V9) if isolated ST depression in V4-V6.

**Synthesis**
The leading diagnosis is NSTEMI given the classic presentation with multiple risk factors, lateral ST depression, and ongoing symptoms. Initial workup priority is serial troponins at 0 and 3 hours, aspirin and heparin per ACS protocol, cardiology notification, and repeat ECG. Aortic dissection should be excluded with bilateral BPs and clinical reassessment before initiating anticoagulation.

## Tips & pitfalls

- This prompt works well with both **GPT-4o** and **Claude**. Both models handle DDx generation effectively when the case is clearly presented with pertinent negatives.
- This template is optimized for **undifferentiated presentations**: chest pain, dyspnea, altered mental status, fever, and similar common chief complaints where the diagnosis is not yet clear.
- The **must-not-miss** section is often the most clinically valuable output. Use it as a rapid safety checklist.
- Include pertinent negatives explicitly, as they significantly improve output quality -- not just positive findings.
- For specialty-specific differentials (e.g., neurology localization, rheumatologic workup), consider adding specialty-specific framing in the customization section.
- This template is for clinical reasoning support, not board prep. For practice differentials, see the **Differential Diagnosis Drill** template under Board Prep.
- **AI models tend to anchor on common diagnoses.** If the presentation is atypical or the patient has not responded to standard treatment, use the Rare Disease Focus variation or the Bias Check template to challenge the AI's output.
- **Never accept an AI-generated differential as complete.** Models can miss rare diagnoses, pediatric-specific presentations, and context-dependent diagnoses (e.g., immunocompromised hosts, post-procedural complications).
- **Verify all "clinching tests" against current evidence.** AI may recommend outdated or low-sensitivity tests. Cross-check with UpToDate or current guidelines.
