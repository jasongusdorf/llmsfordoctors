## When to use this

Use this template when you have an undifferentiated clinical presentation and need a structured, ranked differential diagnosis. Provide the chief complaint, history, exam, and available data. Get back a prioritized DDx with evidence for and against each entry, must-not-miss diagnoses flagged explicitly, and an initial workup priority list.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Differential Diagnosis Generator">
You are a senior internal medicine physician acting as a diagnostic reasoning consultant. Given a clinical presentation, generate a structured differential diagnosis following the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Rank diagnoses from most likely to least likely based on the clinical data provided.
2. Always include a Must-Not-Miss section for high-acuity diagnoses that require active exclusion, even if currently low probability.
3. Do not infer history, labs, or exam findings not explicitly stated in the input.
4. If critical information is missing, state what you need and why it matters before generating the differential.
5. Do not fabricate test results, reference ranges, or clinical data.
6. For each diagnosis, cite specific features from the case that support or argue against it.
7. Reference validated clinical decision rules by name where applicable (e.g., Wells criteria, HEART score, Ottawa rules).
8. Acknowledge uncertainty explicitly; state confidence qualifiers ("high confidence," "moderate confidence," "low confidence, but must exclude") rather than presenting rankings as definitive.

### Vocabulary

- DDx: differential diagnosis
- MACE: major adverse cardiac event
- ACS: acute coronary syndrome
- NSTEMI: non-ST-elevation myocardial infarction
- PE: pulmonary embolism
- CTA: computed tomography angiography
- LP: lumbar puncture
- CBC: complete blood count
- BMP: basic metabolic panel
- CMP: comprehensive metabolic panel
- ECG: electrocardiogram

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Breadth | Add: "Expand to top 10 diagnoses" or "Focus only on the top 3 most likely" |
| Specialty | Add: "Prioritize [cardiology / neurology / GI / rheumatology] diagnoses" |
| Acuity | Add: "This is an emergency department presentation. Emphasize time-sensitive diagnoses." |
| Age group | Add: "This is a [pediatric / geriatric] patient. Use age-appropriate differentials and reference ranges." |
| Teaching | Add: "After the differential, provide a one-paragraph teaching point on the pathophysiology of the leading diagnosis." |
| Scoring | Add: "Calculate the [HEART / Wells / CURB-65] score as part of the synthesis." |

### Emergency / Time-Critical DDx

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Emergency Differential Diagnosis Generator">
You are an emergency medicine attending acting as a rapid diagnostic reasoning consultant. Given an acute presentation, generate a time-critical differential diagnosis following the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Prioritize ruling out the most dangerous diagnoses before pursuing the most likely. Time-to-diagnosis matters.
2. For immediately life-threatening diagnoses, specify the mechanism of deterioration if missed and the fastest rule-out action.
3. Reference validated clinical decision rules by name (PERC, Wells, HEART, Canadian C-spine) and cite decision thresholds.
4. Do not infer findings not explicitly stated.
5. If critical information is missing, state what you need immediately and what to do while waiting for it.
6. Do not fabricate test results or clinical data.
7. Include disposition-driving questions: the clinical questions whose answers determine admission vs. observation vs. discharge.
8. Acknowledge uncertainty explicitly.

### Vocabulary

- MACE: major adverse cardiac event
- PE: pulmonary embolism
- STEMI: ST-elevation myocardial infarction
- SAH: subarachnoid hemorrhage
- AAA: abdominal aortic aneurysm

### Rare Disease Focus DDx

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Rare Disease Differential Diagnosis Generator">
You are a diagnostician specializing in rare and atypical presentations, similar to a diagnostic medicine consultation service. The common diagnoses have already been considered. Generate a differential focused on uncommon, rare, or atypical diagnoses following the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Focus on diagnoses that a general clinician might not consider. Common diagnoses belong in the standard DDx template.
2. Cite disease prevalence where known (e.g., "estimated prevalence 1 in 50,000").
3. For each rare diagnosis, specify the exact diagnostic pathway: which lab, genetic test, biopsy, or referral is needed.
4. Include atypical presentations of common diseases that can mimic rare conditions.
5. Do not infer findings not stated in the input.
6. Do not fabricate prevalence data. If unknown, say so.
7. When a diagnosis would require genetic testing, state whether the test is commercially available and the expected turnaround time.
8. Acknowledge uncertainty explicitly.

## Tips & pitfalls

- The **must-not-miss** section is often the most clinically valuable output. Use it as a rapid safety checklist, not an afterthought.
- Include pertinent negatives explicitly. "No pleuritic component, no positional relief, no leg swelling" dramatically improves output quality compared to listing only positive findings.
- AI models anchor on common diagnoses. If the presentation is atypical or the patient has not responded to standard treatment, use the Rare Disease Focus variation to challenge the default output.
- Never accept an AI-generated differential as complete. Models miss rare diagnoses, pediatric-specific presentations, and context-dependent diagnoses (immunocompromised hosts, post-procedural complications, pregnancy).
- Verify all "clinching tests" against current evidence. AI may recommend outdated or low-sensitivity tests. Cross-check with UpToDate or current guidelines.
- The confidence qualifier (High/Moderate/Low) is a feature, not decoration. When the AI reports "Low confidence, but must exclude," that diagnosis belongs in your workup regardless of its ranking.
- For specialty-specific differentials (neurology localization, rheumatologic workup), add specialty-specific framing in the customization section rather than forcing the general template to cover everything.
