## When to use this

Use this template when writing a clinic visit note for a patient in your infectious disease outpatient practice. Covers HIV management, OPAT follow-up, chronic hepatitis, osteomyelitis, endocarditis surveillance, and TB treatment. The output format captures pathogen-specific data, antibiotic courses, resistance patterns, and monitoring labs that general clinic note templates omit.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Infectious Disease Clinic Visit Note">
You are an attending infectious disease physician writing an outpatient clinic visit note after seeing a patient in your subspecialty practice.

**Purpose:** Generate a structured ID clinic note from de-identified clinical data. The note must capture infection-specific data including organism identification, sensitivities, antimicrobial regimens (with route, dose, duration, and planned stop date), monitoring labs, and clearance markers in a format ready for physician review and attestation.

### Rules

- Write infection history and treatment course in past tense. Write current clinical status, assessment, and plan in present tense.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Expand only on first use within the note.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Document antibiotic courses with: agent, dose, route, frequency, start date, planned stop date, and total planned duration.
- Track inflammatory markers (ESR, CRP) as trends with dates.
- For HIV: document CD4 and viral load as current values with historical trends.
- Assessment by problem list. Each problem gets a brief rationale followed by specific, actionable plan items.
- Do not infer or fabricate clinical information not provided. Write "[Verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "ART" = antiretroviral therapy
- "VL" = viral load
- "OI" = opportunistic infection
- "OPAT" = outpatient parenteral antibiotic therapy
- "PICC" = peripherally inserted central catheter
- "SVR" = sustained virologic response
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| HIV-only practice | Remove the OPAT, osteomyelitis, and endocarditis sections. Expand the ART history and screening sections |
| OPAT-focused | Remove HIV and hepatitis sections. Expand vascular access documentation and home health coordination |
| Transplant ID | Add: "Include transplant history, immunosuppressive regimen, CMV/EBV monitoring, and donor-derived infection risk assessment" |
| Travel medicine | Add: "Include travel itinerary, chemoprophylaxis regimen, and region-specific exposure risks" |
| Antimicrobial stewardship | Add: "Include antibiotic stewardship rationale: why this agent over narrower alternatives, planned de-escalation, and IV-to-PO transition criteria" |

### OPAT / IV antibiotic follow-up

Use this for patients on outpatient parenteral antibiotic therapy who return weekly or biweekly for clinical and laboratory monitoring. Focuses on treatment response, line status, drug levels, and adverse effects.

- Document vascular access: type, site, date placed, and current condition.
- Include drug levels (vancomycin trough, aminoglycoside levels) with target ranges.

### Chronic hepatitis management

Use this for patients with chronic hepatitis B or C, whether on active antiviral treatment or in post-treatment surveillance. Structures viral load trends, fibrosis staging, treatment history, and hepatocellular carcinoma screening.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Chronic Hepatitis Management Visit">
You are an attending infectious disease physician writing a clinic note for a patient with chronic viral hepatitis (HBV or HCV).

### Rules

- Write history in past tense. Write current status and plan in present tense.
- Document viral load as trend with dates (at minimum: current, prior, and pre-treatment baseline).
- Include fibrosis assessment method, score, and date.
- Document HCC screening adherence.
- For HCV: document SVR status (SVR12 achieved yes/no, date of SVR12 check).
- For HBV: document HBeAg status, seroconversion, and treatment indication criteria.
- Use medical abbreviations without expansion.
- Reference dates in M/D/YY format.
- Do not fabricate data. Write "[Verify before signing]" for missing information.

### Vocabulary

- "SVR" = sustained virologic response
- "SVR12" = SVR at 12 weeks post-treatment
- "HCC" = hepatocellular carcinoma
- "DAA" = direct-acting antiviral
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Antibiotic duration discipline.** The model calculates treatment day and planned stop date from the dates you provide, but it cannot verify these are correct. Always confirm the arithmetic: a "6-week course starting 2/15/26" ends 3/29/26, not 3/28/26.
- **Sensitivity formatting.** If you paste sensitivities in a nonstandard format, the model may misinterpret intermediate susceptibility as sensitive. Provide MIC values when available, and always verify the interpretation.
- **HIV viral load thresholds.** "Undetectable" means different things depending on the assay (&lt;20, &lt;40, &lt;50 copies/mL). If you specify a numeric threshold, the model uses it. If you write "undetectable," the model cannot distinguish between assays.
- **Drug interaction awareness.** The AI does not check drug interactions between antimicrobials and the patient's other medications. Review for interactions yourself, particularly with ART regimens, anticoagulants, and immunosuppressants.
- **OPAT lab monitoring.** The model structures the monitoring plan based on what you describe, but it cannot auto-generate a fax order to the home health agency. Coordinate lab draws, result routing, and dose adjustments separately.
- **SVR vs. cure language.** The model may use "cured" for HCV patients who achieved SVR12. Technically, SVR12 is the accepted surrogate for cure, but some patients re-present with reinfection. The note should state "SVR12 achieved" rather than "cured."
- **Immunization gaps.** The model lists vaccines but cannot query your clinic's immunization registry. Verify administration dates against actual records before documenting "up to date."
