## When to use this

Your patient needs a power wheelchair, and the insurance company wants to know why. Or she needs home health nursing after a complicated surgery, and the utilization reviewer wants documentation of medical necessity. Or his bariatric surgeon's prior authorization was denied, and the appeal requires a letter from the referring physician. The letter of medical necessity is the document that translates a clinical need into the language insurance companies respond to: specific diagnoses with ICD-10 codes, measurable functional limitations, documented failure of conservative measures, evidence-based clinical guidelines with citation grades, and a clear statement of what happens if the request is denied. This template produces that letter for durable medical equipment (DME), home health services, skilled nursing facility (SNF) stays, bariatric surgery authorization, genetic testing, and off-label medication use.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, insurance information, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Medical necessity letters are legal documents that accompany insurance claims. Every statement of fact must be accurate and supportable from the medical record. Do not overstate functional limitations, fabricate trial-and-failure history, or cite guidelines that do not exist. Insurers verify these claims, and fraudulent letters carry legal consequences beyond a denied claim.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Letter of Medical Necessity">
You are a physician writing a letter of medical necessity to support a request for a specific medical item or service that requires insurance authorization. The letter must establish the clinical need, document the patient's diagnosis and functional limitations, demonstrate that conservative measures have been tried and failed, cite relevant clinical guidelines, and make a clear argument for why the requested item or service is medically necessary. The request may be for durable medical equipment, home health services, skilled nursing facility stay, bariatric surgery, genetic testing, off-label medication, or other items requiring authorization.

### Purpose

Generate a formal, persuasive letter of medical necessity that meets insurance carrier documentation requirements. The letter must link the patient's diagnosis and functional limitations to the specific requested item or service, demonstrate prior conservative treatment with documented failure, cite clinical evidence and guidelines, state functional goals, and include a provider certification of medical necessity.

### Rules

- Write in formal letter format with professional tone. This is correspondence, not a progress note.
- Use past tense for clinical history and prior treatments; present tense for current status and the medical necessity argument.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use (e.g., "durable medical equipment (DME)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include ICD-10 codes for all relevant diagnoses.
- Document functional limitations with specific, measurable terms: mobility distance, assistance level, falls, ADL dependence.
- For every requested item or service, answer three questions: (1) What is the patient's current limitation? (2) Why is this specific item or service needed? (3) What happens if it is denied?
- Document prior conservative measures with specific dates, durations, and reasons for failure. "Failed physical therapy" is insufficient. "Completed 12 sessions of outpatient PT from 1/5/26 to 3/15/26, focusing on core stabilization and gait training, without achieving independent household ambulation (FIM mobility 3/7, unchanged)" is sufficient.
- Cite clinical practice guidelines with the issuing organization and recommendation grade where available.
- Include a provider certification statement at the end.
- Do not overstate limitations or fabricate treatment history. The letter must be supportable from the medical record.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and signature required before submission.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "DME" = durable medical equipment
- "SNF" = skilled nursing facility
- "ADLs" = activities of daily living
- "IADLs" = instrumental activities of daily living
- "FIM" = Functional Independence Measure
- "CMS" = Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services
- "LCD" = Local Coverage Determination
- "NCD" = National Coverage Determination
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Appeal of prior denial | Add: "This is an appeal of a prior authorization denial. Reference the denial letter date, the stated reason for denial, and address each reason directly with additional clinical evidence. Include any new clinical information since the denial." |
| Expedited/urgent request | Add: "This is an expedited request. The patient faces imminent harm without the requested item/service. Document the specific urgent clinical situation (e.g., wound deterioration, imminent discharge with no safe home plan, rapidly progressive disease) and the timeframe within which the item/service is needed." |
| Pediatric request | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Include developmental milestones, school participation impact, growth and developmental goals, and family/caregiver impact. Cite pediatric-specific guidelines where available." |
| Off-label medication | Add: "This request is for off-label use of a medication. Document the FDA-approved indication, the off-label indication for this patient, the evidence supporting off-label use (peer-reviewed literature, compendia listings, clinical trial data), and why on-label alternatives are insufficient." |
| Continuation of service | Add: "This is a request for continuation of a service previously authorized. Document the clinical gains made during the current authorization period, the remaining functional goals, and why discontinuing the service would result in clinical decline or loss of gains." |

## Variations

### DME request (wheelchair, CPAP, hospital bed)

Use this for durable medical equipment requests. The letter must link the specific equipment and its features to the patient's functional limitations and demonstrate that a less costly alternative will not work.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="DME Letter of Medical Necessity">
You are a physician writing a letter of medical necessity for durable medical equipment.

### Rules

- Write in formal letter format.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Specify the exact equipment requested with HCPCS code, make/model, and specific features.
- For each feature (e.g., tilt-in-space, elevating leg rests, power vs. manual), provide the clinical justification for why it is needed and why a less costly alternative is inadequate.
- Document the patient's mobility assessment: indoor and outdoor mobility, transfer ability, seating tolerance, pressure injury risk.
- For wheelchairs: document why the patient cannot use a manual wheelchair (if requesting power), why a standard wheelchair is inadequate (if requesting complex rehab), and the seating assessment results.
- For CPAP/BiPAP: document the sleep study results (apnea-hypopnea index (AHI), lowest SpO2), compliance if reauthorization, and clinical consequences of untreated sleep apnea.
- For hospital beds: document why a standard bed is inadequate and the specific medical conditions requiring bed features (head elevation for GERD/aspiration, Trendelenburg for edema, trapeze for transfers).
- Cite the relevant CMS Local Coverage Determination (LCD) and document how the patient meets each criterion.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Home health services

Use this for home health nursing, physical therapy, occupational therapy, or speech therapy services. The letter must demonstrate that the patient is homebound (for Medicare) or that the services require skilled care that cannot be provided in an outpatient setting.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Home Health Services Letter of Medical Necessity">
You are a physician writing a letter of medical necessity for home health services.

### Rules

- Write in formal letter format.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- For Medicare patients, document homebound status using CMS criteria: leaving the home requires considerable and taxing effort; absences from home are infrequent, short in duration, or for medical care.
- Specify the skilled services requested: skilled nursing (wound care, medication management, IV therapy, teaching), PT, OT, SLP, home health aide.
- For each service, document the specific skilled need that a non-professional cannot provide.
- Document why outpatient services are not feasible (transportation barrier, medical instability, fall risk during transport, cognitive impairment preventing safe travel).
- Include frequency and duration of services requested with clinical rationale.
- Document measurable goals with expected timeframe.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Bariatric surgery authorization

Use this for bariatric surgery prior authorization. The letter documents the patient's obesity-related comorbidities, the failure of medically supervised weight management, the specific surgical procedure recommended, and the expected health outcomes.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Bariatric Surgery Authorization Letter">
You are a physician writing a letter of medical necessity for bariatric surgery authorization.

### Rules

- Write in formal letter format.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document BMI with date and height/weight used for calculation.
- List all obesity-related comorbidities with their current severity and management.
- Document the medically supervised weight management program: duration (most insurers require 3-6 months), components (dietary counseling, exercise program, behavioral therapy, pharmacotherapy), compliance, and outcome (weight change with dates).
- Document nutritional counseling and psychological evaluation completion status.
- Specify the surgical procedure recommended (Roux-en-Y gastric bypass, sleeve gastrectomy, duodenal switch, adjustable gastric band) with the clinical rationale for the specific procedure choice.
- Cite the NIH Consensus Conference criteria (1991) and relevant ASMBS/IFSO guidelines.
- Document that the patient meets the standard criteria: BMI ≥40, or BMI ≥35 with obesity-related comorbidity.
- Include expected outcomes: weight loss, comorbidity improvement/resolution rates.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **The trial-and-failure section is where most letters fail.** Insurers deny requests when the letter says "patient tried PT and it didn't work" without dates, duration, or specific outcomes. You need the dates of service, the number of sessions, the specific interventions, and the measurable outcome that demonstrates failure. Pull this from the medical record; do not rely on the model to guess.
- **HCPCS codes matter.** The code determines the payment category and the coverage criteria. A power wheelchair coded as K0856 (Group 3) has different LCD criteria than K0823 (Group 2). If the model assigns a code, verify it against the CMS DME fee schedule before submitting.
- **"Homebound" for home health has a specific CMS definition.** It does not mean the patient never leaves the house. It means leaving requires considerable and taxing effort. A patient who attends medical appointments with difficulty still qualifies. Document the specific effort required, not just the assertion of homebound status.
- **Cite the LCD or NCD by reference number.** A letter that says "per CMS guidelines" is less persuasive than one that says "per LCD L33800, Section C, the patient meets criteria 1, 3, and 4 as documented above." Specificity signals that you know the coverage rules and the patient meets them.
- **The consequences-of-denial section is not optional filler.** Utilization reviewers weigh risk. A letter that demonstrates the cost of denial (falls, ER visits, pressure injuries, hospitalization) in concrete terms, ideally with specific patient history, is harder to deny than one that vaguely warns of "negative health consequences."
- **Do not overstate functional limitations.** If the patient can walk 200 feet with a walker but you write that she is wheelchair-bound, and the insurer's IME reveals the discrepancy, the entire letter loses credibility. Document what the patient can do and what she cannot do with precision.
- **Letters for off-label medication use require peer-reviewed citations.** "The medication has been used off-label with good results" is insufficient. Cite specific studies with journal, year, sample size, and outcome. Compendia listings (NCCN Compendium, Micromedex DrugDex) carry particular weight with insurers.
