## When to use this

Your patient just had a procedure and needs to know how to take care of themselves at home: what the wound should look like, what they can and cannot do, how to manage pain, and what should bring them back. This template generates clear recovery instructions tailored to the specific procedure performed. It works for anything from an office biopsy to a cardiac catheterization. The output supplements (not replaces) your institution's standardized post-operative instruction sheets.

Pair this with the Procedure Preparation Guide template for a complete pre-and-post set.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, procedure dates, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Post-Procedure Care Instructions">
You are a physician writing post-procedure recovery instructions for a patient. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to make sure they know how to care for themselves at home and can recognize when something is wrong.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Warning signs must be concrete and specific: "redness spreading more than 1 inch from the incision" not "signs of infection."
- Pain medication instructions must include name, dose, frequency, and maximum daily amount.
- Activity restrictions must include specific durations, not "as tolerated."
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "cut" or "incision" not "surgical site"
- Use "stitches" not "sutures" (define sutures in parentheses if needed)
- Use the simplest accurate word available

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Minor procedure | Add: "This was a minor office procedure. Keep the entire output to 1 page." |
| Major surgery | Add: "This was a major surgery with general anesthesia. Include a week-by-week recovery timeline." |

### Cardiac catheterization

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Post-Catheterization Care">
You are a cardiologist writing post-cardiac catheterization care instructions for a patient. The patient has no medical background.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- Include access-site-specific instructions (groin vs. wrist).
- If a stent was placed, emphasize the critical importance of not stopping blood thinners.
- Warning signs must be specific and concrete.

### Output format

```
YOUR HEART CATHETERIZATION RECOVERY
══════════════════════════════════════════

What Was Done
─────────────
[2-3 sentences explaining cardiac catheterization
in plain language.]

Catheter Site Care
──────────────────
  [Instructions specific to the access site]

  If your catheter was in your groin:
  ────────────────────────────────────
  - Keep the bandage on for: ***
  - Keep your leg straight for: ***
  - Do not bend at the waist or strain
  - A small bruise is normal
  - A large or growing bruise is NOT normal

  If your catheter was in your wrist:
  ───────────────────────────────────
  - Keep the wrist band on for: ***
  - You can use your hand for light tasks
  - No gripping or lifting for: ***

  Normal: small bruise, mild tenderness
  NOT normal: large or growing lump, numbness,
  color change in hand or foot

Activity Restrictions
─────────────────────
- No heavy lifting (more than 10 lbs) for: ***
- No driving for: ***
- No strenuous exercise for: ***
- Climb stairs slowly
- Light walking around the house is fine
- Return to work: ***

Your Medications
────────────────
- Continue all heart medications as prescribed.

  [If a stent was placed:]
  CRITICAL: Do not stop your blood thinner
  (*** / Plavix) without talking to your heart
  doctor first. Stopping too early can cause a
  blood clot in the stent. This can be life-
  threatening.

- Aspirin: continue as directed

Call Your Heart Doctor If
─────────────────────────
- Increasing bruising or swelling at the catheter
  site
- Numbness or tingling in the hand or leg used
- Mild bleeding that stops with pressure
- Fever above 100.4 degrees F
- New or worsening chest pain

Go to the ER or Call 911 If
───────────────────────────
- Heavy bleeding that does not stop with 10 minutes
  of firm pressure
- Your leg or hand becomes cold, pale, or numb
- Severe chest pain or shortness of breath
- You feel faint or lightheaded
- A large, hard lump forms at the catheter site

Follow-Up
─────────
- Schedule your follow-up: ***
- At that visit: ***
- Questions? Call: ***
```

## Example output

```
YOUR RECOVERY INSTRUCTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════

What Was Done
─────────────
You had a colonoscopy. The doctor used a thin,
flexible tube with a camera to look at the inside
of your large intestine (colon). During the
procedure, the doctor found and removed 2 small
polyps (growths on the lining of the colon). The
polyps were sent to a lab to be tested. Removing
polyps helps prevent colon cancer.

Wound and Site Care
───────────────────
There is no incision or wound to care for. The
procedure was done through your rectum. You may
have mild soreness in that area for the rest of
today.

Activity Restrictions
─────────────────────
| Activity             | When You Can Resume          |
|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Walking around house | Right away                   |
| Driving              | Tomorrow (24 hrs after sedation)|
| Light exercise       | Tomorrow                     |
| Heavy exercise       | In 3 days                    |
| Return to work       | Tomorrow (if no heavy lifting)|
| Heavy lifting (>25 lbs)| In 5 days                  |
| Signing legal docs   | Tomorrow                     |

Pain Management
───────────────
  What to Expect
  ──────────────
  - Mild bloating and cramping are normal. This is
    from the air used during the procedure. It will
    pass within a few hours.
  - Walking helps move the air through faster.
  - A warm heating pad on your belly may help with
    cramping.

  For Pain
  ────────
  - Tylenol (acetaminophen) 500 mg every 6 hours
    if needed.
  - Avoid aspirin and ibuprofen for 5 days (since
    polyps were removed, these can increase bleeding
    risk).

  Pain That Is NOT Normal
  ───────────────────────
  - Severe belly pain that is getting worse, not
    better. This needs medical attention.

Warning Signs
─────────────
Call your doctor if:
- Belly pain that lasts more than 24 hours
- Small amount of blood in your stool (a few drops
  is common after polyp removal; more than that is
  not)
- Nausea or vomiting lasting more than a few hours
- Fever above 100.4 degrees F

Go to the ER if:
- Large amount of blood from your rectum (more
  than a few tablespoons)
- Severe belly pain
- Black, tarry stools
- You feel faint, lightheaded, or your heart is
  racing

Follow-Up
─────────
- Your biopsy results will be ready in about 5 to
  7 business days.
- The doctor's office will call you with results.
- Your next colonoscopy: the doctor will recommend
  timing based on the biopsy results (usually 3-5
  years if the polyps are benign).
- Questions before then? Call the office at
  [phone number].
```

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Pain medication instructions are high-risk for AI errors.** Verify every medication name, dose, and interval against the actual prescription. Pay special attention to maximum daily acetaminophen (no more than 3,000 mg/day for most patients, lower for those with liver disease).
- **The "Go to the ER" section is the most safety-critical output.** Warning signs must be specific to the procedure performed. A colonoscopy with polypectomy has different red flags than a cardiac catheterization. Review every line.
- **For procedures with sedation, make the 24-hour restrictions prominent.** Patients feel fine after sedation wears off and underestimate residual cognitive effects. Some will try to drive home.
- **Wound care varies by procedure.** AI generates reasonable general wound care, but your institution likely has specific protocols for dressing changes, wound checks, and drain management. Add those to the input or review and modify the output.
- **Supplement, do not replace, institutional post-op sheets.** Your institution's standardized post-operative instructions have been reviewed by surgery, nursing, and legal. This template generates a more readable companion, not a substitute.
- **This template pairs with the Procedure Preparation Guide.** Together they create a complete pre-and-post set that covers the patient's full experience.
- **Consider the patient's support system.** If someone else will be managing wound care or medications at home, note that in the prompt input so the output addresses the caregiver.
