## When to use this

Use this template when you have a clinical question and need to formulate it into a structured, searchable format. It converts a plain-language question into PICO format, generates MeSH terms, and produces a ready-to-paste PubMed search string. Ideal for evidence-based medicine assignments, starting a literature review, or systematically answering a clinical question that came up on rounds.

Turns a clinical question into a structured PICO format and generates a literature search strategy. Describe your clinical question in plain language and get a formatted PICO, MeSH terms, and a ready-to-use PubMed search string. Perfect for evidence-based medicine assignments, research projects, or answering clinical questions systematically.

<Callout type="tip">
  PICO stands for Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome. It's the standard framework for formulating searchable clinical questions. This template handles therapy, diagnosis, prognosis, and etiology question types.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="PICO Research Question Builder">
You are a medical librarian helping a clinician formulate a research question and search strategy.

Given the clinical question below, provide:

**PICO Breakdown**
- **P (Population):** Who are the patients? (age, sex, condition, setting)
- **I (Intervention/Exposure):** What is being done or studied?
- **C (Comparison):** What is the alternative? (placebo, standard care, another intervention)
- **O (Outcome):** What do we want to know? (mortality, symptom relief, diagnosis accuracy, etc.)

**Question Type**
Classify as: Therapy / Diagnosis / Prognosis / Etiology / Prevention

**Formatted Clinical Question**
"In [P], does [I] compared to [C] improve [O]?"

**Search Strategy**
- **MeSH Terms:** List relevant MeSH headings for each PICO element
- **Keywords:** Alternative text words and synonyms for each element
- **PubMed Search String:** A ready-to-paste Boolean search query combining MeSH and keywords with AND/OR operators
- **Filters to Consider:** Study type, date range, language, human subjects

**Suggested Study Designs**
What types of studies would best answer this question? (RCT, systematic review, cohort, etc.)

**Alternative PICO Formulations**
Suggest 1–2 alternative framings of the question that might yield additional relevant results.

## Variations

### PICO for RCT design

Use this when you are designing a randomized controlled trial and need to formalize your research question, define your endpoints, and plan your study methodology.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="PICO for RCT Design">
You are a clinical trialist helping a physician-researcher design a randomized controlled trial.

Given the research question below, produce a structured RCT planning document:

**PICO Breakdown**
- **P (Population):** Target population with specific inclusion/exclusion criteria
- **I (Intervention):** Specific intervention with dose, duration, and delivery method
- **C (Comparator):** Control arm (placebo, active comparator, usual care) with justification
- **O (Outcomes):** Primary and secondary outcomes with measurement tools and timepoints

**Study Design Recommendations**
- Recommended design: [Parallel / Crossover / Cluster / Factorial / Non-inferiority / Superiority]
- Justification for design choice
- Blinding strategy: [Open-label / Single-blind / Double-blind] with rationale
- Randomization strategy: [Simple / Stratified / Block / Cluster] with stratification variables

**Sample Size Considerations**
- What is the clinically meaningful difference (minimal clinically important difference)?
- What assumptions would go into a power calculation? (effect size, alpha, power, expected dropout)
- Suggest a rough sample size range based on similar published trials

**Potential Pitfalls**
- Key threats to internal validity
- Anticipated enrollment challenges
- Ethical considerations

**Search for Existing Evidence**
- MeSH terms and PubMed search string to find existing trials on this topic
- How to search ClinicalTrials.gov for ongoing or planned studies

Do not fabricate data or statistical estimates. Flag assumptions clearly.

---
RESEARCH QUESTION:
[Describe your research question in plain language]
</PromptPlayground>

### PECO for observational studies

Use this when your clinical question involves an exposure rather than an intervention (e.g., risk factors, prognosis, harm), which requires an observational study design rather than an RCT.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="PECO for Observational Study Design">
You are a clinical epidemiologist helping a researcher formulate a question for an observational study.

Given the clinical question below, provide:

**PECO Breakdown**
- **P (Population):** Target population with specific eligibility criteria
- **E (Exposure):** The exposure, risk factor, or prognostic factor being studied
- **C (Comparison):** The unexposed or reference group
- **O (Outcome):** The outcome of interest with measurement method

**Question Type**
Classify as: Etiology / Prognosis / Harm / Risk Factor / Natural History

**Formatted Research Question**
"In [P], is [E] compared to [C] associated with [O]?"

**Recommended Study Design**
- Best design for this question: [Cohort / Case-control / Cross-sectional / Nested case-control]
- Why this design is appropriate
- Key methodological considerations (temporality, confounding, selection bias)

**Confounding Variables**
List the most important potential confounders that should be measured and adjusted for. Explain why each is a confounder (associated with both exposure and outcome).

**Search Strategy**
- **MeSH Terms:** Relevant MeSH headings for each PECO element
- **Keywords:** Alternative text words and synonyms
- **PubMed Search String:** Ready-to-paste Boolean query
- **Observational study filters:** STROBE-compliant study types, cohort and case-control study filters

**Alternative PECO Formulations**
1–2 alternative framings that might capture additional relevant literature.

Do not fabricate data or references.

---
CLINICAL QUESTION:
[Describe your clinical question, e.g., "Does long-term shift work increase the risk of cardiovascular disease in nurses?"]
</PromptPlayground>

### Systematic review question

Use this when you are planning a systematic review and need a tightly defined, protocol-ready research question with comprehensive search strategy and PROSPERO registration components.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Systematic Review Question Development">
You are a systematic review methodologist helping a researcher develop a protocol-ready review question.

Given the research topic below, produce:

**Review Question**
A precisely worded systematic review question suitable for PROSPERO registration.

**PICO/PECO Breakdown**
- **P:** Specific population with explicit inclusion and exclusion criteria
- **I/E:** Intervention or exposure, with any subgroups
- **C:** Comparator(s), including whether no-comparator studies will be included
- **O:** Primary and secondary outcomes, with minimum follow-up duration if applicable

**Eligibility Criteria**
- Study designs to include: [RCTs only / RCTs + observational / all designs]
- Publication types: [Full publications / conference abstracts / preprints]
- Language restrictions
- Date restrictions (with justification)

**Comprehensive Search Strategy**
For each database:
- **PubMed/MEDLINE:** Full search string with MeSH terms and text words
- **Embase:** Adapted search string with Emtree terms
- **Cochrane CENTRAL:** Adapted search string
- **Additional sources:** Grey literature, ClinicalTrials.gov, reference list searching, citation tracking

**Risk of Bias Assessment**
Recommended tool for assessing bias (Cochrane RoB 2, ROBINS-I, Newcastle-Ottawa, etc.) based on expected study designs.

**Data Synthesis Plan**
- Will meta-analysis be possible? What conditions would make it appropriate?
- Anticipated sources of heterogeneity
- Planned subgroup analyses

Do not fabricate references. Flag any assumptions about the existing evidence base.

---
RESEARCH TOPIC:
[Describe the topic area and what you want to systematically review]
</PromptPlayground>

## Example output

Here is what the main prompt produces when given a realistic clinical question:

**Input provided:**
"Does early physical therapy after hip replacement reduce 30-day readmission rates compared to standard post-operative recovery protocols?"

**LLM output:**

**PICO Breakdown**
- **P (Population):** Adults (>=18 years) who have undergone primary total hip arthroplasty for osteoarthritis. Any surgical approach. Excludes revision arthroplasty, fracture-related arthroplasty, and patients with significant neurological comorbidity limiting mobility.
- **I (Intervention):** Early physical therapy, defined as PT initiated within 24 hours of surgery (day of surgery or POD0), with structured mobilization protocol.
- **C (Comparison):** Standard post-operative recovery protocol, typically PT initiated on POD1 or POD2, at the discretion of the surgical team.
- **O (Outcome):** 30-day all-cause hospital readmission rate. Secondary outcomes: length of stay, 30-day complications (DVT, PE, wound infection, dislocation), patient-reported functional outcomes (HOOS-JR, TUG test), and time to independent ambulation.

**Question Type**
Therapy

**Formatted Clinical Question**
"In adults undergoing primary total hip arthroplasty, does early physical therapy (within 24 hours of surgery) compared to standard post-operative PT timing reduce 30-day hospital readmission rates?"

**Search Strategy**
- **MeSH Terms:**
  - P: "Arthroplasty, Replacement, Hip"[MeSH]
  - I: "Early Ambulation"[MeSH], "Physical Therapy Modalities"[MeSH]
  - O: "Patient Readmission"[MeSH]
- **Keywords:**
  - P: "total hip arthroplasty" OR "total hip replacement" OR "hip arthroplasty"
  - I: "early mobilization" OR "same-day mobilization" OR "accelerated rehabilitation" OR "early physical therapy" OR "day-of-surgery PT"
  - O: "readmission" OR "rehospitalization" OR "30-day readmission"
- **PubMed Search String:**
  `("Arthroplasty, Replacement, Hip"[MeSH] OR "total hip arthroplasty"[tiab] OR "total hip replacement"[tiab]) AND ("Early Ambulation"[MeSH] OR "early mobilization"[tiab] OR "same-day mobilization"[tiab] OR "early physical therapy"[tiab] OR "accelerated rehabilitation"[tiab]) AND ("Patient Readmission"[MeSH] OR "readmission"[tiab] OR "rehospitalization"[tiab])`
- **Filters:** Humans, English, 2015-present (to reflect current surgical and rehabilitation practices)

**Suggested Study Designs**
Best evidence: RCTs or systematic reviews of RCTs. Pragmatic trials are most relevant given the workflow nature of the intervention. Large retrospective cohort studies from joint registries may provide complementary real-world data.

**Alternative PICO Formulations**
1. "In adults undergoing total hip arthroplasty, does same-day mobilization compared to next-day mobilization reduce length of stay?" (narrower intervention, different outcome)
2. "In adults undergoing total hip or total knee arthroplasty, does early PT compared to standard PT improve 90-day functional outcomes?" (broader population, longer-term outcome)

## Tips & pitfalls

- The generated PubMed search string is a starting point. You will likely need to iterate, broadening or narrowing based on the number of results.
- MeSH terms may not be perfectly matched; verify against the MeSH Browser at nlm.nih.gov/mesh/.
- For systematic reviews, the search strategy needs to be more comprehensive (multiple databases, hand-searching references). Use the **Systematic review question** variation above for protocol-grade search strategies.
- **Common AI failure mode:** The model may generate MeSH terms that do not exist or have been retired. Always verify MeSH headings in the NLM MeSH Browser before running the search.
- **Search string syntax:** PubMed, Embase, and Cochrane use different Boolean syntax. A search string generated for PubMed will not work directly in Embase without adaptation.
- **Over-specified PICO:** If your initial search returns very few results, the PICO may be too narrow. Try broadening the population or outcome, or remove one PICO element from the search string.
- **Question type matters:** Therapy questions are best answered by RCTs. Prognosis questions by cohort studies. Diagnosis questions by cross-sectional studies with a reference standard. The model's study design suggestion should match the question type.
