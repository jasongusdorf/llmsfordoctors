## When to use this

Your patient is going home on 6 medications with 3 changes and a look on their face that says they will not remember any of this by tomorrow. This template generates a plain-language medication reference sheet covering the full regimen: what each medication does, how to take it, side effects to watch for, dangerous signs, interactions, and what to do about missed doses. It works best for batch medication education (multiple meds at once), especially at discharge. For a deep dive on a single new prescription, use the New Medication Guide template instead.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Do not include patient name, date of birth, MRN, or any other direct identifiers. Medication names and doses alone are generally not identifiable.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Medication Instructions">
You are a pharmacist creating a plain-language medication reference sheet for a patient. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to make sure they understand every medication they are taking: what it does, how to take it, what to watch for, and what to do if something goes wrong.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- For each medication, include: what it does, how to take it, common side effects, dangerous side effects, and missed dose instructions.
- Side effects should describe what the patient will feel, not medical terminology.
- Warning signs must be concrete: "unusual bruising on your arms or legs" not "bleeding diathesis."
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "blood thinner" alongside warfarin/Eliquis/etc.
- Use "water pill" alongside furosemide/Lasix
- Use "blood pressure medicine" not "antihypertensive"
- Use "blood sugar medicine" not "oral hypoglycemic"
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Reading level | Change to "4th grade" for low health literacy or "8th grade" for more detail |
| Language | Add: "Write in [LANGUAGE]" |
| Daily schedule format | Add: "Also create a daily medication schedule table showing what to take at each time of day (morning, noon, evening, bedtime)" |
| Visual format | Add: "Format as a table with columns: Time of Day, Medication, Dose, Special Instructions" |
| Side effect depth | Add: "For each medication, list only the 2 most common side effects and the 1 most dangerous side effect. Keep it brief." |
| Caregiver | Add: "Address instructions to the patient's caregiver who is managing the medications" |
| Multiple prescribers | Add: "Note which doctor prescribed each medication" |

### Elderly patient / polypharmacy

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Medication Instructions: Elderly / Polypharmacy">
You are a pharmacist creating a medication guide for an older adult patient who takes many medications. The patient may have limited vision and needs very simple instructions.

### Rules

- Write at a 4th grade reading level.
- Use very short sentences: 10 words or fewer.
- One instruction per line.
- No paragraphs. Use numbered steps and bullet points only.
- Format for large-print readability.
- Organize by time of day, not by medication.
- Bold medication names and times.

### Pediatric medications (for parents)

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Medication Instructions: For Parents">
You are a pediatric pharmacist explaining a child's medications to their parent or caregiver. The parent has no medical background.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Use "your child" throughout.
- Liquid doses must include mL and syringe markings.
- Include tips for giving medicine to children who resist.
- Include what to do if the child spits it out or vomits within 30 minutes.

### Controlled substance medication guide

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Medication Instructions: Controlled Substance">
You are a physician creating a medication guide for a patient who has been prescribed a controlled substance. The patient has no medical background. Be direct and clear about safety without being judgmental.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- Include maximum daily amount.
- Include safe storage and disposal instructions.
- Include overdose warning signs.
- Be honest about dependency risk without stigmatizing.
- Bold all critical safety information.

Metoprolol 25 mg (Lopressor)
─────────────────────────────
  What it does: Slows down your heart rate and
  lowers your blood pressure. This reduces the
  work your heart has to do.

  How to take it:
  1. Take 1 tablet twice a day: morning and evening
  2. Take with food
  3. Take at the same times each day

  Common side effects:
  - Tiredness: You may feel more tired than usual,
    especially in the first week. This usually
    gets better.
  - Dizziness: Stand up slowly. Sit down if you
    feel unsteady.
  - Cold hands and feet: This is normal. Wear warm
    socks and gloves.

  Dangerous signs (call your doctor right away):
  - Your heart rate drops below 50 beats per minute
  - You faint or nearly faint
  - You have trouble breathing when lying down

  Things to avoid:
  - Do not stop taking this medicine suddenly.
    Stopping without your doctor's guidance can
    cause a dangerous spike in heart rate and
    blood pressure.

  If you miss a dose: Take it as soon as you
  remember. If it is less than 4 hours until your
  next dose, skip the missed one. Never take two
  doses at once.

──────────────────────────────────────────

Clopidogrel 75 mg (Plavix)
───────────────────────────
  What it does: This is a blood thinner. It stops
  blood cells from clumping together. This keeps
  your new stent open and prevents blood clots.

  How to take it:
  1. Take 1 tablet every morning
  2. You can take it with or without food
  3. Take it at the same time each day

  Common side effects:
  - Bruising more easily: Small bruises are normal.
  - Minor bleeding: Small cuts may take longer to
    stop bleeding. Apply pressure for a few extra
    minutes.

  Dangerous signs (call your doctor right away):
  - Unusual or heavy bruising
  - Blood in your urine (pink or red) or stool
    (black or tarry)
  - A nosebleed that will not stop after 10 minutes
    of pressure
  - Vomiting blood or material that looks like
    coffee grounds

  Things to avoid:
  - Do not take ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) or aspirin
    beyond what your doctor prescribed. These increase
    bleeding risk.
  - Tell your dentist and any new doctor that you
    take a blood thinner.

  If you miss a dose: Take it as soon as you
  remember that day. If it is the next day, skip
  the missed dose and take your regular dose.
  Do not double up.

──────────────────────────────────────────

Drug and Food Interactions
──────────────────────────
- Avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice while
  taking atorvastatin. It increases the amount of
  medicine in your blood.
- Avoid ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) and naproxen
  (Aleve). These can increase bleeding risk with
  your blood thinners and raise blood pressure.
  Use Tylenol (acetaminophen) for pain instead.
- Limit alcohol to 1 drink or less per day.

───────────────────

  your doctor first. Some of these medications are
  dangerous to stop suddenly.

- Store at room temperature unless told otherwise.
- Bring this list to every doctor visit.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Verify every name, dose, and frequency against the actual med rec.** AI can generate plausible but wrong dosing. This is the highest-risk section in any patient education output.
- **Cross-reference interactions against a drug reference.** AI may miss interactions with the patient's other medications or supplements if those are not listed in the input. Lexicomp, Micromedex, or UpToDate are the standard references.
- **Trim the side effect list.** AI tends to list every technically possible side effect, including very rare ones. Keep the 2-3 most common and the 1-2 most dangerous. A list of 15 side effects overwhelms more than it informs.
- **For polypharmacy, create a single daily schedule.** Patients are more likely to follow one consolidated schedule than cross-reference 5 separate instruction sheets. Use the elderly/polypharmacy variation or the daily schedule customization.
- **High-risk medications need institutional backup.** For anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, and chemotherapy, the AI output is a starting draft. Pair it with your institution's standardized medication education handouts.
- **The "Things to Avoid" section is actionable and error-prone.** Drug-food and drug-drug interactions are among the most useful items for patients and also among the most error-prone for AI. Double-check this section.
- **Controlled substances have their own variation.** Use it. The safety rules, storage instructions, and overdose warning signs are different enough to warrant a dedicated prompt.
