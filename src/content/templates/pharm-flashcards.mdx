## When to use this

Use this template to generate a structured pharmacology review for any drug class. It produces drug-by-drug cards covering mechanism, indications, side effects, contraindications, and the one fact boards always test, followed by a class comparison table and common board questions.

Focus on one drug class at a time. Requesting "all cardiology drugs" produces shallow cards. Requesting "loop diuretics" produces cards with the specificity you actually need to answer a board question.

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pharmacology Board Review">
You are a pharmacology professor creating a structured board review of a drug class for medical students.

### Purpose
Generate a comprehensive, board-focused pharmacology review covering every drug in a class. Each drug gets a structured card. The class gets a comparison table and common board questions with answers.

### Rules
- One drug per card. Do not combine drugs.
- Mechanism of action must be one sentence, precise enough to distinguish this drug from others in the class.
- "Board Pearl" must be the single most commonly tested fact about this drug, not a generic statement.
- Side effects should list only those that boards test, not a comprehensive adverse event profile.
- Drug interactions should name the most clinically significant one, with the mechanism.
- The comparison table must highlight the distinguishing features between drugs in the class, not repeat shared properties.
- Common board questions must include the answer and reasoning, not just the question.

### Vocabulary
- "Mechanism" (not "MOA")
- "Indications" (not "uses")
- Drug names should be generic (not brand)
- Use standard board phrasing: "most likely side effect," "best initial therapy," "contraindicated in"

### Output format

PHARMACOLOGY CARDS: [DRUG CLASS]
═════════════════════════════════

DRUG: [Generic Name]
────────────────────
Class: [pharmacologic class]
Mechanism: [MOA in one precise sentence]
Indications: [primary clinical uses, bulleted]
Key Side Effects: [the side effects boards test]
Contraindications: [absolute contraindications]
Board Pearl: [the one fact they always ask]
Monitoring: [what to check and how often]
Drug Interaction: [most important interaction and its mechanism]

[Repeat for each drug in the class]

CLASS COMPARISON TABLE
──────────────────────
| Drug | Unique Feature | Board Buzzword |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| [Drug 1] | [What distinguishes it] | [The tested association] |
| [Drug 2] | [What distinguishes it] | [The tested association] |

COMMON BOARD QUESTIONS
──────────────────────
Q: [Common board question about this class]
A: [Answer with one-sentence reasoning]

Q: [Second common question]
A: [Answer with reasoning]

Q: [Third common question]
A: [Answer with reasoning]

---
Drug class: [DRUG CLASS OR SPECIFIC DRUGS, e.g., "ACE inhibitors," "anti-epileptics," "drugs used in heart failure"]
Exam level: [USMLE Step 1 / Step 2 CK / Shelf / Specialty Board]
Focus (optional): [Specific drugs to include/exclude, mechanisms vs. clinical, known weak areas]
</PromptPlayground>

## Variations

### By organ system (cardiac drugs, neuro drugs, etc.)

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Organ System Pharmacology Review">
You are a pharmacology professor creating an organ-system-based pharmacology review for boards.

### Purpose
Review all major drug classes acting on a single organ system, organized by mechanism and clinical use. Each drug gets a card anchored in a clinical scenario. The output should function as a comprehensive pre-exam review of one organ system's pharmacology.

### Rules
- Organize drugs by their pharmacologic category within the system (e.g., for cardiovascular: anti-hypertensives, anti-arrhythmics, anti-anginals, heart failure drugs, lipid-lowering agents).
- Each drug card must include a one-sentence clinical scenario where this drug is the correct answer.
- Include 5 "which drug?" clinical scenario cards at the end.
- Focus on board-tested distinctions, not comprehensive pharmacology.

Board Pearl: [one high-yield fact]

### Mechanism-focused (enzyme inhibitors, receptor agonists, etc.)

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Mechanism-Focused Pharmacology Review">
You are a pharmacology professor creating a review organized by mechanism of action for Step 1 preparation.

### Purpose
Group drugs by their mechanism of action rather than by clinical use. This format is ideal for Step 1, which tests whether you understand how drugs work, not just when to prescribe them.

### Rules
- Organize by mechanism category: enzyme inhibitors, receptor agonists, receptor antagonists, ion channel blockers, transport inhibitors, etc.
- For each drug, trace the mechanism to the clinical effect: [drug blocks X] → [Y pathway is inhibited] → [clinical result Z].
- Include "mechanism twins" where two drugs from different classes share a mechanism step.
- End with a mechanism matching exercise.

### Output format

MECHANISM-FOCUSED REVIEW: [TOPIC]
═════════════════════════════════

MECHANISM: [Category, e.g., "Enzyme Inhibitors"]
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

DRUG: [Name]
Target: [specific enzyme, receptor, or channel]
Mechanism chain: [Drug] → blocks [target] → [downstream effect] → [clinical result]
Clinical use: [indication]
What happens in overdose/excess: [toxic effect, which is the mechanism taken too far]

[Repeat for each drug]

MECHANISM TWINS
───────────────
[Drug A] and [Drug B] both affect [shared mechanism step], but differ because [key distinction].

MATCHING EXERCISE
─────────────────
Match the mechanism to the drug:
1. [Mechanism description] → ?
2. [Mechanism description] → ?
3. [Mechanism description] → ?
Answers: [Listed below]

---
Topic: [MECHANISM CATEGORY OR DRUG GROUP, e.g., "enzyme inhibitors," "receptor agonists," "ion channel blockers"]
Exam level: [USMLE Step 1]
</PromptPlayground>

### Toxicology and overdose

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Toxicology and Overdose Review">
You are a pharmacology and toxicology professor creating a board review of drug overdoses and their management.

### Purpose
Review the classic toxidromes and specific drug overdoses tested on boards. For each poison or drug, cover the mechanism of toxicity, the clinical presentation boards describe, and the specific antidote.

### Rules
- Organize by toxidrome or drug category.
- Each entry must include the classic board presentation (the vignette stem you will see).
- Antidotes must be specific: drug name and mechanism of how the antidote works.
- Include the "if you see X, think Y" buzzword associations that boards love.
- End with a rapid-fire antidote matching table.

### Output format

TOXICOLOGY REVIEW: [CATEGORY]
═════════════════════════════

TOXIDROME / DRUG: [Name]
─────────────────────────

Antidote: [specific drug and mechanism]
Board Pearl: [the one fact they always test]
Lethal pitfall: [the complication that kills the patient if missed]

[Repeat for each entry]

ANTIDOTE MATCHING TABLE
───────────────────────
| Toxin/Drug | Antidote | Mechanism of Antidote |

## Example output

PHARMACOLOGY CARDS: ACE INHIBITORS
═══════════════════════════════════

DRUG: Lisinopril
────────────────
Class: ACE inhibitor
Mechanism: Blocks angiotensin-converting enzyme, preventing conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II; reduces aldosterone secretion and increases bradykinin levels.
Indications:
- Hypertension
- Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (mortality benefit)
- Diabetic nephropathy (slows progression of proteinuria)
- Post-MI (reduces ventricular remodeling)
Key Side Effects: Dry cough (bradykinin accumulation), hyperkalemia, angioedema (rare, more common in Black patients)
Contraindications: Pregnancy (teratogenic: renal agenesis, oligohydramnios), bilateral renal artery stenosis, history of ACE inhibitor-induced angioedema
Board Pearl: The cough is caused by bradykinin accumulation, not angiotensin. Switching to an ARB resolves the cough because ARBs do not affect the bradykinin pathway.
Monitoring: Serum creatinine and potassium within 1-2 weeks of initiation; a creatinine rise up to 30% is acceptable
Drug Interaction: Potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone, amiloride) increase hyperkalemia risk through dual RAAS blockade; NSAIDs reduce ACE inhibitor efficacy and compound renal injury

DRUG: Enalapril
────────────────
Class: ACE inhibitor
Mechanism: Prodrug (enalaprilat is the active form); same ACE inhibition as lisinopril but requires hepatic activation.
Indications: Same as lisinopril; also used in pediatric heart failure (one of the few ACE inhibitors with pediatric dosing data)
Key Side Effects: Same class effects (cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema); first-dose hypotension, especially in volume-depleted patients
Contraindications: Same as lisinopril; hold if patient is volume-depleted until resuscitated
Board Pearl: Enalapril is a prodrug. Boards test this because prodrugs require hepatic metabolism, so patients with severe liver disease may not activate it adequately. Lisinopril does not require activation.
Monitoring: Same as lisinopril
Drug Interaction: Lithium levels increase with ACE inhibitors (reduced renal lithium clearance); monitor lithium closely

CLASS COMPARISON TABLE
──────────────────────
| Drug | Unique Feature | Board Buzzword |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| Lisinopril | Active drug (no hepatic activation needed) | Cough → switch to ARB |
| Enalapril | Prodrug (requires hepatic activation to enalaprilat) | Prodrug question on Step 1 |
| Captopril | Shortest-acting; contains sulfhydryl group | Taste disturbance, rash (sulfhydryl) |
| Ramipril | HOPE trial: proven CV mortality reduction in high-risk patients | "Which ACE inhibitor has mortality data in high-risk patients?" |
| Benazepril | Dual elimination (hepatic and renal) | Dose adjustment less critical in mild renal impairment |

COMMON BOARD QUESTIONS
──────────────────────
Q: A patient on lisinopril develops a persistent dry cough. What is the mechanism, and what is the best alternative?
A: Bradykinin accumulates because ACE normally degrades it. Switch to an ARB (e.g., losartan), which blocks the angiotensin II receptor without affecting bradykinin metabolism.

Q: A 28-year-old woman with hypertension controlled on lisinopril discovers she is pregnant. What do you do?
A: Discontinue lisinopril immediately. ACE inhibitors are teratogenic in all trimesters (renal agenesis, oligohydramnios, skull ossification defects). Switch to labetalol or nifedipine for blood pressure management in pregnancy.

Q: A patient with bilateral renal artery stenosis is started on an ACE inhibitor. What happens to the GFR and why?
A: GFR drops precipitously. The kidneys depend on angiotensin II to constrict the efferent arteriole and maintain filtration pressure. ACE inhibition removes this compensatory mechanism, causing acute renal failure.

## Tips & pitfalls

- Verify every drug fact. This is the single most important warning for pharmacology templates. AI can mix up side effect profiles between drugs in the same class, cite wrong CYP enzymes, or list outdated indications. Cross-check against Katzung, First Aid, or Lexicomp before committing anything to a study deck.
- One class at a time produces the best results. "Beta-blockers" or "fluoroquinolones" gives the AI enough focus to generate specific, testable cards. "All Step 1 drugs" does not.
- The comparison table is often the highest-yield section. Boards test "which drug in the class" distinctions more than individual drug facts. If you only have time for one section, prioritize comparisons.
- Pair with the Question Generator template after reviewing a drug class: learn the drugs here, then test yourself with clinical vignette questions.
- AI training data may not reflect the most current prescribing guidelines, especially in oncology, infectious disease, and cardiology. When a card references a guideline, check the publication year.
- For Anki import, add to the prompt: "Format with TAB separating front and back for TSV import." The default output format requires manual reformatting.
- Watch for "mechanism twins" across classes. Boards love asking about drugs from different classes that share a mechanism step (e.g., ACE inhibitors and neprilysin inhibitors both increase bradykinin).
