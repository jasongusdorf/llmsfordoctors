## When to use this

Use this template when you have a working differential diagnosis and need to build a prioritized, rationale-driven diagnostic workup plan organized by urgency and yield.

Converts a clinical impression and differential diagnosis into a prioritized, rationale-driven diagnostic workup plan. Provide your leading diagnoses and the clinical context, and get a structured list of tests organized by urgency and diagnostic yield.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="evidence">
  Diagnostic test recommendations reflect general clinical principles. Institutional protocols, local prevalence, patient values, and cost constraints must all factor into actual test-ordering decisions. AI output does not replace clinical judgment or specialty consultation.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="ChatGPT (GPT-4o)" title="Diagnostic Workup Planner">
You are an experienced attending physician helping to plan a diagnostic workup for an undifferentiated clinical presentation.

Given the clinical scenario and differential diagnoses below, generate a structured workup plan organized by priority tier:

**Tier 1: Immediate (order now, results needed within hours)**
List tests that must be obtained urgently to rule out dangerous diagnoses or guide immediate management. For each test:
- Test name
- What diagnosis or question it addresses
- Threshold that would change management

**Tier 2: Early (order now, results within 24-48 hours)**
List tests that are diagnostically important but not immediately time-critical. Include the same format as Tier 1.

**Tier 3: Deferred (pending Tier 1/2 results or outpatient)**
List tests that can wait for initial results before ordering, or that are appropriate in an outpatient or follow-up setting.

**Procedures / Consultations**
List any procedures (e.g., lumbar puncture, paracentesis, biopsy) or specialty consultations indicated, with the specific question to be answered.

**Decision Branch Points**
Briefly describe how key Tier 1/2 results would shift the workup. Use "If [result], then [next step]" format for the 2–3 most pivotal tests.

**What to Avoid**
List 2–3 tests that might seem indicated but are low-yield or potentially harmful in this specific scenario. Briefly explain why.

Rules:
- Prioritize tests by diagnostic yield and clinical urgency, not by comprehensiveness.
- Do not fabricate test sensitivities, specificities, or cutoff values. State them only if well-established.
- Do not infer clinical information not explicitly provided below.

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Setting | Add: "This is an outpatient visit; limit workup to tests available in clinic or sendout" |
| Resource constraints | Add: "Prioritize the 3 highest-yield tests given limited resources" |
| Prior workup | Add: "These tests were already obtained [LIST]. Focus the plan on what remains." |
| Subspecialty | Add: "This is a [nephrology / hematology / rheumatology] workup; use specialty-appropriate sequencing" |
| Patient factors | Add: "This patient has [CKD / contrast allergy / coagulopathy]; adjust accordingly" |

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Resource-Limited Workup Planner">
You are an experienced physician planning a diagnostic workup in a resource-limited setting (rural clinic, community hospital without advanced imaging, or international setting with constrained lab availability).

Given the clinical scenario and differential below, generate a workup plan that prioritizes widely available, low-cost, high-yield tests. Assume you may NOT have access to: MRI, CT angiography, interventional radiology, specialized send-out labs, or subspecialty consultation on the same day.

**Available Now (bedside and basic lab)**
For each test:
- Test name (e.g., point-of-care ultrasound, CBC, BMP, urinalysis, ECG, plain radiograph)
- What diagnosis it addresses
- Key finding that would change management

**Available with Delay (send-out labs or transfer for imaging)**
For each:
- Test name
- Turnaround time expectation
- What you do while waiting for the result

**Transfer Criteria**
Describe the specific clinical findings or test results that should trigger transfer to a higher level of care. Be explicit about what constitutes "too sick to manage here."

**Clinical Reasoning Without Advanced Testing**
In 3–4 sentences, describe how you would synthesize the available bedside and basic lab data to make a working management decision without advanced imaging or specialty labs.

Rules:
- Prioritize tests by availability, cost, and yield in this order.
- Do not recommend tests that require resources you may not have.
- State explicitly when clinical observation over time is more valuable than additional testing.

- What you're looking for

### Urgent Inpatient Workup

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Urgent Inpatient Workup Planner">
You are a hospital medicine attending planning the diagnostic workup for a newly admitted patient with an urgent but not immediately emergent presentation.

Given the clinical scenario and differential below, generate a workup plan for the first 24 hours of admission. The patient is hemodynamically stable but requires inpatient-level evaluation.

**Admission Orders (first 2 hours)**
For each test:
- Test name and timing (stat vs. routine)
- What diagnosis it addresses
- Action threshold (the result that changes management tonight)

**Overnight Monitoring Plan**
- Specific vitals frequency and parameters to watch
- Nursing call criteria (what findings should prompt you to be called)
- Repeat labs if indicated (which labs, when, and why)

**Morning Workup Plan (hours 6–24)**
For each test:
- Test name
- What it addresses
- How the overnight data changes whether you order it

**Consultations to Place**
For each:
- Service to consult
- Specific question
- Urgency (tonight vs. morning)

**24-Hour Decision Point**
In 2–3 sentences, describe what clinical picture at the 24-hour mark would make you (a) escalate care, (b) continue the current plan, or (c) transition toward discharge.

Rules:
- Organize by time: what happens now, overnight, and in the morning.
- Be explicit about overnight monitoring parameters.
- Do not infer clinical information not provided.

## Example output

> **Prompt used:** Diagnostic Workup Planner for a 72-year-old female with new-onset confusion.
>
> **Clinical scenario provided:** 72F with HTN, type 2 DM, CKD stage 3. Brought in by family for 2 days of progressive confusion and decreased oral intake. No fever at home. Vitals: HR 98, BP 102/64, RR 18, O2 96% RA, Temp 37.8C. Exam: oriented to person only, dry mucous membranes, no focal neuro deficits, lungs clear, abdomen soft. No labs or imaging yet. Setting: Emergency department. DDx: UTI/urosepsis, hypovolemia/AKI on CKD, metabolic encephalopathy, stroke.

**Tier 1: Immediate (order now, results needed within hours)**
- **CBC with differential** — Addresses infection/sepsis. WBC >12 or <4 with bandemia supports infectious etiology.
- **BMP (Chem-8)** — Addresses AKI, electrolyte derangement, hyperglycemia. Creatinine above her baseline (likely >1.8-2.0) confirms AKI. Sodium <125 or >155 could explain encephalopathy.
- **Urinalysis with culture** — Addresses UTI/urosepsis. Positive nitrites, leukocyte esterase, or bacteria on micro supports urinary source.
- **Lactate** — Addresses sepsis severity. Lactate >2 mmol/L in the setting of hypotension raises concern for septic shock.
- **Blood cultures x2** — Addresses bacteremia. Must be drawn before antibiotics if sepsis is suspected.
- **CT head without contrast** — Addresses stroke or intracranial pathology. Any acute hemorrhage, mass effect, or large territory infarct changes management immediately.
- **Point-of-care glucose** — Addresses hypoglycemia or DKA. Glucose <60 or >400 requires immediate intervention.

**Tier 2: Early (order now, results within 24-48 hours)**
- **TSH** — Addresses myxedema or thyroid storm as a cause of encephalopathy, particularly in older women.
- **Ammonia level** — Addresses hepatic encephalopathy if liver disease is a possibility.
- **Chest X-ray** — Addresses pneumonia as an occult infectious source causing confusion in the elderly.
- **LFTs** — Baseline hepatic function, especially relevant with altered mental status.

**Tier 3: Deferred (pending Tier 1/2 results)**
- **MRI brain** — If CT head is negative and confusion does not resolve with metabolic correction, MRI is warranted to evaluate for acute ischemic stroke (CT misses early and small infarcts).
- **Lumbar puncture** — If no clear metabolic or infectious source is identified and fever persists, LP should be performed to evaluate for meningitis/encephalitis.
- **EEG** — If altered mental status persists without clear explanation, non-convulsive status epilepticus should be considered.

**Procedures / Consultations**
- **Neurology consult** (if CT head abnormal or AMS persists >24h without clear metabolic cause): "72F with new-onset progressive confusion, no focal deficits, CT pending — concern for stroke vs. metabolic encephalopathy."

**Decision Branch Points**
- If **creatinine is significantly above baseline** and BUN/Cr ratio is elevated → aggressive IV hydration, hold nephrotoxins, recheck in 12 hours. If no improvement, consider renal ultrasound for obstruction.
- If **UA is positive and lactate is elevated** → start empiric antibiotics for urosepsis (adjust for CKD), repeat lactate in 4–6 hours to assess response.
- If **CT head shows acute infarct** → activate stroke pathway, neurology consult, hold anticoagulation pending further characterization.

**What to Avoid**
- **MRI brain on arrival** — Low yield as the first test when metabolic causes are more likely and faster to diagnose. CT head is sufficient to exclude hemorrhage and mass effect urgently.
- **Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics before cultures** — Unless she meets sepsis criteria with hemodynamic instability, draw cultures and UA first. Her temp is only 37.8C and she is not in shock.
- **CT angiography head/neck** — Not indicated without focal neurological deficits or a positive CT head finding.

## Tips & pitfalls

- Works best when paired with the **DDx Generator** template: first generate your differential, then paste it here to build the workup plan.
- The **Decision Branch Points** section is the highest-value output: it makes your clinical reasoning explicit and helps anticipate what you'll do before results return.
- The **What to Avoid** section helps with both test stewardship and patient safety (e.g., avoiding contrast in AKI, avoiding LP before CT in certain cases).
- For complex multi-system presentations, run this template once per major diagnostic question rather than trying to cover everything in a single prompt.
- **AI may recommend tests that are not available at your institution or that have long turnaround times.** Always verify test availability and expected turnaround at your facility.
- **Test sensitivity and specificity values cited by AI should be verified.** Models sometimes confabulate performance characteristics of diagnostic tests. Cross-reference with UpToDate or primary literature.
- **Cost and patient burden matter.** AI tends to recommend comprehensive workups. Apply clinical judgment about which tests are truly necessary and which can wait for initial results.
