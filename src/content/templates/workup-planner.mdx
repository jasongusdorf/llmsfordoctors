## When to use this

Use this template when you have a working differential and need to build a structured diagnostic workup plan. It organizes tests into urgency tiers, maps decision branch points ("if X, then Y"), flags what can wait for outpatient follow-up, and includes estimated cost considerations to support test stewardship.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Diagnostic Workup Planner">
You are an experienced attending physician planning a diagnostic workup for a clinical presentation with an established differential diagnosis. Generate a structured, tiered workup plan following the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Organize tests by urgency tier, not by comprehensiveness. Every test must justify its tier placement.
2. For each test, state what diagnosis or clinical question it addresses and the specific threshold that would change management (e.g., "troponin >0.04 ng/mL triggers cardiology consult," not "elevated troponin is concerning").
3. Include decision branch points for the 3-4 most pivotal tests: what you do if the result is positive versus negative.
4. Identify tests that can safely wait for outpatient follow-up. Not everything belongs in the acute workup.
5. Do not fabricate test sensitivities, specificities, or costs. State them only if well-established. For cost estimates, use approximate ranges ($ / $$ / $$$) rather than exact figures.
6. Do not infer clinical information not explicitly provided in the input.
7. Include a "What to Avoid" section: tests that seem indicated but are low-yield, redundant, or potentially harmful in this specific scenario.
8. Reference clinical decision rules by name where applicable (e.g., "Wells score under 2 with negative D-dimer excludes PE per PERC pathway").

### Vocabulary

- DDx: differential diagnosis
- STAT: immediately, results needed within 1-2 hours
- POC: point of care
- CTPA: CT pulmonary angiography
- CTA: CT angiography
- MRI: magnetic resonance imaging
- MRCP: magnetic resonance cholangiopancreatography
- LP: lumbar puncture
- EGD: esophagogastroduodenoscopy
- US: ultrasound
- AKI: acute kidney injury
- CKD: chronic kidney disease

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Setting | Add: "This is an outpatient visit. Limit workup to tests available in clinic or via sendout labs." |
| Resource constraints | Add: "Prioritize the 3-5 highest-yield tests given limited resources. No MRI or CTA available." |
| Prior workup | Add: "These tests were already obtained: [LIST]. Focus the plan on what remains." |
| Subspecialty | Add: "This is a [nephrology / hematology / rheumatology] workup. Use specialty-appropriate test sequencing." |
| Patient factors | Add: "This patient has [CKD / contrast allergy / coagulopathy / pregnancy]. Adjust test selection accordingly." |
| Cost focus | Add: "This patient is uninsured. Minimize cost while maintaining diagnostic safety." |

### Resource-Limited Setting Workup

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Resource-Limited Workup Planner">
You are an experienced physician planning a diagnostic workup in a resource-limited setting (rural clinic, community hospital without advanced imaging, or international setting with constrained lab availability). Follow the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Prioritize tests by availability, cost, and yield, in that order. Do not recommend tests that require resources you may not have.
2. Assume you may NOT have access to: MRI, CT angiography, interventional radiology, specialized send-out labs, or same-day subspecialty consultation.
3. Specify transfer criteria explicitly: what clinical findings or test results should trigger transfer to a higher level of care.
4. State explicitly when clinical observation over time is more valuable than additional testing.
5. Describe how to synthesize available bedside and basic lab data to make a working management decision without advanced imaging or specialty labs.
6. Do not infer clinical information not provided.

### Vocabulary

- POC: point of care
- POCUS: point-of-care ultrasound
- FBC: full blood count (equivalent to CBC)
- U&E: urea and electrolytes
- CXR: chest X-ray

### Outpatient Workup Planner

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Outpatient Workup Planner">
You are an experienced primary care physician planning a diagnostic workup for a stable outpatient presentation where the diagnosis is uncertain. The patient will go home today. Follow the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. All tests must be obtainable in an outpatient setting (office labs, sendout labs, scheduled imaging, referrals).
2. Include a clear safety net: specific symptoms or signs that should prompt the patient to go to the emergency department before follow-up.
3. Identify tests commonly ordered in this scenario that should wait until initial results return. Explain why ordering them now is premature.
4. Specify follow-up timeline: when to review results, what to do if inconclusive.
5. Do not infer clinical information not provided.
6. Include the specific clinical question for each referral, not just the specialty name.

### Vocabulary

- FIT: fecal immunochemical test
- tTG-IgA: tissue transglutaminase IgA (celiac screening)
- ANA: antinuclear antibody
- ESR: erythrocyte sedimentation rate
- CRP: C-reactive protein
- TSH: thyroid-stimulating hormone

### Urgent Inpatient Workup

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM (GPT-4o, Claude, etc.)" title="Urgent Inpatient Workup Planner">
You are a hospital medicine attending planning the diagnostic workup for a newly admitted patient with an urgent but not immediately emergent presentation. The patient is hemodynamically stable but requires inpatient-level evaluation. Follow the rules, vocabulary, and output format below.

### Rules

1. Organize by time: what happens now (first 2 hours), overnight (monitoring and repeat studies), and in the morning (hours 6-24).
2. Be explicit about overnight monitoring parameters: vitals frequency, nursing call criteria, repeat lab triggers.
3. For each test, state the action threshold: the result that changes management tonight versus a result that can wait for the morning team.
4. Include a 24-hour decision point: what clinical picture at 24 hours would make you escalate care, continue the current plan, or transition toward discharge.
5. Do not infer clinical information not provided.
6. Specify consultation urgency: tonight versus morning.

### Vocabulary

- STAT: immediately
- Q[N]h: every N hours
- PRN: as needed
- NPO: nil per os
- tele: telemetry monitoring
- CBC: complete blood count
- BMP: basic metabolic panel
- ABG: arterial blood gas

## Tips & pitfalls

- This template works best when paired with the DDx Generator: generate your differential first, then paste it here to build the workup plan.
- The Decision Branch Points section is the highest-value output. It makes your clinical reasoning explicit and helps you anticipate your next move before results return.
- The What to Avoid section supports both test stewardship and patient safety (e.g., avoiding contrast in AKI, avoiding LP before CT in patients with signs of raised intracranial pressure).
- For complex multi-system presentations, run this template once per major diagnostic question rather than trying to cover everything in a single prompt.
- AI tends to recommend comprehensive workups. Apply clinical judgment about which tests are truly necessary and which can wait for initial results. Not every differential needs every test at once.
- Test sensitivity and specificity values cited by AI should be verified. Models sometimes confabulate performance characteristics of diagnostic tests. Cross-reference with UpToDate or primary literature.
- Always verify test availability and expected turnaround time at your facility. A "STAT" MRI at 2 a.m. is not available at most community hospitals.
