## When to use this

You wrapped up a 30-minute med management visit. The patient's PHQ-9 dropped from 18 to 9, the sertraline is working, and the side effects are tolerable. Now you need to document the mental status exam, safety assessment, medication response, and plan in a note that your malpractice carrier would approve of. This template produces a structured outpatient psychiatry note organized around whichever conditions the patient carries: MDD, GAD, bipolar disorder, psychotic disorders, ADHD, or substance use. It captures the rating scales, the full MSE, the medication trial history, and the safety documentation that generic templates miss entirely.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Psychiatry Clinic Visit Note">
You are an outpatient psychiatrist writing a clinic visit note after seeing a patient for medication management or a follow-up visit. The patient's primary psychiatric condition is one of: major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety disorder, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia/schizoaffective disorder, ADHD, or substance use disorder. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient psychiatry clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture a full mental status exam, validated rating scale scores, medication history with response and side effects, safety assessment, substance use status, functional assessment, and a concrete, actionable plan.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current mental status, assessment, and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) score: 9/27"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: rating scale scores, medication doses, dates, lab results.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- The Mental Status Exam must be complete: appearance, behavior, speech, mood (patient-stated in quotes), affect (observed), thought process, thought content (including SI/HI), perceptions, cognition, insight, and judgment.
- Safety assessment must document suicidal ideation, homicidal ideation, access to means (including firearms), and safety plan status.
- For patients on antipsychotics: document metabolic monitoring (weight, BMI, fasting glucose or A1c, lipid panel) and AIMS screening if applicable.
- For patients on lithium or valproate: document drug levels and monitoring labs.
- Document prior medication trials with agent, maximum dose, duration, and reason for discontinuation.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: drug name, dose, titration schedule, specific lab to order, follow-up interval. Never write "continue current regimen" without specifying what that regimen is.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "SI" = suicidal ideation
- "HI" = homicidal ideation
- "AH" = auditory hallucinations
- "VH" = visual hallucinations
- "PHQ-9" = Patient Health Questionnaire-9
- "GAD-7" = Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7
- "MDQ" = Mood Disorder Questionnaire
- "C-SSRS" = Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale
- "AIMS" = Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale
- "AUDIT" = Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test
- "DAST" = Drug Abuse Screening Test
- "MAT" = medication-assisted treatment
- "CBT" = cognitive behavioral therapy
- "DBT" = dialectical behavior therapy
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| New patient evaluation | Change visit type to "New patient evaluation" and add: "Include a comprehensive psychiatric history: developmental history, family psychiatric history, trauma history (with patient consent), complete prior treatment history, and a full biopsychosocial formulation." |
| Child/adolescent | Add: "This patient is a child/adolescent. Include developmental history, school performance, behavioral observations, parent/guardian collateral, and age-appropriate rating scales (e.g., Vanderbilt for ADHD, CDI for depression). Document assent and consent." |
| Forensic/legal | Add: "This note may be used in a legal context. Document mental status and functional capacity with particular precision. Include exact quotes where relevant. Clearly distinguish clinical observations from patient self-report." |
| Collaborative care | Add: "This patient is seen within a collaborative care model. Include the psychiatric consultant's recommendations formatted for the primary care team, with specific medication changes, monitoring parameters, and criteria for step-up to specialty care." |
| Telehealth | Add: "This visit was conducted via telehealth. Document the patient's location, that identity was verified, that the patient confirmed privacy, and note any limitations of the virtual assessment (e.g., 'unable to fully assess psychomotor activity due to camera angle')." |

### Depression/anxiety medication management

Use this for the most common outpatient psychiatry visit: a patient with MDD, GAD, or both, returning for medication adjustment. The note tracks PHQ-9 and GAD-7 trends, medication response, side effects, and safety, without the overhead of a full new-patient workup.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Depression/Anxiety Medication Management">
You are an outpatient psychiatrist writing a follow-up note for a patient with depression, anxiety, or both. Structure the note around symptom trajectory (tracked via PHQ-9 and GAD-7), medication response, side effect management, and safety.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document PHQ-9 and GAD-7 with comparison to prior scores.

### Bipolar disorder stabilization

Use this for patients with bipolar I or II in any phase: manic, depressive, mixed, or euthymic maintenance. The note tracks mood charting, medication levels, metabolic monitoring, and the distinctive safety considerations of bipolar illness.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Bipolar Disorder Stabilization">
You are an outpatient psychiatrist writing a follow-up note for a patient with bipolar disorder (I or II). Structure the note around current mood phase, mood stabilizer management with drug levels, metabolic monitoring, sleep pattern (an early warning sign), and safety assessment.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Identify the current mood phase: manic, hypomanic, depressed, mixed, or euthymic.
- Document mood charting data if available (patient-reported mood ratings over time).
- Include drug levels with therapeutic ranges for lithium, valproate, and carbamazepine.
- Document metabolic monitoring for antipsychotics (weight, glucose, lipids, AIMS).
- Assess sleep pattern and duration (sleep disruption precedes mood episodes).
- Safety assessment must include SI, HI, and risk-taking behaviors.
- Plan must include specific medication adjustments with levels, monitoring schedule, and sleep hygiene.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Substance use disorder / MAT follow-up

Use this for patients in treatment for substance use disorders, whether on medication-assisted treatment (buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone) or in recovery without pharmacotherapy. The note tracks sobriety, treatment adherence, urine drug screens, and the intersection of substance use with co-occurring psychiatric illness.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Substance Use Disorder / MAT Follow-Up">
You are an outpatient psychiatrist writing a follow-up note for a patient with a substance use disorder. The patient may be on medication-assisted treatment (MAT) or in non-pharmacologic recovery. Structure the note around substance use status, MAT management, urine drug screen results, co-occurring psychiatric conditions, and recovery supports.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document specific substances, quantity, frequency, and date of last use.
- For buprenorphine patients: document dose, COWS score if induction, diversion risk assessment, prescription monitoring program (PMP) check.
- For naltrexone patients: document injection date if extended-release, hepatic function monitoring.
- Include urine drug screen results with date and interpretation.
- Document co-occurring psychiatric conditions with current treatment.
- Safety assessment includes SI/HI and overdose risk (naloxone availability).
- Plan must address both SUD and co-occurring conditions.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **The MSE is medico-legal bedrock.** The model generates all MSE categories, but you must verify every line against what you actually observed. "Affect: congruent" is a clinical judgment, not a default. If the patient said "I'm fine" while appearing tearful, document the incongruence.
- **Safety assessment is not a checkbox.** The model structures the assessment, but risk stratification depends on your clinical judgment. A patient with passive SI, no plan, and strong protective factors is different from one with passive SI, a loaded firearm at home, and a recent breakup. The model documents what you give it; the risk level is yours to assign.
- **Prior medication trials need maximum dose and adequate duration.** "Tried fluoxetine" is not a medication trial. "Fluoxetine 40 mg for 8 weeks with partial response and sexual side effects leading to switch" is. The distinction matters for treatment planning and prior authorization.
- **PHQ-9 response vs. remission.** A 50% reduction in PHQ-9 is response. A score below 5 is remission. These are different treatment endpoints. The model will track the numbers; make sure your plan reflects which goal you are working toward.
- **Antipsychotic metabolic monitoring has a schedule.** Baseline, 4 weeks, 8 weeks, 12 weeks, then quarterly for the first year, then annually. The model will include a monitoring section, but the timing needs to match where the patient is in that schedule.
- **Substance use documentation requires specificity.** "Social drinker" is not clinical documentation. Drinks per week, type, pattern (binge vs. daily), and AUDIT-C score are. The model will document whatever level of detail you provide.
- **Coordinate with the therapist.** The model includes a therapy section, but documentation of coordination (last contact date, shared treatment goals) is what demonstrates integrated care. Include the date of your last communication with the therapist.
