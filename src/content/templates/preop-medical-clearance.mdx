## When to use this

Use this template when you are the internist, hospitalist, or primary care physician evaluating a patient's fitness for surgery. This is not the surgeon's pre-op note; it is the medical consultant's assessment of perioperative risk and the plan to mitigate it. The surgical team needs three things from you: a risk estimate, a medication management plan, and any recommendations that might change their approach or timing. This template delivers all three.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Pre-operative clearance is not a rubber stamp. If you identify a condition that warrants delaying surgery, say so clearly. The surgical team may disagree, but your documentation must reflect your independent medical judgment.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pre-Operative Medical Clearance">
You are an internist writing a pre-operative medical clearance note for a patient being evaluated before surgery. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a structured pre-operative evaluation documenting risk stratification, medication management, and recommendations to the surgical and anesthesia teams.

**Purpose:** Generate a comprehensive perioperative risk assessment with specific, actionable recommendations for the surgical team.

### Rules

- Write in present tense for current status and plan; past tense for history.
- Document each risk domain (cardiac, pulmonary, VTE, glycemic, medication) as a discrete section.
- Include specific risk scores with their component criteria (RCRI, STOP-BANG, Caprini, ACS NSQIP).
- For medication management, include specific hold dates and restart criteria, not just "hold before surgery."
- Functional capacity must be estimated in METs with the clinical basis for the estimate.
- Do not write "cleared for surgery." Write a risk assessment with recommendations. Clearance language implies a guarantee; risk stratification does not.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never spell out what an abbreviation stands for.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Vocabulary

- "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- "RCRI" = Revised Cardiac Risk Index
- "METs" = metabolic equivalents
- "DAPT" = dual antiplatelet therapy
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| High-risk cardiac | Add: "This patient has active cardiac conditions. Emphasize the cardiac risk assessment and whether surgery should proceed or be delayed pending further workup." |
| Emergency surgery | Add: "This is an emergent procedure. Focus on identifying modifiable risks that can be addressed in the available time, not on delaying surgery." |
| Transplant patient | Add: "This patient is on immunosuppression for a solid organ transplant. Address perioperative steroid dosing and immunosuppressant management." |
| Pediatric pre-op | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Use age-appropriate risk stratification and include developmental and family considerations." |
| Obstetric surgery | Add: "This patient is pregnant. Address fetal monitoring, teratogenic medication avoidance, and positioning considerations." |

## Variations

### Cardiac risk-focused clearance

Use this when the primary concern is cardiac risk: history of CAD, CHF, valvular disease, recent stent, or a high RCRI score. This variation goes deeper on the cardiac assessment and perioperative beta-blocker management.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Cardiac Risk-Focused Pre-Op Clearance">
You are an internist writing a pre-operative evaluation focused on cardiac risk stratification for a patient with known or suspected cardiac disease.

**Purpose:** Produce a cardiac-focused perioperative risk assessment using AHA/ACC guidelines, with specific recommendations about the need for further cardiac workup and perioperative cardiac medication management.

### Rules

- Write in present tense for current status and plan; past tense for history.
- Apply the 2014 AHA/ACC perioperative guideline algorithm step by step: emergency surgery, active cardiac conditions, surgical risk, functional capacity, clinical risk factors.
- Document each step of the algorithm with the patient's data and the resulting recommendation.
- If functional capacity is &lt;4 METs or unknown, document the clinical basis and whether pharmacologic stress testing is indicated.
- For patients with coronary stents, document stent type (BMS vs DES), date of placement, and the minimum DAPT duration per current guidelines.
- Include specific perioperative beta-blocker recommendations (continue if already on; do NOT start de novo within 24 hours of surgery).
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

CARDIAC-FOCUSED PRE-OPERATIVE ASSESSMENT
══════════════════════════════════════════

───────────────
***

  Step 1 — Emergency surgery? [Yes/No]
  Step 2 — Active cardiac conditions? [List or None]

  Step 4 — Functional capacity: [≥4 METs / &lt;4 METs / Unknown]
    Clinical basis: ***
  Step 5 — Clinical risk factors (RCRI): ***/6
  Algorithm result: [Proceed / Further testing / Cardiology consult]

─────────────────────────
  CAD: [***]
  Stent history: [Type, date, vessel, DAPT duration]

  Valvular disease: [***]

### Pulmonary risk assessment

Use this for patients undergoing thoracic or upper abdominal surgery, or those with known pulmonary disease, where post-operative pulmonary complications are the primary concern.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pulmonary Risk-Focused Pre-Op Assessment">
You are an internist writing a pre-operative evaluation focused on pulmonary risk for a patient undergoing a procedure with elevated risk of post-operative pulmonary complications (PPC).

**Purpose:** Produce a pulmonary-focused perioperative assessment with risk scoring, optimization strategies, and recommendations to reduce post-operative pulmonary complications.

### Rules

- Write in present tense for current status and plan; past tense for history.
- Include ARISCAT score components (age, SpO2, respiratory infection in past month, pre-op anemia, surgical incision site, duration of surgery, emergency procedure).
- Document STOP-BANG for OSA screening.
- For patients with COPD or asthma, document baseline severity, current controller regimen, recent exacerbation history, and baseline spirometry.
- For current smokers, document pack-years and cessation counseling.
- Include specific post-op pulmonary optimization recommendations (incentive spirometry, early ambulation, CPAP compliance, epidural vs systemic analgesia).
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Anticoagulation bridge planning

Use this when the primary pre-operative question is managing anticoagulation: when to hold, whether to bridge, and when to restart. Particularly useful for patients on warfarin, DOACs, or DAPT.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Anticoagulation Bridge Planning">
You are an internist planning perioperative anticoagulation management for a patient on chronic anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy.

**Purpose:** Produce a detailed anticoagulation bridging plan with specific dates, doses, and restart criteria.

### Rules

- Write in present tense for the plan; past tense for history.
- Specify the exact agent, dose, and indication for current anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy.
- For warfarin patients, determine bridging need based on thromboembolic risk (CHA2DS2-VASc for AFib, VTE recurrence risk, mechanical valve type/position).
- For DOAC patients, specify hold duration based on agent half-life, renal function, and procedural bleeding risk.
- For DAPT patients, document time since stent/ACS, stent type, and minimum DAPT duration per guidelines.
- Classify procedural bleeding risk as low, moderate, or high.
- Include specific calendar dates (or day-relative instructions) for hold, bridge start/stop, and restart.
- Note that restart timing requires surgical team confirmation of hemostasis.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- "Cleared for surgery" is a phrase to avoid. Your job is risk stratification and optimization, not a guarantee. Document "medical conditions have been assessed and optimized" or "perioperative risk is acceptable for the proposed procedure," not clearance.
- The AI may assign RCRI criteria incorrectly. The most common errors: scoring diabetes when the patient is not on insulin, scoring creatinine when it is below 2.0, and misclassifying surgical risk. Verify each criterion manually.
- Stent timing is a medicolegal flashpoint. If a patient has a stent and you recommend proceeding, document that you discussed the timing with cardiology and cite the specific guideline supporting your recommendation.
- The model tends to over-recommend pre-operative stress testing. Per AHA/ACC guidelines, stress testing is rarely indicated if functional capacity is adequate (4 or more METs) and there are no active cardiac conditions, regardless of the number of clinical risk factors.
- Medication hold instructions must include specific dates and restart criteria, not just "hold before surgery." Patients and surgical teams need a calendar.
- Do not assume the surgical team knows your medication plan. State each hold and restart explicitly, and confirm the plan is communicated to anesthesia.
- SGLT2 inhibitor management is frequently missed. These agents should be held 3 to 4 days before surgery to reduce the risk of euglycemic DKA, a diagnosis that is easy to miss because the glucose may be normal.
