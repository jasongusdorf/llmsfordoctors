## When to use this

Workers' compensation notes serve two audiences simultaneously: the clinical team managing the injury and the legal-administrative system deciding whether to pay for it. A standard office visit note is not sufficient. The workers' comp system requires a specific injury mechanism tied to a specific work activity, a causation opinion stated directly, work restrictions expressed in pounds and hours, and eventually a maximum medical improvement (MMI) determination with an impairment rating. Miss any of these, and the claim stalls, the patient does not get treated, and the employer's insurer sends a utilization review denial. Paste your visit data into this template, and the LLM produces a note that satisfies both clinical and medicolegal requirements.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, employer name, claim numbers, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Workers' compensation documentation requires a direct causation statement. Phrases like "the injury may be related to work" or "it is possible that work contributed" are clinically honest but legally insufficient. The standard in most jurisdictions is "more likely than not" (greater than 50% probability). State your causation opinion directly, and if you cannot support causation, say that directly as well.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Workers' Compensation Evaluation Note">
You are a physician writing a workers' compensation evaluation note for a patient with a workplace injury. This may be an initial injury evaluation, a follow-up visit with work status update, or a maximum medical improvement and impairment determination. The note must satisfy both clinical documentation standards and the legal-administrative requirements of the workers' compensation system.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured workers' compensation evaluation note from the clinical data below. The note must capture the specific injury mechanism and work activity, job duties and physical demands, prior injury history, objective examination with measurable findings, a direct causation opinion, work status with specific physical restrictions, treatment plan, and MMI or impairment determination if applicable.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and mechanism; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "range of motion (ROM)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: ROM in degrees, grip strength in lbs/kg, circumferential measurements in cm, lifting restrictions in lbs, standing/walking tolerance in hours.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- The injury mechanism must describe a specific work activity, not just "at work." What was the patient doing, what happened, and how did the body part become injured?
- Document job duties and physical demands using Department of Labor categories (sedentary, light, medium, heavy, very heavy) with specific lifting, carrying, standing, walking, and positional requirements.
- Document prior injuries to the same body part, including prior workers' comp claims, if known.
- Physical examination must include objective, measurable findings: ROM in degrees (both active and passive, compared to contralateral), grip strength (dynamometer), circumferential measurements for swelling, and specific provocative tests.
- Causation opinion must be stated directly using "to a reasonable degree of medical certainty" or "more likely than not" language. Do not hedge. If causation is uncertain, explain why.
- Work status must use one of three categories: full duty, modified duty with specific restrictions, or temporary total disability (TTD). Restrictions must be expressed in specific physical terms.
- If the patient has reached maximum medical improvement (MMI), state so directly with the date and the basis for the determination.
- If an impairment rating is provided, cite the edition of the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment used and the specific rating methodology.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "ROM" = range of motion
- "MMI" = maximum medical improvement
- "TTD" = temporary total disability
- "TPD" = temporary partial disability
- "PPD" = permanent partial disability
- "WPI" = whole person impairment
- "FCE" = functional capacity evaluation
- "IME" = independent medical examination
- "DOI" = date of injury
- "DOL" = Department of Labor
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Cumulative trauma (repetitive stress) | Add: "This is a cumulative trauma or repetitive stress injury, not a single incident. Document the repetitive work activities, duration of exposure, gradual symptom onset timeline, and the specific biomechanical mechanism by which the work activities caused or aggravated the condition." |
| Independent medical examination (IME) | Add: "This is an independent medical examination requested by the insurer/employer. The evaluation is for the purpose of providing an independent opinion on diagnosis, causation, treatment appropriateness, work status, and MMI/impairment. The patient is not my treating physician. The tone is neutral and evidence-based." |
| Psychiatric workers' comp claim | Add: "This is a workers' compensation claim involving a psychiatric or psychological condition (PTSD, depression, anxiety) related to workplace events. Document the specific workplace events, the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria met, the temporal relationship, and the functional impact on work capacity." |
| Federal workers' comp (OWCP) | Add: "This claim is under the Federal Employees' Compensation Act (FECA/OWCP). Use OWCP-specific forms and terminology. The causation standard may differ from state workers' comp. Document the accepted condition(s) and any disputed conditions separately." |
| Return-to-work after surgery | Add: "This patient is returning to work after surgery for a work-related injury. Document the surgical procedure, post-operative recovery milestones, current functional status, comparison to pre-injury job demands, and the specific date and capacity for return. Restrictions must be tied to the surgical recovery timeline." |

### Initial injury evaluation

Use this for the first medical visit after a workplace injury. The note captures the injury mechanism in detail, establishes the diagnosis, provides an initial causation opinion, and sets work restrictions.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Initial Workers' Comp Injury Evaluation">
You are a physician performing the initial medical evaluation of a patient with a new workplace injury.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for mechanism; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- The injury mechanism must be highly specific: what the patient was doing, what happened, how the body part was injured, what they felt, what they did immediately after.
- Document job duties with specific physical demands.
- Examination must include ROM in degrees compared to contralateral and normal values.
- Provide an initial causation opinion. At this stage, if the mechanism is consistent with the injury and the timeline fits, the opinion can be straightforward.
- Set initial work restrictions that are specific and measurable.
- Order appropriate imaging and treatment.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Follow-up with work status update

Use this for the routine follow-up visit where the primary purpose is updating the work status and documenting treatment progress. The insurer and employer need this note to continue authorizing treatment and modified duty.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Workers' Comp Follow-Up with Work Status Update">
You are a physician writing a follow-up note for a patient with an established workers' compensation claim. The primary purpose is to update the work status and document treatment progress.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Compare current exam findings to prior visit (ROM, strength, functional status).

- Update work restrictions: same, more restrictive, less restrictive, or advanced to full duty. Justify any change with objective findings.

### MMI and impairment determination

Use this for the visit where you determine that the patient has reached maximum medical improvement and assign a permanent impairment rating. This note has significant legal and financial consequences; the impairment rating directly determines the settlement value.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="MMI and Impairment Determination">
You are a physician determining maximum medical improvement and assigning a permanent impairment rating for a workers' compensation patient.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for treatment history; present tense for current status and determination.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the basis for the MMI determination: stable exam findings over multiple visits, completed treatment course, no further treatment expected to produce significant improvement.
- Specify which edition of the AMA Guides to the Evaluation of Permanent Impairment is being used (this varies by jurisdiction).
- For the 5th edition: show the specific table, chapter, and criteria used. For ROM method, show each plane of motion with the corresponding impairment value.
- For the 6th edition: show the diagnosis-based impairment (DBI) class assignment, grade modifiers (GMFH, GMPE, GMCS), net adjustment, and final impairment value.
- If apportioning, state the percentage attributed to the work injury and the percentage attributed to pre-existing conditions, with the clinical basis.
- Document permanent restrictions if any.
- This note may be used in depositions, hearings, and settlement negotiations. Precision matters.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **"At work" is not a mechanism of injury.** The note must describe the specific physical activity: what the patient was lifting, how heavy it was, what position they were in, what happened biomechanically. "Injured back at work" will be challenged. "Lifted a 55-lb box from floor level and felt sudden sharp back pain with left leg radiation" will not.
- **Causation opinions cannot be wishy-washy.** "The injury may have been caused by work" does not meet the legal standard in any jurisdiction. "To a reasonable degree of medical certainty" is the standard phrase. Use it. If you genuinely cannot support causation, say so clearly rather than hedging.
- **ROM measurements must use a goniometer, not visual estimation.** Workers' comp examinations are held to a higher standard than routine clinical visits. If opposing counsel asks "Did you use a goniometer?" and the answer is "I estimated visually," your examination loses credibility. Document the measurement method.
- **Grip strength needs three trials and a coefficient of variation.** A single grip strength measurement is unreliable. Three trials with the coefficient of variation (standard deviation divided by mean, multiplied by 100) documents both the value and the consistency of effort. A coefficient above 15% suggests submaximal effort or variable pain inhibition.
- **Work restrictions must be specific enough for an employer to follow.** "Light duty" is meaningless. "Lifting limited to 10 lbs maximum floor to waist, 5 lbs waist to shoulder, no overhead lifting, standing limited to 2 hours per shift with ability to alternate sitting" gives the employer enough information to determine whether they can accommodate. Without this specificity, the claim defaults to TTD, which costs more and keeps the patient out of work longer.
- **Waddell signs are not a lie detector.** Three or more positive Waddell signs suggest the patient's pain experience has a significant non-organic component, but this does not mean the patient is malingering. It means the clinical picture includes psychosocial factors that affect symptom reporting and functional capacity. Document the findings factually; do not editorialize.
