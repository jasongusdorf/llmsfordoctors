## When to use this

A college soccer player sits on your exam table, 10 days out from a concussion, symptom-free at rest but unsure whether she can start running again. You need to document her Sport Concussion Assessment Tool (SCAT5) findings, her vestibular/ocular motor screening (VOMS) results, where she stands in the graded return-to-play protocol, and your clearance decision, all in a format that the athletic trainer, the team, and the family can follow. Paste your visit data into this template, and the LLM produces a structured sports medicine note that captures the SCAT5 and Immediate Post-Concussion Assessment and Cognitive Testing (ImPACT) results, VOMS scores, Buffalo treadmill protocol progression, sport and position and competition level, return-to-play (RTP) criteria and protocol stage, functional testing thresholds, pre-participation physical examination (PPE) cardiovascular screening using the American Heart Association (AHA) 14-point checklist, and Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) and female athlete triad screening.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Return-to-play decisions carry risk for the athlete and liability for the physician. Document the specific criteria met (or not met) at each protocol stage. "Cleared for return to play" without documented symptom resolution, cognitive recovery, and exertion tolerance is defensible neither clinically nor legally.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Sports Medicine Clinic Visit Note">
You are a sports medicine physician writing an outpatient clinic note after seeing an athlete for injury evaluation, concussion management, pre-participation examination, or return-to-play assessment. The patient may present with one or more of the following: concussion, ACL or meniscal injury, rotator cuff pathology, stress fracture, tendinopathy, or a return-to-play evaluation. Identify the clinical context from the information provided and structure the note accordingly.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient sports medicine clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture the athlete's sport-specific context, injury mechanism, validated assessment tools, functional testing results, imaging interpretation, treatment plan, and return-to-play protocol status with criteria-based progression.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and mechanism; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "Sport Concussion Assessment Tool (SCAT5)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: SCAT5 subscores, VOMS scores, ImPACT composite scores, ROM in degrees, strength testing, functional test results.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Always document the athlete's sport, position, competition level (recreational, high school, college, professional), and season timing.
- Organize the Assessment as a numbered problem list with the primary injury first, then contributing factors, then return-to-play status.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: protocol stage advancement criteria, therapy prescription with type and frequency, imaging orders, surgical referral indications, and explicit clearance or non-clearance decision with rationale.
- For concussion: document SCAT5 subscores (symptom number and severity, cognitive, balance), VOMS results (each domain with symptom provocation score), ImPACT composite scores compared to baseline, Buffalo treadmill protocol stage and heart rate achieved, and specific RTP protocol stage.
- For ACL/meniscal injury: document mechanism, exam findings (Lachman, anterior drawer, pivot shift, McMurray, joint line tenderness), MRI findings, surgical vs. non-operative criteria, and functional testing if post-operative (single-leg hop, Y-balance, quad symmetry index).
- For rotator cuff: document provocative tests (Neer, Hawkins, empty can, external rotation lag, belly press), imaging, and cuff integrity assessment.
- For stress fractures: document high-risk vs. low-risk classification, imaging findings, weight-bearing status, and return-to-running protocol.
- For pre-participation exam: document AHA 14-point cardiovascular screening, musculoskeletal screening, and RED-S/female athlete triad screening.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt" and "athlete" interchangeably based on context
- "s/p" = status post
- "SCAT5" = Sport Concussion Assessment Tool, 5th edition
- "VOMS" = Vestibular/Ocular Motor Screening
- "ImPACT" = Immediate Post-Concussion Assessment and Cognitive Testing
- "RTP" = return to play
- "RTA" = return to activity
- "PPE" = pre-participation physical examination
- "AHA" = American Heart Association
- "RED-S" = Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport
- "ACL" = anterior cruciate ligament
- "MRI" = magnetic resonance imaging
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Pediatric/adolescent athlete | Add: "This is a pediatric or adolescent athlete. Apply more conservative return-to-play timelines. Include growth plate assessment, parental involvement in decision-making, and school accommodation plan for concussion." |
| Professional/elite athlete | Add: "This is a professional or elite athlete. Include contract and season considerations in the context (not as medical decision factors), coordination with team medical staff, and documentation of informed consent for any accelerated timeline." |
| Chronic exertional compartment syndrome | Add: "Evaluate for chronic exertional compartment syndrome. Include exercise-induced symptoms, compartment pressure measurements (pre and post exercise with values in mmHg), and surgical referral criteria." |
| Shoulder instability | Add: "This patient has recurrent shoulder instability. Include number of dislocations, direction, provocative tests (apprehension, relocation, surprise), Beighton hypermobility score, and surgical vs. rehabilitation decision criteria." |
| Running injury | Add: "This is a running-related injury. Include weekly mileage, recent mileage changes, shoe type and age, running surface, training errors, biomechanical assessment findings, and return-to-running protocol (walk-run intervals)." |

### Concussion return-to-play

Use this for the concussion patient being evaluated for return-to-play advancement. The note is focused entirely on concussion resolution: symptom status, cognitive recovery, vestibular-ocular normalization, exertion tolerance, and the specific protocol stage the athlete is cleared to enter.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Concussion Return-to-Play Evaluation">
You are a sports medicine physician evaluating a concussed athlete for return-to-play protocol advancement.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document time since injury, total symptom duration, and current symptom status.
- Include SCAT5 subscores compared to baseline (or normative data if no baseline).
- Include VOMS results with specific domain scores.
- Include ImPACT composite scores compared to baseline if available.
- Document Buffalo treadmill protocol results with symptom-free heart rate.
- Specify the exact RTP protocol stage the athlete is being cleared to enter, the criteria met, and what must happen before the next stage.
- For athletes &lt;18 years old, note that more conservative timelines are recommended.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### ACL surgical decision

Use this for the athlete with a confirmed or suspected ACL tear being evaluated for surgical versus non-operative management. The note captures the injury mechanism, exam findings, MRI interpretation, and the specific factors that inform the surgical decision: sport, competition level, associated injuries, functional instability, and patient goals.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="ACL Injury Evaluation and Surgical Decision">
You are a sports medicine physician evaluating an athlete with a confirmed or suspected ACL injury, determining whether surgical reconstruction is indicated.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the mechanism of injury with specific detail (contact vs. non-contact, deceleration, pivoting, valgus).
- Document all knee exam findings: Lachman (grade, endpoint), anterior drawer, pivot shift, McMurray, joint line tenderness, valgus/varus stress at 0° and 30°, effusion, ROM.
- Document MRI findings: ACL status (complete tear, partial tear, location), meniscal status (medial/lateral, tear type, location), chondral status, bone bruise pattern, MCL/LCL status.

- If surgical: include graft discussion (BPTP, hamstring, quad tendon, allograft), timing, and pre-hab plan.
- If non-operative: include specific criteria for reconsidering surgery (giving-way episodes, meniscal damage progression).
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Pre-participation physical examination

Use this for the annual pre-participation physical. The note is organized around the AHA 14-point cardiovascular screen, musculoskeletal screening, and sport-specific risk factor assessment including RED-S and female athlete triad screening where indicated.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pre-Participation Physical Examination">
You are a sports medicine physician performing a pre-participation physical examination for an athlete.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Complete all 14 elements of the AHA cardiovascular screening checklist with specific findings.
- Document the musculoskeletal screening exam systematically.
- For female athletes: screen for the female athlete triad (energy deficiency, menstrual dysfunction, low bone mineral density) and RED-S.
- For all athletes: screen for heat illness risk, sickle cell trait status, concussion history, mental health (PHQ-2 minimum).
- Clearance determination must be one of: cleared without restriction, cleared with recommendations, not cleared pending further evaluation (specify what), or not cleared (specify reason).
- Document sport-specific considerations if relevant (e.g., weight-class sports, contact sports, aquatic sports).
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **No baseline ImPACT does not mean you cannot make a return-to-play decision.** Normative data exists by age and sex. Compare the post-injury scores to published norms. A "return to baseline" requirement without an actual baseline creates an impossible standard and delays clearance unnecessarily.
- **VOMS abnormalities may persist after symptoms resolve.** An athlete who reports zero symptoms but has a near point of convergence of 12 cm and symptom provocation with VOR testing is not recovered. Test the vestibular-ocular system specifically; do not rely on symptom self-report alone.
- **The Buffalo treadmill protocol is diagnostic, not therapeutic.** Passing it (reaching 80% of age-predicted max HR without symptom provocation) confirms physiologic recovery. Failing it identifies athletes who need subsymptom-threshold aerobic exercise as treatment for prolonged recovery, not further rest.
- **Functional testing thresholds for ACL return-to-sport are moving targets.** The commonly cited 90% limb symmetry index on hop testing is a minimum, not a guarantee of readiness. Quad strength symmetry and psychological readiness (ACL-RSI score) are equally important. Document all three.
- **Pre-participation cardiovascular screening catches structural disease, not all sudden death risk.** The AHA 14-point screen is designed to identify hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), anomalous coronary arteries, and other structural causes. A normal screen does not exclude channelopathies (long QT, Brugada) or commotio cordis risk. Document what the screen evaluates and what it does not.
- **RED-S is not limited to female athletes.** Male athletes in weight-class sports, endurance sports, and aesthetic sports (gymnastics, diving) are also at risk for energy deficiency with bone, hormonal, and cardiovascular consequences. Screen any athlete with unexplained stress fractures, declining performance, or weight concerns, regardless of sex.
- **Document the clearance decision with specificity.** "Cleared for return to play" is not the same as "cleared to advance to stage 3 of a 6-stage RTP protocol." The latter is defensible. The former, if the athlete is re-injured at practice, may not be.
