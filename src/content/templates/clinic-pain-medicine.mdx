## When to use this

The visit lasted 25 minutes. In that time, you reviewed a urine drug screen with an unexpected finding, checked the Prescription Drug Monitoring Program (PDMP), discussed a spinal cord stimulator (SCS) trial, adjusted a buprenorphine patch, and documented a pain agreement renewal. Now you need to write a note that satisfies clinical, regulatory, and medicolegal requirements all at once. Paste your visit data into this template, and the LLM produces a structured pain medicine note that tracks the Pain, Enjoyment of life, and General activity (PEG) score, calculates morphine equivalent daily dose (MEDD), documents the Opioid Risk Tool (ORT) score, interprets the urine drug screen (UDS) with expected versus found substances, records the PDMP check, catalogs interventional procedures with dates and response, and captures the opioid agreement status. The note treats documentation as both clinical care and risk management, because in pain medicine the two are inseparable.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Pain medicine documentation carries legal weight beyond the clinical encounter. UDS results, PDMP checks, opioid agreements, and risk stratification scores are auditable elements. If you document a PDMP check, you performed one. If you omit it, auditors and regulators will assume you did not. Get the facts right before signing.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pain Medicine Clinic Visit Note">
You are a pain medicine physician writing an outpatient clinic note after seeing a patient for chronic pain management, interventional procedure follow-up, or new pain consultation. The patient may carry one or more of the following conditions: chronic low back pain, complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS), neuropathic pain, refractory headache, cancer pain, or failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS). Identify all active pain diagnoses from the clinical information provided and structure the note around them.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient pain medicine clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture pain assessment with validated scoring, opioid risk stratification, urine drug screen interpretation, PDMP documentation, interventional procedure history with response tracking, current medication regimen with MEDD calculation, and a specific plan for each pain condition.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "morphine equivalent daily dose (MEDD)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: pain scores, MEDD, ORT score, UDS results, procedure dates, doses.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a numbered problem list with the primary pain diagnosis first, then contributing conditions, then opioid management as a distinct problem.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: medication name, dose, change direction, procedure name with target level/structure, referral with reason.
- For opioid therapy: document MEDD, ORT score, UDS results (expected vs. found), PDMP check date and findings, opioid agreement status with date, naloxone prescription status, and functional assessment (PEG score).
- For interventional procedures: document procedure name, date, target structure/level, technique (fluoroscopy, ultrasound), outcome (percent relief, duration of relief), and whether diagnostic or therapeutic.
- For CRPS: document Budapest criteria findings and whether criteria are met.
- For neuropathic pain: document the specific nerve or distribution, etiology, and response to neuropathic agents.
- Never recommend abrupt opioid discontinuation. Tapers must specify rate and monitoring plan.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "MEDD" = morphine equivalent daily dose
- "PEG" = Pain, Enjoyment of life, General activity score
- "ORT" = Opioid Risk Tool
- "UDS" = urine drug screen
- "PDMP" = Prescription Drug Monitoring Program
- "SCS" = spinal cord stimulator
- "ESI" = epidural steroid injection
- "RFA" = radiofrequency ablation
- "MBB" = medial branch block
- "CRPS" = complex regional pain syndrome
- "FBSS" = failed back surgery syndrome
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Cancer pain | Add: "This patient has cancer-related pain. Opioid risk stratification (ORT, UDS, PDMP) is less relevant than symptom control and quality of life. Focus on adequate analgesia, breakthrough dosing, palliative radiation or nerve block considerations, and coordination with oncology." |
| Opioid taper | Add: "This visit focuses on an opioid taper plan. Document the clinical rationale for the taper, the current MEDD, the target MEDD, the taper rate (typically 10% per month for chronic therapy), and the monitoring plan including frequent follow-up, UDS, and screening for opioid withdrawal symptoms." |
| Pediatric/adolescent pain | Add: "This is a pediatric or adolescent patient. Use age-appropriate pain assessment tools (FLACC, Wong-Baker). Opioid prescribing follows stricter guidelines. Include school attendance, family dynamics, and developmental impact." |
| Headache-focused visit | Add: "This patient's primary diagnosis is a refractory headache disorder. Include headache diary data (frequency, severity, duration, triggers), acute medication usage days per month, medication overuse headache screening, and preventive medication trial history with doses and reasons for discontinuation." |
| Pre-SCS evaluation | Add: "This visit is a pre-spinal cord stimulator evaluation. Include the psychological clearance status, SCS trial plan (percutaneous vs. paddle, permanent lead placement), pain mapping, insurance authorization status, and specific functional goals for the trial." |

### Chronic opioid therapy management

Use this for the visit that is primarily about monitoring and managing chronic opioid therapy. The clinical question is whether the current regimen is working, safe, and still indicated, and the documentation needs to show you asked all three.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Chronic Opioid Therapy Management">
You are a pain medicine physician writing an outpatient note focused on chronic opioid therapy management. The visit centers on monitoring safety, efficacy, and ongoing indication for opioid use.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the "4 A's" of opioid therapy monitoring: Analgesia (PEG score), Activity (functional status), Adverse effects (side effects), and Aberrant behavior (agreement adherence, UDS, PDMP).
- Calculate and document MEDD with conversion math shown.
- Interpret UDS results with expected vs. found for each substance, including discussion of discrepancies.
- Document PDMP check with date and concordance.
- Include ORT score with category and opioid agreement status.
- If tapering, document rate, current vs. target MEDD, and monitoring frequency.
- Never recommend abrupt discontinuation; always specify a taper plan if dose reduction is indicated.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Interventional procedure follow-up

Use this for the visit after an epidural steroid injection, radiofrequency ablation, medial branch block, joint injection, or nerve block. The note captures the procedure details, the patient's response, and the decision about next steps.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Interventional Procedure Follow-Up">
You are a pain medicine physician writing an outpatient follow-up note after an interventional pain procedure. The note documents the procedure response and guides the next treatment decision.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the specific procedure performed with date, target, technique, and injectate.
- Quantify pain relief as percentage reduction from baseline and duration of relief.
- For diagnostic blocks (MBB), document whether the result meets criteria for RFA (typically ≥80% relief for the duration of the local anesthetic).
- For ESIs, document the number performed in the past 12 months and cumulative steroid exposure.
- For SCS, document trial parameters, lead position, programming settings, and patient-reported relief.
- Include functional improvement, not just pain score reduction.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### CRPS management

Use this for the patient being evaluated or treated for complex regional pain syndrome. The note documents Budapest criteria, CRPS-specific findings, and the multidisciplinary treatment approach.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="CRPS Management">
You are a pain medicine physician writing an outpatient note for a patient being evaluated or managed for complex regional pain syndrome. Structure the note around Budapest diagnostic criteria, disease severity, and multidisciplinary management.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document all four Budapest criteria categories: sensory, vasomotor, sudomotor/edema, motor/trophic. Record specific findings in each category.
- State whether clinical or research Budapest criteria are met.
- Include affected limb temperature comparison (side-to-side difference), edema measurement, ROM comparison, and grip/pinch strength if upper extremity.
- Document CRPS severity score if used.
- Include triple-phase bone scan, MRI, or thermography results if available.
- Treatment must address the biopsychosocial model: pharmacologic, interventional (sympathetic blocks, SCS, intrathecal), rehabilitative (functional restoration, desensitization, mirror therapy, graded motor imagery), and psychological.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **MEDD calculation errors are the single most dangerous mistake in this template.** The model may use incorrect conversion factors. Verify every conversion against the CDC or equianalgesic table your practice uses. The most common errors: treating hydrocodone as equianalgesic to morphine (it is, 1:1), but converting oxycodone incorrectly (the conversion factor is 1.5, not 1.0). Methadone conversion is nonlinear and should always be verified manually.
- **UDS interpretation requires clinical judgment, not just positive/negative matching.** A negative result for a prescribed opioid may reflect timing (last dose more than detection window), rapid metabolism, or diversion. A positive result for a non-prescribed substance may be a false positive (poppy seeds for opiates, diphenhydramine for PCP on immunoassay). Document your clinical interpretation, not just the lab result. Confirm unexpected findings with liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) before making clinical decisions.
- **The PDMP check must have a date.** Documenting "PDMP reviewed" without a date does not satisfy most state requirements. Include the date you checked, what you found, and whether it was concordant.
- **PEG scores track what matters more than pain scores alone.** A patient whose pain is 6/10 but whose PEG is 3/10 is doing well functionally. A patient whose pain is 4/10 but whose PEG is 8/10 is not. The PEG captures what opioid therapy is supposed to improve: function and quality of life, not just the number.
- **Interventional procedure decisions follow diagnostic logic.** Do not skip the diagnostic medial branch block before proceeding to RFA. Document the percentage relief, duration, and concordance with the expected local anesthetic duration. Payers and peer reviewers will look for this sequence.
- **ESI frequency limits exist for steroid exposure, not arbitrary rationing.** Document the number of ESIs in the past 12 months and the cumulative steroid dose. Most guidelines recommend no more than 3-4 ESIs per spinal region per year. If you are approaching this limit, the note should reflect why you are continuing ESIs versus pursuing other interventions.
- **Never document "patient is drug-seeking" or "aberrant behavior" without specific factual basis.** Document the facts: "UDS positive for benzodiazepines, not prescribed. Patient states she took a friend's diazepam for anxiety on [date]." Let the facts speak. Labels without evidence create legal liability and damage the therapeutic relationship.
