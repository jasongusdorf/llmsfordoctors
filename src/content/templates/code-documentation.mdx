## When to use this

The code lasted 18 minutes. You have a crumpled code sheet with half-legible timestamps, a nurse's note on the back of a towel, and a pharmacist who remembers the second dose of epinephrine but not the third. The patient either has a pulse now and needs a post-cardiac arrest care bundle, or does not and needs a death summary. Either way, you need a structured note that accounts for every minute, every drug, every shock, and every decision. This template takes the raw, disorganized data from a resuscitation event and produces a time-stamped record organized by initial rhythm, cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) intervals, defibrillation with times and energy, medications with doses and timing, airway management, reversible cause workup (H's and T's), team roles, return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC) time or time of death pronouncement, post-ROSC care, and family notification.

This template is used after the event, not during it. It is a reconstruction tool, not a real-time documentation system.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Code documentation is a medicolegal record. Timestamps matter. Drug doses and intervals matter. Who intubated and who ran the code matters. If the code sheet has gaps, document what you know, mark what you do not know with "***," and note in the record that the documentation was reconstructed from available sources. Do not fabricate timestamps to fill gaps.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Code / Rapid Response Documentation">
You are a physician documenting a cardiac arrest (code blue) or rapid response event. You are writing this note AFTER the event, organizing the raw data from code sheets, nursing notes, pharmacy records, and your memory into a structured, time-stamped resuscitation record. The event may have resulted in return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC), ongoing resuscitation with transfer, or death. Structure the note accordingly.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured resuscitation event record from the clinical data below. The note must capture the time-stamped sequence of events, initial rhythm, CPR quality indicators, defibrillation details, medication administration with exact times and doses, airway management, reversible cause workup, team composition, outcome (ROSC or death), post-arrest care if applicable, and family notification.

### Rules

- Write in past tense throughout; this is a retrospective account of a completed event.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "return of spontaneous circulation (ROSC)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Every medication, defibrillation, and major intervention must have a timestamp. If the exact time is unknown, write "approximately [time]" or "***."
- Epinephrine timing matters: document interval between doses (target every 3-5 minutes per ACLS). If intervals were longer, document the reason if known.
- For defibrillation: document each shock with time, energy (joules), and resulting rhythm.
- Document the H's and T's workup explicitly: which reversible causes were considered, which were evaluated, and which were found or excluded.
- Document airway management: device (laryngeal mask airway (LMA), endotracheal tube (ETT), surgical airway), placed by whom, method of confirmation (end-tidal CO2 (ETCO2), direct visualization, chest rise, bilateral breath sounds), tube size, depth at lip.
- If ROSC achieved: document the exact time, the first post-ROSC rhythm, blood pressure, and the post-ROSC care bundle (targeted temperature management (TTM), vasopressors, emergent cardiac catheterization, ventilator settings, labs).
- If death: document time of death, who pronounced, and the discussion with family.
- Include pre-arrest context: what the patient was admitted for, baseline code status, what triggered the arrest or rapid response.
- Do not fabricate any timestamps or clinical data. Write "***" for unknown information. Note if the record was reconstructed.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "ROSC" = return of spontaneous circulation
- "ACLS" = Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support
- "CPR" = cardiopulmonary resuscitation
- "ETCO2" = end-tidal CO2
- "PEA" = pulseless electrical activity
- "VF" = ventricular fibrillation
- "VT" = ventricular tachycardia (pulseless)
- "TTM" = targeted temperature management
- "H's and T's" = reversible causes of cardiac arrest
- "LMA" = laryngeal mask airway
- "ETT" = endotracheal tube
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| In-hospital non-cardiac arrest | Add: "This was a respiratory arrest without cardiac arrest. The patient had a pulse throughout. Focus on airway management, respiratory support interventions, and the escalation decision (intubation, BiPAP, transfer to ICU). Omit the cardiac arrest-specific sections (defibrillation, CPR quality, H's and T's)." |
| Obstetric code | Add: "This is a maternal cardiac arrest. Include: left uterine displacement, perimortem cesarean delivery consideration (4-5 minutes if no ROSC), fetal monitoring status, OB and NICU team activation, and modifications to ACLS for pregnancy (avoid compression of IVC, adjusted defibrillation pad placement)." |
| Anesthesia-related arrest | Add: "This cardiac arrest occurred in the OR under anesthesia. Include: anesthetic agents administered before arrest, volatile agent and neuromuscular blocker status, last set of hemodynamic parameters from the anesthesia record, and consideration of malignant hyperthermia, local anesthetic toxicity (lipid emulsion), and anaphylaxis as causes." |
| Out-of-hospital arrest (ED arrival) | Add: "This patient arrived to the ED in cardiac arrest from the field. Include EMS data: response time, initial field rhythm, field CPR duration, shocks delivered in field, medications given by EMS, transport time. Document the transition from EMS to ED team." |
| Comfort measures / DNR event | Add: "This patient had a code status change to comfort measures / DNR during the event. Document who made the decision (healthcare proxy, attending), the time of the decision, the time resuscitation was discontinued, and the discussion that led to the change." |

### Rapid response (non-arrest)

Use this for the rapid response call that does not escalate to a cardiac arrest. The patient has a pulse but is deteriorating: hypotension, respiratory failure, altered mental status, arrhythmia, or clinical concern that triggered the rapid response team. The note captures the clinical assessment, interventions, and disposition decision.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Rapid Response (Non-Arrest) Documentation">
You are a physician documenting a rapid response team activation for a patient with acute clinical deterioration who did NOT progress to cardiac arrest.

### Rules

- Write in past tense throughout.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the trigger for rapid response activation (vital sign criteria, nursing concern, family concern, clinical deterioration).
- Document the patient's condition on arrival: vitals, rhythm, mental status, respiratory status, and the clinical assessment.
- Include interventions performed with times: fluid boluses, vasopressors, airway interventions (high-flow nasal cannula, BiPAP, intubation), medication administration.
- Include labs and imaging obtained during the response with results.
- Document the disposition decision: remain on current floor with increased monitoring, transfer to step-down, transfer to ICU, activate code team if arrest.
- Document communication: attending notified, family notified, receiving team if transferred.
- Do not fabricate timestamps. Write "***" for unknown data.

### Pediatric resuscitation

Use this for a pediatric cardiac arrest or resuscitation. The note follows Pediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS) algorithms with weight-based dosing, age-appropriate rhythms and causes, and family presence considerations.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pediatric Resuscitation Documentation">
You are a physician documenting a pediatric cardiac arrest or resuscitation event.

### Rules

- Write in past tense throughout.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- All drug doses must be weight-based. Document the patient's weight (actual or estimated via length-based tape) and show the dose calculation.
- Epinephrine: 0.01 mg/kg (0.1 mL/kg of 1:10,000 concentration) IV/IO every 3-5 minutes.
- Defibrillation: 2 J/kg first shock, 4 J/kg subsequent shocks, maximum 10 J/kg or adult dose.
- Document the specific Pediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS) algorithm followed.
- Pediatric-specific reversible causes to consider: congenital heart disease, ductal-dependent lesions, sepsis, accidental ingestion, NAT (non-accidental trauma).
- Document airway with age-appropriate ETT size (cuffed: age/4 + 3.5; uncuffed: age/4 + 4), depth at lip, and method of confirmation.
- Include IO access if placed: site, needle size, and flush.
- Family presence during resuscitation: document if offered, if family was present, and support provided.
- Do not fabricate timestamps or data. Write "***" for unknown data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Epinephrine timing is the most common documentation gap.** The ACLS protocol specifies epinephrine every 3-5 minutes, but during an actual code, the interval frequently stretches to 7-8 minutes. Document the actual intervals, not the ideal ones. If the interval was longer than 5 minutes, the note should reflect that. Quality review committees will check.
- **PEA is a rhythm, not a diagnosis.** Documenting "PEA arrest" without a workup for reversible causes suggests the code team did not look for the reason. The H's and T's section should show what was considered, how it was evaluated (bedside echo, labs, clinical assessment), and what was found or excluded. A PEA arrest with an identified and treated cause has a fundamentally different outcome trajectory than one without.
- **ETCO2 during CPR is your best indicator of CPR quality and prognosis.** An ETCO2 consistently below 10 mmHg during CPR suggests either poor compressions or a low-flow state that will not respond to further resuscitation. An abrupt rise to 40+ suggests ROSC before you can even check a pulse. Document the ETCO2 values; they are underused and highly informative.
- **Post-ROSC care is a distinct clinical phase, not an afterthought.** The post-cardiac arrest syndrome includes myocardial stunning, reperfusion injury, neurological injury, and the underlying cause of the arrest. Document the care bundle: hemodynamics (MAP target, vasopressors), ventilator (avoid hyperoxia and hypocapnia), temperature management decision, and catheterization decision. Each of these has evidence-based targets.
- **Do not backfill timestamps.** If you do not know when the second dose of epinephrine was given, write "approximately [time]" or "***." A note with fabricated timestamps is worse than one with documented gaps, both clinically and legally.
- **Family notification is part of the medical record.** Document who was notified, when, by whom, the method (in person vs. phone), and whether family was offered the opportunity to be present during the resuscitation. Many institutions now support family presence during codes, and the documentation should reflect the institution's practice.
