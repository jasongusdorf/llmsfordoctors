## When to use this

Use this template when an insurance company has denied coverage for a service you believe is medically necessary and you need to draft a formal written appeal.

Drafts a formal appeal letter in response to an insurance denial. Provide the denial reason, the clinical case, and any supporting evidence, and get a structured appeal that addresses the insurer's specific objections.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, insurance ID, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Insurance Denial Appeal Letter">
You are a physician writing a formal appeal of an insurance coverage denial.

Using the information below, write an appeal letter that:

**Header**
[Date]
To: [INSURANCE COMPANY] Appeals Department
Re: Appeal of Denial, [REQUESTED SERVICE]
Reference/Case Number: [DENIAL REFERENCE NUMBER]
Member ID: [REDACTED, add before sending]

**Opening**
State that you are formally appealing the denial of [REQUESTED SERVICE]. Reference the denial letter date and stated reason for denial.

**Denial Reason Rebuttal**
Address the specific reason(s) for denial point by point:
- Quote the insurer's stated reason
- Explain why this reason does not apply to this patient's situation
- Provide clinical evidence that contradicts the denial rationale

**Clinical Case Summary**
Concise summary of why this service is medically necessary (do not simply repeat the prior auth letter; add new information or reframe the argument).

**New or Additional Evidence**
Include any new clinical data, specialist opinions, updated guidelines, or clinical deterioration since the initial request that strengthens the case.

**Regulatory and Contractual Arguments**
Where applicable:
- Reference the plan's own coverage policy and how this patient meets criteria
- Cite applicable state or federal regulations (mental health parity, step therapy reform laws, etc.)
- Reference any external review rights

**Request for Action**
Formally request reversal of the denial and approval of the service. Request a written response within the regulatory timeframe. Note your intent to escalate to external review if not resolved.

**Closing**
Offer to discuss the case by phone. Include contact information placeholder.

Tone: professional, factual, firm. Focus on clinical evidence and contractual obligations, not frustration.

## Variations

### First-level internal appeal

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="First-Level Internal Appeal">
You are a physician writing a first-level internal appeal of an insurance coverage denial.

Write a formal appeal letter that:

1. States this is a first-level internal appeal under the plan's grievance and appeals process
2. References the denial letter date, denial reason, and reference number
3. Provides a focused rebuttal of the stated denial reason with clinical evidence
4. Summarizes the clinical case and medical necessity
5. Cites the insurer's own coverage policy criteria and explains how this patient meets each criterion
6. Requests reversal and written response within the plan's standard appeal timeframe (typically 30 days)
7. Notes that you reserve the right to request external review if this appeal is denied

Tone: professional, factual, and direct. This is the first formal challenge -- be thorough but not adversarial.

### Second-level / external review appeal

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Second-Level / External Review Appeal">
You are a physician writing a second-level appeal after a first-level internal appeal was denied. This letter may be used for external independent review.

Write an appeal letter that:

1. States this is a second-level appeal and/or request for external independent review
2. Summarizes the appeal history: original request date, denial date, first appeal date, and second denial date
3. Identifies any new clinical evidence, deterioration, or guideline updates since the first appeal
4. Provides a detailed rebuttal of both the original denial reason and any new reasons cited in the first appeal denial
5. Cites peer-reviewed literature (2-3 key references) supporting medical necessity
6. References applicable federal or state regulations: ERISA, mental health parity, state external review statutes, or step therapy reform laws as relevant
7. Explicitly requests independent external review by a board-certified specialist in the relevant field
8. Notes the patient's right to file a complaint with the state insurance commissioner if external review is denied

Tone: firm, thorough, and formal. This is the escalation -- establish a clear record for regulatory review.

### Expedited appeal for urgent cases

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Expedited Appeal – Urgent Clinical Need">
You are a physician writing an expedited appeal for an insurance denial where delay poses a serious risk to the patient's health.

Write an urgent appeal letter that:

1. States prominently at the top: "REQUEST FOR EXPEDITED APPEAL -- URGENT CLINICAL NEED"
2. Cites the regulatory basis for expedited review (72-hour turnaround under ACA/state law)
3. Explains the specific clinical urgency: what harm will occur if the service is delayed
4. Provides a concise clinical summary focused on time-sensitive elements
5. Rebuts the denial reason with the strongest available evidence
6. Requests immediate authorization or peer-to-peer review within 24 hours
7. States that you will contact the state insurance department if expedited review is not granted
8. Requests confirmation of receipt and expedited processing

Tone: urgent, direct, factual. Emphasize the clinical timeline and risk of harm from delay.

## Example output

> **Re: Appeal of Denial -- MRI Lumbar Spine with Contrast**
> Reference Number: DN-2026-44819
>
> Dear Appeals Review Committee,
>
> I am writing to formally appeal the denial of an MRI lumbar spine with contrast for my patient, issued on March 10, 2026 (Reference: DN-2026-44819). The stated reason for denial was "conservative management not yet exhausted."
>
> **Rebuttal of Denial Reason**
> The denial states that the patient has not completed an adequate trial of conservative therapy. This is factually incorrect. The patient has completed:
> - 8 weeks of physical therapy (12 sessions, documented functional decline during treatment)
> - 6-week trial of naproxen 500 mg BID (discontinued for GI side effects)
> - 4-week trial of cyclobenzaprine 10 mg TID (no improvement in radicular symptoms)
>
> **Clinical Summary**
> This is a 52-year-old patient with 4 months of progressive left L5 radiculopathy with new onset of foot drop (dorsiflexion strength 3/5) documented on exam two weeks ago. EMG/NCS performed on March 5, 2026 confirmed active L5 denervation. The progressive motor deficit represents a time-sensitive indication for advanced imaging to evaluate for surgical lesion.
>
> **Supporting Evidence**
> The American College of Radiology Appropriateness Criteria rates MRI lumbar spine as "usually appropriate" for low back pain with new or progressive neurological deficit (ACR 2024, Variant 4). The North American Spine Society guidelines recommend urgent imaging when progressive motor weakness is present to rule out cauda equina syndrome or compressive lesion requiring surgical intervention.
>
> **Request**
> I respectfully request immediate reversal of this denial and authorization of the MRI. Given the progressive motor deficit, any further delay in imaging poses a risk of irreversible nerve damage. I am available to discuss this case at [PHONE]. If this appeal is not resolved within 30 days, I intend to pursue external independent review.
>
> Sincerely,
> [PHYSICIAN SIGNATURE]

## Tips & pitfalls

- Always include the denial reference number and quote the exact denial reason from the letter. Reviewers process hundreds of appeals; make it easy to match yours to the case.
- Keep a copy of every appeal letter sent, as you may need it for external review or state insurance department complaints.
- Time limits for appeals vary by plan and state. Check the denial letter for the deadline -- missing it can forfeit your appeal rights.
- Insurance reviewers look for documentation that the patient meets their specific coverage criteria, not just general medical necessity. Get the insurer's policy document and address each criterion individually.
- Common reasons appeals fail: the letter simply restates the original prior auth argument, doesn't address the specific denial reason, or lacks supporting guideline citations.
- Verify every clinical guideline citation and study reference before sending. AI-generated citations may sound plausible but be fabricated.
- For ERISA-governed plans, federal law requires the insurer to provide the specific plan provisions on which the denial is based. If they haven't, note this deficiency in your appeal.
- Always confirm that your appeal is being submitted within the plan's appeal window and through the correct channel (fax, portal, or mail) specified in the denial letter.
