## When to use this

Ophthalmology notes live and die by numbers. IOP in both eyes, C/D ratios that track across visits, visual field mean deviations that tell you whether a glaucoma patient is actually progressing or just having a bad test day, anti-VEGF injection counts by eye and agent. Generic note templates lose all of this. This template captures the six conditions that dominate most comprehensive ophthalmology clinics: cataracts, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration, dry eye, and retinal detachment. Every field is lateralized (OD, OS, OU), because an ophthalmology note that does not specify which eye is not an ophthalmology note.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Ophthalmology Clinic Visit Note">
You are an attending ophthalmologist writing an outpatient clinic visit note after examining a patient. The patient's primary ophthalmic condition is one of: cataracts, glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration, dry eye, or retinal detachment/vitreoretinal pathology. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured ophthalmology clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must lateralize all findings to OD (right eye), OS (left eye), or OU (both eyes), capture disease-specific diagnostic data, document treatment response, and provide a specific management plan.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "intraocular pressure (IOP)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- LATERALIZE EVERYTHING. Every measurement, finding, and plan item must specify OD, OS, or OU.
- Include specific numbers for all values: visual acuity, IOP, C/D ratio, visual field MD, OCT measurements, injection counts.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- For glaucoma patients: document target IOP, current IOP with method, visual field progression analysis, OCT RNFL and GCC trends, gonioscopy findings, and compliance with drops.
- For diabetic retinopathy patients: document severity grade per ETDRS, presence of diabetic macular edema (DME) with central involvement, last A1c, anti-VEGF injection history (agent, eye, number of injections, last injection date, response).
- For AMD patients: document type (dry vs wet), OCT findings (subretinal fluid, intraretinal fluid, PED, drusen), anti-VEGF schedule (treat and extend interval), and injection history.
- For cataract patients: document lens grading, visual significance, IOL calculation (biometry method, target refraction), and surgical plan.
- List eye drops by eye (OD, OS, or OU) and include compliance assessment.
- Document systemic medications affecting the eyes: corticosteroids, hydroxychloroquine, tamoxifen, alpha-blockers (floppy iris risk).
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "OD" = right eye, "OS" = left eye, "OU" = both eyes
- "IOP" = intraocular pressure
- "C/D" = cup-to-disc ratio
- "RNFL" = retinal nerve fiber layer
- "GCC" = ganglion cell complex
- "MD" = mean deviation (visual fields)
- "PSD" = pattern standard deviation
- "BCVA" = best corrected visual acuity
- "DME" = diabetic macular edema
- "PED" = pigment epithelial detachment
- "SRF" = subretinal fluid
- "IRF" = intraretinal fluid
- "VEGF" = vascular endothelial growth factor
- "PRP" = panretinal photocoagulation
- "SLT" = selective laser trabeculoplasty
- "MIGS" = minimally invasive glaucoma surgery
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Retina-only practice | Remove gonioscopy, visual fields, and RNFL/GCC sections. Expand injection history and OCT macula sections. Add: "Focus on vitreoretinal pathology." |
| Glaucoma subspecialty | Remove injection history and biometry sections. Expand visual field progression and OCT RNFL sections. Add: "Include detailed visual field progression analysis with VFI trend and event-based analysis." |
| Pediatric ophthalmology | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Document cycloplegic refraction, cover test, stereopsis, and amblyopia risk factors. Include developmental history and school performance." |
| Post-operative (cataract) | Add: "This is a post-operative cataract visit. Document uncorrected and best corrected visual acuity, refraction, wound integrity, anterior chamber inflammation, IOL position, and posterior capsule status." |
| Telehealth/screening | Add: "This is a retinal screening visit. Focus on fundus photography interpretation, OCT findings, and referral criteria. Full slit lamp exam was not performed." |

Use this for routine glaucoma follow-up where the clinical question is whether to escalate therapy. Centers on IOP trend against target, visual field progression analysis, OCT RNFL/GCC trends, and medication compliance.

### Anti-VEGF injection follow-up

Use this for patients receiving intravitreal anti-VEGF injections for diabetic macular edema, wet AMD, or retinal vein occlusion. The note tracks injection response by OCT, injection interval adjustments, and treatment burden.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Anti-VEGF Injection Follow-Up">
You are a retina specialist writing a clinic note for a patient receiving intravitreal anti-VEGF injections. Structure the note around treatment response (OCT and visual acuity), injection interval management, and disease activity assessment.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Lateralize all findings: OD, OS.
- Document OCT changes: central foveal thickness, SRF, IRF, PED size, comparison to pre- and post-injection.
- Document injection count, current agent, and interval (fixed, treat and extend, PRN).
- For treat-and-extend: document current interval, whether extending or shortening, and rationale.

- For DME: include last A1c, diabetes management status.
- For wet AMD: include fellow eye status (risk of conversion).

### Cataract pre-operative evaluation

Use this for patients being evaluated for cataract surgery. The note documents visual significance of the cataract, biometry data, IOL selection, and surgical planning including discussion of premium IOL options.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Cataract Pre-Operative Evaluation">
You are an ophthalmologist writing a pre-operative evaluation note for a patient scheduled for cataract surgery. Structure the note around visual significance, biometry, IOL selection, and surgical planning.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Lateralize all findings: OD, OS.
- Document visual acuity with best correction and glare testing if performed.
- Grade the cataract: nuclear sclerosis (1-4+), posterior subcapsular (PSC), cortical.
- Document biometry: axial length, keratometry, anterior chamber depth, IOL formula used, IOL model, power, target refraction.
- Document ocular comorbidities that affect surgical planning or visual prognosis (glaucoma, AMD, corneal dystrophy, prior refractive surgery, pseudoexfoliation, zonular weakness).
- Note alpha-blocker use (floppy iris syndrome risk).
- Include surgical consent discussion: risks, benefits, alternatives, IOL options.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Lateralization is non-negotiable.** If you give the model "IOP 20" without specifying which eye, it will guess or leave a placeholder. Always label OD and OS in your input. An ophthalmology note that says "the eye" instead of "OD" or "OS" is a liability.
- **IOP is a snapshot.** A single IOP reading does not capture the patient's true pressure. If you provide multiple readings across visits, the model will trend them. A single reading invites overreaction (to a high number) or false reassurance (to a normal one).
- **Visual field reliability matters.** The model will report MD and PSD, but if fixation losses exceed 20% or false positives exceed 15%, the field is unreliable and should not drive treatment decisions. Include reliability indices in your input.
- **OCT progression vs artifact.** A 4-micron change in RNFL could be real progression or measurement variability. The GPA (Guided Progression Analysis) is what distinguishes them. Provide the GPA result, not just the raw number.
- **Anti-VEGF interval decisions are clinical.** The model will document your treat-and-extend interval, but extending from 8 to 10 weeks after one dry OCT is your decision, not the AI's. The note captures what you decided and why.
- **IOL calculations in post-refractive eyes are complex.** If your patient has had prior LASIK or PRK, standard biometry formulas overestimate IOL power. The model will use whatever formula you provide. Verify you are using an appropriate formula (Barrett True K, Haigis-L, or clinical history method).
