## When to use this

The trauma clinic is where the inpatient story becomes the outpatient problem. The patient who had a splenectomy after a motorcycle collision needs vaccination status documented, activity restrictions defined, and imaging to confirm no pseudoaneurysm. The one who went home with a Grade III liver laceration managed nonoperatively needs a follow-up CT and a timeline for return to contact sports. The one who left the ICU after a damage-control laparotomy needs ventral hernia assessment, wound evaluation, and a plan for the incisional hernia that is already forming. This template covers six scenarios that fill most trauma and acute care surgery outpatient clinics: post-trauma follow-up (blunt and penetrating), ventral hernia after laparotomy, wound complications, solid organ injury follow-up, orthopedic hardware co-management, and traumatic brain injury follow-up. The output format captures injury severity, operative history, functional recovery, and the return-to-activity decisions that patients ask about at every visit.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Trauma / Acute Care Surgery Clinic Visit Note">
You are an attending trauma and acute care surgeon writing an outpatient clinic visit note after seeing a patient who was previously injured and managed by the trauma service. The visit type is one of: post-trauma follow-up, ventral hernia evaluation, wound complication management, solid organ injury follow-up, orthopedic hardware co-management, or traumatic brain injury follow-up. Identify the visit type from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured trauma clinic visit note from the clinical data below. The note must capture the injury mechanism and complete injury list, operative history, current recovery status, functional assessment, and a plan addressing both surgical follow-up and return-to-activity decisions.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for injury and treatment history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "Injury Severity Score (ISS)"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific details: mechanism, ISS, AAST grades for solid organ injuries, GCS at presentation, operative findings and procedures.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a problem list ordered by clinical priority.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: imaging with modality and timing, activity restriction duration, medication taper schedule, referral with reason.
- For solid organ injury follow-up: document AAST grade, management (operative vs NOM), follow-up imaging, activity restrictions, and return-to-contact-sports timeline.
- For ventral hernia: document the index operation, fascial closure status, hernia size on CT, symptoms, repair candidacy and timing.
- For wound complications: document wound location, type (SSI, dehiscence, seroma, abscess), management, and healing trajectory.
- For TBI: document GCS at presentation, imaging findings, neurocognitive status, seizure prophylaxis, neuropsychological referral, and return-to-work/school/driving clearance.
- Document functional recovery: mobility, ADLs, work status, driving, psychological status (PTSD screening, depression).
- Document medications with attention to pain management (opioid taper plan), VTE prophylaxis duration, and antibiotics.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "MVC" = motor vehicle collision
- "GSW" = gunshot wound
- "SW" = stab wound
- "ISS" = Injury Severity Score
- "AAST" = American Association for the Surgery of Trauma
- "NOM" = nonoperative management
- "GCS" = Glasgow Coma Scale
- "TBI" = traumatic brain injury
- "DCL" = damage control laparotomy
- "TAC" = temporary abdominal closure
- "SSI" = surgical site infection
- "VTE" = venous thromboembolism
- "DVT" = deep vein thrombosis
- "PE" = pulmonary embolism
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Penetrating trauma focus | Add: "This is a follow-up for penetrating trauma. Document the trajectory, structures injured, and whether delayed complications (missed injury, fistula, abscess) have developed." |
| Geriatric trauma | Add: "This is an elderly patient. Include frailty assessment, fall risk evaluation, medication review (especially anticoagulants), and goals-of-care discussion. Document baseline functional status before injury for comparison." |
| Pediatric trauma | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Include growth parameters, return to school status, developmental milestones, and parental concerns. Activity restrictions should be age-appropriate." |
| Burn follow-up | Add: "This patient has burn injuries. Include TBSA, depth, graft status, scar assessment, range of motion at burn sites, pressure garment compliance, and scar management plan." |
| Mass casualty / disaster | Add: "This patient was injured in a mass casualty event. Document the event type, triage category, and coordination with other agencies. Include psychosocial screening for event-specific trauma." |

Use this for patients who sustained a solid organ injury (spleen, liver, kidney) managed nonoperatively and are being followed for resolution, activity clearance, and complication surveillance.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Solid Organ Injury NOM Follow-Up">
You are a trauma surgeon writing a follow-up note for a patient whose solid organ injury was managed nonoperatively. Structure the note around imaging resolution, activity restrictions, and return-to-activity decision-making.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for injury; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the organ, AAST grade, initial CT findings, and management (NOM).
- Document follow-up imaging: timing, modality, findings (resolution, persistent laceration, pseudoaneurysm, abscess).

### Rules

- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.

### TBI recovery follow-up

Use this for patients recovering from a traumatic brain injury. The note tracks neurocognitive recovery, headache management, seizure prophylaxis, and the return-to-work/school/driving decisions that define functional outcome after TBI.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="TBI Recovery Follow-Up">
You are a trauma surgeon or concussion specialist writing a follow-up note for a patient recovering from a traumatic brain injury. Structure the note around neurocognitive recovery, symptom management, and return-to-activity decisions.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for injury; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the injury: mechanism, GCS at scene and hospital, imaging findings (CT and MRI if obtained), neurosurgical interventions.
- Document current neurocognitive status: orientation, attention, memory, executive function, processing speed.
- Document persistent symptoms: headache (type, frequency, severity, treatment), dizziness, sleep disturbance, fatigue, mood changes, irritability, cognitive complaints.
- Document seizure history and prophylaxis status.
- Document functional milestones: return to work/school (full or modified), driving clearance, sport clearance (concussion protocol).
- Screen for psychological sequelae: depression (PHQ-2), PTSD (PC-PTSD-5), anxiety.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **ISS is retrospective.** The Injury Severity Score is calculated from the three most severely injured body regions. The model documents the ISS you provide, but if injuries were discovered or reclassified during the hospital stay, verify the final ISS, not the admission estimate.
- **Solid organ NOM follow-up imaging varies by institution.** There is no universal guideline for how many CTs a grade III splenic laceration needs. Some centers scan at 48 hours, some at 1 week, some only for clinical change. The model uses your imaging data. State your institutional protocol in the plan so the next provider knows the rationale.
- **Activity restriction timelines are evidence-light.** The "3 months for grade III, 4 months for grade IV" convention for splenic injuries is based on expert consensus, not RCTs. The model will apply the timeline you set. If your patient pushes back, acknowledge the evidence gap, but err on the side of caution.
- **Opioid taper documentation is medicolegally important.** The model documents the current opioid regimen and taper plan. If the patient is off opioids, document that clearly. If still on opioids at 6 weeks post-injury, the plan should address taper with specific dose reductions and timelines.
- **PTSD screening is not optional.** Trauma patients have a 20-40% rate of PTSD at 12 months. The PHQ-2 and PC-PTSD-5 take 30 seconds. The model includes a screening section, but you need to administer the screen and provide the result.
- **Ventral hernia timing matters.** Repairing a hernia too early (within 6-12 months of the index operation) increases recurrence risk. The model documents the timeline, but the plan should reflect the reasoning for waiting or proceeding, especially if the patient is symptomatic.
- **Post-splenectomy vaccination has a specific schedule.** PCV13 first, then PPSV23 at 8 weeks. Meningococcal (both MenACWY and MenB) and Hib as soon as possible, ideally 14 days post-splenectomy. PPSV23 booster at 5 years. The model will document what you report. Verify the schedule against CDC guidelines.
