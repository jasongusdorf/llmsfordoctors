## When to use this

Use this template when transferring a patient to a skilled nursing facility, rehab, long-term acute care hospital, or another hospital. The receiving team needs a specific kind of document: concise enough to read quickly, detailed enough to manage the patient safely. This template produces that document, organized around what the next team needs to know right now.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, admission/discharge dates, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Transfer Summary">
You are an excellent physician writing a transfer summary for a patient being transferred to another facility. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a complete, structured transfer summary optimized for the receiving team.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for events that occurred during the hospitalization. Use present tense for the patient's current status and active needs.
- Write in paragraph prose for the hospital course summary. Medication lists and pending items may use structured lists.
- Include specific numbers (lab values, vitals, doses, dates) whenever available.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never spell out what an abbreviation stands for.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- The medication list must be the current, reconciled list at time of transfer, including dose, route, and frequency for every medication.
- Flag recent medication changes (started, stopped, or dose-adjusted within the past 7 days) with the date of change.
- Include all pending results (cultures, pathology, outpatient labs) with expected turnaround time if known.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Verify before transfer]" for missing data.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

### Output format

```
TRANSFER SUMMARY

REASON FOR TRANSFER
───────────────────
[One to two sentences: why the patient is being transferred
and to what type of facility.]

───────────────

on [date] for [***], hospital course notable for [***],
now being transferred to [facility type] for [***].

HOSPITAL COURSE SUMMARY
────────────────────────
[Concise paragraph covering: presenting complaint, key
diagnoses made, significant interventions (procedures,

trajectory. One to two paragraphs maximum.]

ACTIVE PROBLEMS
───────────────
  1. [Problem] - [current status and ongoing management needs]
  2. [Problem] - [current status and ongoing management needs]
  3. [Problem] - [current status and ongoing management needs]

───────────────

  - [Test (date sent)]: Expected [timeframe]
  - [Write "None" if no pending results.]

───────────────────────

  - [Medication, dose, route, frequency]
  - [Medication, dose, route, frequency] *STARTED [date]*
```

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Receiving facility type | Add: "This patient is being transferred to a [SNF / LTAC / rehab / outside hospital]. Emphasize the information most relevant to that setting." |
| Wound care detail | Add: "Include detailed wound care instructions: location, size, stage, dressing type, frequency, and supplies needed." |
| Rehab focus | Add: "Emphasize functional status, therapy recommendations (PT/OT/SLP), weight-bearing status, and cognitive baseline." |
| Psychiatric needs | Add: "Include behavioral management plan, psychiatric medications with titration schedule, and safety precautions." |

### Hospital-to-hospital transfer

Use this for transfers to another acute care hospital, typically for a higher level of care or specialized service not available at the sending facility.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Hospital-to-Hospital Transfer Summary">
You are an excellent physician writing a transfer summary for a patient being transferred to another acute care hospital for a higher level of care or specialized services. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a transfer summary that gives the receiving team everything they need to assume care immediately.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for hospital events; present tense for current status and active management.
- Write in paragraph prose for the hospital course.
- Include specific numbers (lab values, vitals, doses, dates, drip rates) whenever available.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- Emphasize the clinical reason for transfer, what has been done, and what the receiving team needs to do next.
- Include current drips, lines, and monitoring in detail.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "[Verify before transfer]" for missing data.

### Step-down / floor transfer

Use this for internal transfers between units within the same hospital (ICU to step-down, step-down to floor, or floor to floor).

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Internal Unit Transfer Note">

### Rules

- Keep this concise. The receiving team has access to the full chart; focus on what changed and what they need to continue.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Medication reconciliation is the highest-stakes section.** The receiving facility will use this list to enter orders. Every medication needs a dose, route, and frequency. Cross-reference the actual med rec before transfer.
- **Recent changes matter most.** A medication that was started, stopped, or dose-adjusted during the hospitalization needs to be flagged with the date. The receiving provider cannot distinguish new from chronic without this context.
- **Pending results get lost in transfer.** Cultures, pathology, and outpatient labs ordered during the admission may return results after transfer. Include every pending item, who should follow up, and what to do with the result.
- **Functional status specificity.** "Deconditioned" tells the receiving rehab nothing. "Ambulating 50 ft with FWW and min assist, baseline independent with cane" tells them everything.
- **Code status documentation.** If goals of care were discussed during the hospitalization, document the outcome of that conversation, not just the current code status. The receiving facility needs context, not just a checkbox.
- **AI defaults to optimism.** The model may understate ongoing care needs or describe the patient as more independent than they are. Verify functional status against actual PT/OT documentation.
- **Anticoagulation bridging.** Transfer summaries involving anticoagulation bridging are high-risk transitions. Spell out the target INR, when to recheck, and when to stop the bridge. Do not leave this to interpretation.
