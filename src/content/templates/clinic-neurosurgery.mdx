## When to use this

Neurosurgery clinic notes live or die on the neurological exam. A spine note without dermatomal findings is guesswork. A brain tumor note without Karnofsky performance status gives oncology nothing to work with. This template identifies the primary neurosurgical condition and structures the note around the exam findings, imaging details, and functional measures that drive the surgical decision. It handles lumbar stenosis and disc herniation, brain tumors, cervical myelopathy, hydrocephalus, trigeminal neuralgia, and Chiari malformation: the conditions that fill most neurosurgery outpatient schedules.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Neurosurgery Clinic Visit Note">
You are an outpatient neurosurgeon writing a clinic visit note after seeing a patient. The patient's primary neurosurgical condition is one of: lumbar stenosis or disc herniation, brain tumor, cervical myelopathy, hydrocephalus, trigeminal neuralgia, or Chiari malformation. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient neurosurgery clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture a thorough neurological examination, disease-specific imaging interpretation, functional status assessment, and a concrete surgical or surveillance plan.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and prior events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use (e.g., "modified Japanese Orthopedic Association (mJOA) score"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers: MRC strength grades, reflex grading, tumor measurements, stenosis severity.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a problem list with the primary neurosurgical condition first.
- Plan items must be specific: procedure name, approach, levels, implant type, imaging interval, medication changes. Never write "surgical intervention may be warranted" without specifying what surgery.
- For spine: clearly distinguish radiculopathy from myelopathy. Document exam by nerve root level. State whether imaging correlates with the clinical syndrome.
- For brain tumor: document tumor type, grade, molecular markers, prior treatments, current size relative to prior imaging, and Karnofsky performance status (KPS).
- For cervical myelopathy: document mJOA score, Hoffman sign, clonus, gait assessment, hand dexterity (grip-release test), and cord signal change on MRI.
- For hydrocephalus: document shunt type, valve setting, symptoms of malfunction (headache pattern, gait change, cognitive decline, urinary incontinence), and last imaging comparison.
- For trigeminal neuralgia: document Burchiel classification, trigger zones, pain distribution (V1/V2/V3), medication trials with doses and response.
- For Chiari: document tonsillar descent measurement, syrinx status, cine MRI flow study results, and symptom correlation.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "MRC" = Medical Research Council (strength grading)
- "mJOA" = modified Japanese Orthopedic Association
- "KPS" = Karnofsky performance status
- "ODI" = Oswestry Disability Index
- "ACDF" = anterior cervical discectomy and fusion
- "SLR" = straight leg raise
- "EHL" = extensor hallucis longus
- "CSF" = cerebrospinal fluid
- "VP" = ventriculoperitoneal
- "MVD" = microvascular decompression
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Pediatric neurosurgery | Add: "This is a pediatric patient. Include developmental milestones, head circumference percentile, fontanelle status (if applicable), and age-appropriate neurological exam." |
| Functional neurosurgery | Add: "This patient is being evaluated for functional neurosurgery (DBS, epilepsy surgery, spasticity). Document the specific functional indication, response to medical therapy, and relevant electrophysiology or monitoring data." |
| Neuro-oncology co-management | Add: "This is a joint neuro-oncology visit. Include tumor board recommendations, chemotherapy cycle and response, radiation treatment summary, and RANO criteria for response assessment." |
| Peripheral nerve | Add: "This patient has a peripheral nerve condition. Document the specific nerve, electrodiagnostic findings (EMG/NCS with specific values), Tinel sign location, and Seddon/Sunderland classification." |
| Second opinion | Add: "This is a second opinion consultation. Review the outside provider's recommendation, your independent assessment of the imaging and clinical findings, and your agreement or disagreement with rationale." |

### Spine surgery consultation (lumbar stenosis)

Use this when a patient with lumbar stenosis or disc herniation has been referred for neurosurgical evaluation. The note is structured around the clinical-imaging correlation that determines whether surgery will help, and which surgery to offer.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Lumbar Spine Surgical Consultation">
You are an outpatient neurosurgeon writing a consultation note for a patient referred with lumbar stenosis or disc herniation. Structure the note around neurological assessment, clinical-imaging correlation, failed conservative treatment, and surgical recommendation.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Distinguish radiculopathy (single root, dermatomal) from neurogenic claudication (bilateral, positional, distance-limited) from cauda equina (emergency).
- Document neurological exam by root level: L2 through S1.
- State explicitly whether the imaging findings correlate with the clinical syndrome. If they do not, flag this.
- Document all failed conservative treatments with dates, duration, and response.
- Surgical recommendation must specify: decompression alone vs decompression with fusion, levels, approach (posterior/lateral/anterior), and rationale.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.

### Shunt follow-up

Use this for the hydrocephalus patient returning for shunt evaluation. The note is structured around symptoms of shunt malfunction, imaging comparison for ventricular size, and the valve adjustment or surgical decision.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Shunt Follow-Up Visit">
You are an outpatient neurosurgeon writing a clinic note for a patient with a cerebrospinal fluid shunt returning for follow-up. Structure the note around symptoms of shunt malfunction, imaging comparison, and shunt management.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document shunt type, valve manufacturer and model, current setting, implant date, revision history.
- Screen for malfunction symptoms: headache (positional character), gait change, cognitive decline, urinary incontinence, visual changes.
- Screen for over-drainage: postural headache (worse upright, better supine), subdural collections.
- Compare current imaging to baseline (last known good scan): ventricular size, catheter position, subdural collections.
- Document shunt series X-ray if obtained: catheter continuity, kinks, disconnections, valve position.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **mJOA score is the currency of cervical myelopathy.** A score of 18 is normal; below 12 is severe. The model calculates it from the component scores you provide. If you give a total without components, it uses the total but cannot identify which domains are most affected. Always provide the breakdown.
- **Cord signal change on MRI changes the prognosis.** T2 hyperintensity in the spinal cord indicates myelomalacia and predicts less complete recovery after surgery. The model uses this finding to temper outcome expectations in the plan. Make sure you specify whether signal change is present.
- **Brain tumor molecular markers drive everything.** An IDH-mutant, 1p/19q-codeleted oligodendroglioma has a fundamentally different treatment algorithm and prognosis than an IDH-wildtype glioblastoma. The model uses molecular data to frame the surveillance plan and expected trajectory. If you paste pathology without molecular markers, the note will be incomplete.
- **Shunt valve settings must be verified after MRI.** Programmable valves can be reset by MRI magnetic fields. The model includes this reminder in the plan, but it is your responsibility to verify the setting post-MRI. If you adjust the valve in clinic, provide the old and new setting so the model documents the change and rationale.
- **Level-by-level spine imaging matters.** "Multilevel stenosis" is not useful. The model needs to know which levels are stenotic, how severely, and whether there is cord or root compression at each level to generate a surgical plan that addresses the right levels.
- **Trigeminal neuralgia medication doses determine surgical candidacy.** If the patient has not tried carbamazepine at a therapeutic dose (800-1200 mg/day), most neurosurgeons would not offer microvascular decompression. Provide the maximum dose tried and the reason it was stopped.
- **Hoffman sign is bilateral until proven otherwise.** Unilateral Hoffman may be a normal variant. Bilateral Hoffman in the context of hand clumsiness and gait disturbance is a myelopathic sign. The model documents laterality, so specify which side(s) are positive.
