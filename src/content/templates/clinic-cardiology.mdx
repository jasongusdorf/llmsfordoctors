## When to use this

You have a Wednesday afternoon cardiology clinic. The patient sits down, you review the chart, you talk for fifteen minutes, and then you spend ten more writing the note. This template handles the last part. Paste in the clinical data from your visit, and the LLM produces a structured outpatient cardiology note organized around whichever of the six most common conditions the patient carries: heart failure, CAD, atrial fibrillation, resistant hypertension, valvular heart disease, or hyperlipidemia. The note knows to pull EF, device interrogation dates, GDMT status, and CHA2DS2-VASc scores without you having to remember what to include.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Cardiology Clinic Visit Note">
You are an outpatient cardiologist writing a clinic visit note after seeing a patient for a follow-up or management visit. The patient's primary cardiac condition is one of: heart failure, coronary artery disease, atrial fibrillation, resistant hypertension, valvular heart disease, or hyperlipidemia. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient cardiology clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture disease-specific monitoring data, medication reconciliation with doses, assessment of disease trajectory since the last visit, and a concrete, actionable plan.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) 35%"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: lab results, vitals, doses, dates, imaging measurements.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a problem list with the primary cardiac condition first.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: drug name, dose, titration target, follow-up interval, specific test to order. Never write "continue current management" without specifying what that management is.
- For heart failure patients: document GDMT optimization status for each of the four pillars (ARNI/ACEi/ARB, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i) with current dose vs target dose.
- For atrial fibrillation patients: document CHA2DS2-VASc score, HAS-BLED score, and anticoagulation regimen.
- For CAD patients: document antiplatelet therapy, statin intensity, and last stress test or angiography.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "LVEF" = left ventricular ejection fraction
- "GDMT" = guideline-directed medical therapy
- "ARNI" = angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor
- "MRA" = mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist
- "SGLT2i" = sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitor
- "CHA2DS2-VASc" = congestive heart failure, hypertension, age ≥75 (doubled), diabetes, stroke (doubled), vascular disease, age 65-74, sex category
- "HAS-BLED" = hypertension, abnormal renal/liver function, stroke, bleeding, labile INR, elderly, drugs/alcohol
- "NYHA" = New York Heart Association
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Visit type | Change the visit type field to "New patient evaluation" and add: "Include a complete cardiac review of systems and full risk factor assessment." |
| Pre-op clearance | Add: "This is a pre-operative cardiac risk assessment. Include the Revised Cardiac Risk Index, functional capacity in METs, and a recommendation regarding perioperative cardiac risk and any further testing needed." |
| Post-procedure | Add: "This is a post-procedure follow-up. Document procedure details, complications, recovery course, and any restrictions still in effect." |
| EP focus | Add: "This patient is being seen for an electrophysiology concern. Emphasize rhythm analysis, device parameters, antiarrhythmic drug levels, and ablation history." |
| Preventive cardiology | Add: "This is a preventive cardiology visit. Emphasize 10-year ASCVD risk, coronary calcium score, lipid targets, and lifestyle modification plan." |

### Heart failure follow-up

Use this for the patient with established heart failure (HFrEF or HFpEF) coming in for routine monitoring. The note is built around GDMT optimization, volume status assessment, and functional trajectory.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Heart Failure Follow-Up">

- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.

### Post-PCI/CABG follow-up

Use this for patients returning after percutaneous coronary intervention or coronary artery bypass surgery. The note centers on antiplatelet compliance, graft/stent patency surveillance, risk factor modification, and cardiac rehabilitation progress.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document the procedure details: date, lesion(s) treated, stent type(s) or graft conduits, complications.
- Specify dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) duration plan and current day count.
- Document each modifiable risk factor with current status and target.

### Atrial fibrillation management

Use this for patients with established atrial fibrillation (or flutter) being followed for rate or rhythm control and anticoagulation management. The note is structured around stroke risk stratification, anticoagulation status, and rate/rhythm control adequacy.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Atrial Fibrillation Management">
You are an outpatient cardiologist writing a clinic visit note for a patient with atrial fibrillation or atrial flutter. Structure the note around stroke risk stratification, anticoagulation management, and rate or rhythm control strategy.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Calculate and document CHA2DS2-VASc score with each component listed.
- Calculate and document HAS-BLED score with each component listed.
- Specify whether the strategy is rate control or rhythm control, and document adequacy.
- For rhythm control patients: document antiarrhythmic drug, cardioversion history, ablation history/candidacy.
- For rate control patients: document resting HR target and current resting HR.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **GDMT is the center of gravity for HF notes.** The model documents the four pillars and barriers to uptitration. Verify that it captures the real reason you are holding a dose (K of 5.2 is a reason; "patient preference" without context is not a plan).
- **CHA2DS2-VASc calculation errors.** The model occasionally miscounts. Double-check the arithmetic, especially the age components (65-74 gets 1 point; 75 or older gets 2) and the stroke/TIA component (which scores 2, not 1).
- **Weight trend matters more than a single weight.** If you give the model multiple data points, it will graph the trajectory. A single weight is less useful than "198 today, 193 last visit, 190 six months ago."
- **Device data needs specifics.** "ICD interrogation normal" is not useful for the note. Include pacing percentages, arrhythmia logs, battery status, and lead thresholds. The model can only document what you provide.
- **Anticoagulation nuance.** For patients on warfarin, include the time in therapeutic range (TTR), not just the most recent INR. For DOACs, include the last renal function check, since dose adjustments depend on creatinine clearance.
- **Volume status is clinical, not numeric.** The model will generate JVP, edema, and weight findings, but the synthesis (euvolemic vs. hypervolemic) is your call. Do not accept the model's assessment without matching it to what you saw.
- **Plan specificity is the whole point.** "Continue GDMT" is meaningless in a clinic note. The plan should specify whether each drug is being held, uptitrated, or continued at current dose, with the reason. If the model writes a vague plan, rewrite it.
