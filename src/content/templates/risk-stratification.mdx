## When to use this

Use this template when you need to apply one or more validated clinical scoring tools to a patient presentation to support risk stratification, disposition decisions, or treatment thresholds.

Applies validated clinical scoring tools to a patient presentation to support risk stratification and disposition decisions. Provide the clinical data and specify which scoring tools to apply.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="evidence">
  Risk scores are decision-support tools, not decision-making tools. They are validated at the population level and may not apply to every individual patient. Clinical judgment always supersedes a calculated score.
</Callout>

If multiple scores are calculated, compare the risk stratifications and note any discordance. Recommend which score is most applicable to this clinical scenario and why.

Do not fabricate score criteria, cutoff values, or validation data. Use the published scoring criteria. If a required data point for a score is missing, note it and calculate the range of possible scores.

## Example output

> **Prompt used:** Clinical Risk Stratification for a patient with chest pain.
>
> **Clinical data provided:** 52M presenting with 4 hours of substernal chest pain, dull, non-radiating, onset at rest. PMH: HTN, hyperlipidemia, former smoker (quit 5 years ago). Vitals: HR 88, BP 142/88, RR 16, O2 99% RA, Temp 36.8C. Labs: troponin I 0.04 ng/mL (ULN 0.04), creatinine 1.0. ECG: no ST changes, normal sinus rhythm. Scores to calculate: HEART score, TIMI risk score.

**HEART Score**

| Component | Finding | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **H**istory | Moderately suspicious (chest pain at rest, dull, non-radiating, no classic radiation or diaphoresis) | 1 |
| **E**CG | Normal | 0 |
| **A**ge | 52 years (45–64) | 1 |
| **R**isk factors | HTN, hyperlipidemia, former smoker (3 or more risk factors) | 2 |
| **T**roponin | At the upper limit of normal (0.04, equals ULN) | 1 |

- **Total Score:** 5 points
- **Risk Category:** Moderate risk
- **Interpretation:** A HEART score of 5 places this patient in the moderate-risk category (score 4–6), with an estimated 30-day MACE rate of approximately 12–17%. Guidelines suggest observation, serial troponins, and further risk stratification (stress testing or coronary CTA) rather than immediate discharge.
- **Caveat:** The HEART score was validated primarily in ED chest pain populations. Troponin assay cutoffs vary by institution — confirm that 0.04 is your lab's 99th percentile.

**TIMI Risk Score for UA/NSTEMI**

| Component | Finding | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Age ≥65 | No (52 years) | 0 |
| ≥3 CAD risk factors | Yes (HTN, hyperlipidemia, former smoker) | 1 |
| Known CAD (≥50% stenosis) | No known CAD | 0 |
| ASA use in past 7 days | Not stated | 0 |
| ≥2 anginal episodes in 24h | Not stated (only 1 episode described) | 0 |
| ST deviation ≥0.5mm | No | 0 |
| Positive cardiac marker | Borderline (troponin at ULN, not clearly positive) | 0 |

- **Total Score:** 1 point
- **Risk Category:** Low risk
- **Interpretation:** A TIMI score of 1 suggests a low risk of 14-day all-cause mortality, new or recurrent MI, or urgent revascularization (approximately 5%). This would suggest a less aggressive strategy may be appropriate.
- **Caveat:** The TIMI score was derived from clinical trial populations with confirmed ACS, not undifferentiated ED chest pain. It may underperform in the initial ED evaluation compared to the HEART score.

**Comparison & Synthesis**
There is discordance between the two scores: the HEART score places this patient at moderate risk (score 5), while the TIMI score suggests low risk (score 1). This discordance is expected — the HEART score was designed for undifferentiated ED chest pain and captures this patient's risk factor burden and borderline troponin more effectively. In this clinical scenario, the HEART score is more applicable, and the moderate-risk classification should guide management: serial troponins at 3 and 6 hours, observation, and further risk stratification with stress testing or coronary CTA before discharge.

## Tips & pitfalls

- Common scoring tools this works well with: HEART, Wells (PE and DVT), CHA₂DS₂-VASc, HAS-BLED, CURB-65, qSOFA, NEWS2, MELD, Child-Pugh, PERC, Glasgow-Blatchford, ABCD², CRB-65, APACHE II.
- If you're unsure which score to use, paste the clinical scenario and ask: "What validated risk scores apply to this presentation?"
- For serial assessments, paste both time points and ask for trend comparison.
- **AI models can confabulate score criteria and cutoff values.** Always verify the calculated score against a trusted source like MDCalc. A single incorrect criterion point can shift the risk category.
- **Risk scores do not replace clinical judgment.** A patient can have a low risk score and still have a life-threatening diagnosis. Use scores as one input, not the sole decision driver.
- **Be aware of score validation populations.** Many scores were validated in specific populations (e.g., HEART in ED chest pain, CURB-65 in community-acquired pneumonia). Applying a score outside its validated population may give misleading results.
- **AI may not account for patient-specific factors that override a score** (e.g., a low HEART score in a patient with known severe CAD and prior stent thrombosis). Integrate the score with the full clinical picture.
