## When to use this

Endocrinology clinic generates notes dense with numbers: A1c trends, CGM time-in-range, insulin dose calculations, TSH trajectories, DEXA T-scores, cortisol levels. This template covers the six conditions that fill most endocrine schedules: complex type 2 diabetes, thyroid disorders, osteoporosis, adrenal insufficiency, PCOS, and pituitary disorders. Paste in clinical data, and the LLM produces a structured note that already knows to document CGM metrics, complication screening dates, FRAX scores, replacement dose timing, or pituitary hormone panels depending on the diagnosis. The disease-specific data fields are built into the output so you can focus on the clinical decisions.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Endocrinology Clinic Visit Note">
You are an outpatient endocrinologist writing a clinic visit note after seeing a patient for a follow-up or management visit. The patient's primary endocrine condition is one of: complex type 2 diabetes, thyroid disorders, osteoporosis, adrenal insufficiency, PCOS, or pituitary disorders. Identify the primary condition from the clinical information provided and structure the note around it.

### Purpose

Generate a complete, structured outpatient endocrinology clinic note from the clinical data below. The note must capture disease-specific monitoring data (glycemic metrics, hormone levels, imaging, bone density), medication reconciliation with doses, assessment of disease trajectory, and a concrete plan with specific next steps.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history and interval events; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with expansion on first use in the note (e.g., "continuous glucose monitor (CGM) time in range 62%"), then abbreviation only thereafter.
- Include specific numbers for all values: lab results, hormone levels, doses, imaging measurements, CGM metrics.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY (e.g., 3/15/26).
- Organize the Assessment as a problem list with the primary endocrine condition first.
- Plan items must be specific and actionable: insulin dose adjustments with exact units, medication changes with dose, specific labs to order, imaging to schedule. Never write "continue current management" without specifying what that management is.
- For diabetes patients: document A1c trend, CGM data (time in range, GMI, average glucose, time below range, coefficient of variation), insulin regimen with specific doses (basal units, bolus ratios, correction factor), and complication screening dates.
- For thyroid patients: document TSH and free T4 trends, antibody status, nodule ultrasound history, and levothyroxine dose with timing instructions.
- For osteoporosis patients: document DEXA T-scores with sites and dates, FRAX score, fracture history, and treatment duration with drug holiday consideration.
- For adrenal insufficiency patients: document replacement regimen with timing, sick day rules, and medical alert status.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "CGM" = continuous glucose monitor
- "TIR" = time in range (70-180 mg/dL)
- "GMI" = glucose management indicator
- "TBR" = time below range (&lt;70 mg/dL)
- "TAR" = time above range (&gt;180 mg/dL)
- "CV" = coefficient of variation
- "ICR" = insulin-to-carb ratio
- "ISF" = insulin sensitivity factor (correction factor)
- "DEXA" = dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry
- "FRAX" = Fracture Risk Assessment Tool
- Use all abbreviations provided in the input without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Type 1 diabetes | Add: "This patient has type 1 diabetes. Emphasize insulin management (MDI or pump), CGM data interpretation, hypoglycemia prevention, DKA risk assessment, and autoimmune comorbidity screening (celiac, thyroid)." |
| Gestational diabetes | Add: "This patient has gestational diabetes. Document fasting and postprandial glucose targets, dietary compliance, insulin requirements, fetal growth assessment, and delivery planning." |
| Thyroid cancer surveillance | Add: "This patient has a history of differentiated thyroid cancer. Document TNM staging, RAI history, thyroglobulin trend (stimulated and unstimulated), TgAb, TSH suppression target, and neck ultrasound surveillance." |
| Transgender hormone therapy | Add: "This patient is on gender-affirming hormone therapy. Document hormone levels, target ranges, screening protocols, and relevant safety monitoring (hematocrit, lipids, liver function)." |
| Hyperparathyroidism | Add: "This patient has primary hyperparathyroidism. Document calcium, PTH, 24-hour urine calcium, vitamin D, DEXA, renal imaging for stones, and surgical candidacy assessment per guidelines." |

### Complex diabetes management

Use this for the patient with type 2 diabetes on intensive insulin therapy (MDI or pump), often with CGM data, who needs detailed glycemic pattern analysis and regimen adjustment. This is the note for the patient whose diabetes is too complex for primary care.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Complex Diabetes Management">

Structure the note around glycemic data interpretation, insulin dose optimization, complication screening, and cardiovascular risk reduction.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document A1c with trend (at least 2 values).
- If CGM data available: document all standard CGM metrics (TIR, TAR, TBR, GMI, average glucose, CV, sensor wear time).
- Identify specific glycemic patterns: overnight lows/highs, postprandial spikes (specify meals), dawn phenomenon, exercise-related changes.
- Document insulin regimen with exact doses. For pump users: basal rates by time period, ICR, ISF, and any recent auto-mode usage.
- Document all diabetes complication screening dates and results.
- Plan must include specific dose changes (exact units or ratios), not vague instructions like "adjust insulin."
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Thyroid nodule/cancer follow-up

Use this for patients being followed for thyroid nodules, post-thyroidectomy for cancer, or on surveillance after differentiated thyroid cancer treatment. The note is organized around nodule characterization, cancer staging, thyroglobulin surveillance, and TSH management.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Thyroid Nodule/Cancer Follow-Up">
You are an outpatient endocrinologist writing a clinic visit note for a patient being followed for thyroid nodules or thyroid cancer. Structure the note around nodule characterization (TI-RADS, FNA results), cancer staging and treatment history, thyroglobulin surveillance, and TSH target management.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- For nodules: document each nodule with size, TI-RADS score, and FNA result (Bethesda category) if performed.
- For thyroid cancer: document histology, staging (AJCC TNM), initial treatment (surgery, RAI dose and date), and risk stratification (ATA low/intermediate/high).
- Track thyroglobulin (Tg) and thyroglobulin antibodies (TgAb) trends; document whether Tg is measured on levothyroxine suppression or after stimulation.
- Document TSH target based on risk category and specify whether currently at target.
- For surveillance: document neck ultrasound findings with comparison to prior.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

### Osteoporosis treatment monitoring

Use this for patients with osteoporosis on pharmacologic therapy who need treatment response assessment, drug holiday consideration, and fracture risk reassessment. The note centers on DEXA trends, FRAX recalculation, and treatment duration management.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Osteoporosis Treatment Monitoring">
You are an outpatient endocrinologist writing a clinic visit note for a patient with osteoporosis on pharmacologic treatment. Structure the note around DEXA trends, fracture risk reassessment, treatment response, and drug holiday decision-making.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for history; present tense for current status and plan.
- Use medical abbreviations with first-use expansion.
- Document DEXA T-scores at lumbar spine, total hip, and femoral neck with dates for trend comparison.
- Calculate or document FRAX score (10-year probability of major osteoporotic fracture and hip fracture).
- Document fracture history with type, date, and mechanism (fragility vs traumatic).
- Track treatment duration and assess for drug holiday eligibility (bisphosphonates: consider after 3-5 years oral or 3 years IV if low-moderate risk; denosumab: no drug holiday, requires transition plan).
- Document fall risk assessment and prevention measures.
- Include secondary osteoporosis workup results if applicable.
- Do not fabricate clinical data. Write "[Not provided; verify before signing]" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- **CGM data is the new physical exam for diabetes.** The model can document TIR, TAR, TBR, GMI, and CV, but pattern identification (which meal is the problem, whether the dawn phenomenon is present, whether lows cluster at a specific time) requires you to review the actual AGP report. Give the model specific pattern observations, not just summary metrics.
- **Insulin dose changes should be small and targeted.** The model sometimes makes sweeping changes (increase all basal rates by 20%). Good practice: change one variable at a time, by 10-20%, and reassess in 1-2 weeks. If the model proposes multiple simultaneous changes, consider whether you can sequence them.
- **A1c and GMI discordance is real.** If A1c is 8.0% but GMI is 7.2%, the discrepancy may reflect red cell turnover differences, hemoglobin variants, or anemia. The model documents both but may not flag the discordance. If they diverge by more than 0.5%, note it.
- **Denosumab discontinuation is dangerous.** Unlike bisphosphonates, denosumab cannot be stopped without a transition plan. Rebound bone loss and vertebral fractures occur within months. The model knows this, but verify that any plan to stop denosumab includes a bisphosphonate bridge.
- **Thyroglobulin interpretation depends on TgAb.** If thyroglobulin antibodies are positive, the thyroglobulin level is unreliable (can be falsely low). The model documents both values but may not explicitly flag this interference. Track TgAb trends as a surrogate in antibody-positive patients.
- **Adrenal crisis prevention is the safety item.** For any patient with adrenal insufficiency, the note should confirm sick day rules were reviewed, emergency injection is available and not expired, and the patient has a medical alert bracelet. The model includes these in the template, but if you do not provide the data, it cannot fill them in. Ask about these at every visit.
- **FRAX has known limitations.** It does not account for fall risk, does not adjust for dose-dependent steroid exposure, and underestimates risk in patients with recent fractures. The model will document the FRAX score, but clinical judgment may override it. If you disagree with the FRAX-based recommendation, document why.
