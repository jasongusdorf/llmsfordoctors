## When to use this

Use this template when writing an outpatient general neurology clinic note after seeing a patient for a new or follow-up visit. Covers the six conditions that fill most general neurology schedules: migraine and headache disorders, epilepsy, peripheral neuropathy, stroke follow-up, multiple sclerosis, and Parkinson's disease. Paste in your clinical data (history, neurological exam, imaging, electrodiagnostics, medications, and functional status) and the LLM produces a structured clinic note with a disease-specific assessment and plan.

For subspecialty visits where the disease-specific data collection runs deeper (headache diaries, seizure logs, motor fluctuation timing, EMG patterns), use the dedicated headache, epilepsy, movement disorders, or neuromuscular templates instead.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="General Neurology Clinic Visit">
You are a board-certified neurologist writing an outpatient clinic note after evaluating a patient. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a complete, structured neurology clinic visit note.

**Purpose:** Generate a comprehensive general neurology outpatient visit note that captures the full neurological examination, relevant diagnostics, disease-specific assessment, and a plan organized by neurological problem.

### Rules

- Write the HPI as a narrative paragraph in past tense for history, present tense for current symptoms.
- Document the neurological exam in full, organized by domain: mental status, cranial nerves, motor (strength by muscle group, tone), sensory (light touch, pinprick, vibration, proprioception), reflexes (with numeric grading 0-4), coordination (finger-nose, heel-shin), and gait (tandem, Romberg).
- Include symptom onset, time course (acute, subacute, chronic), progression pattern (progressive, relapsing-remitting, static), laterality, and timing.
- For each problem in the assessment, write a brief clinical rationale before the plan.
- Reference imaging (MRI brain/spine, CT/CTA) with dates and key findings.
- Reference electrodiagnostics (EEG, EMG/NCS) with dates and findings when available.
- List current neurologic medications with doses. Include drug levels if applicable (e.g., antiseizure medication levels).
- Document functional impact: work status, driving status, ADL independence.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never spell out what an abbreviation stands for.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY.
- Do not infer or fabricate any clinical information not provided. Write "***" for missing data.
- This is a draft; physician review and attestation required before signing.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- "LUE/RUE/LLE/RLE" = left/right upper/lower extremity
- "DTR" = deep tendon reflexes
- "F-N-F" = finger-nose-finger
- "H-S" = heel-shin
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| New patient depth | Add: "This is a new patient evaluation. Include a comprehensive past neurologic history and detailed review of outside records." |
| Follow-up brevity | Add: "This is a routine follow-up. Keep the HPI focused on interval changes since the last visit. Abbreviated exam is acceptable." |
| Cognitive screening | Add: "Include MoCA or MMSE score with subscores in the mental status section." |
| Telehealth | Add: "This is a telehealth visit. Document limitations of the remote neurological exam. Note which exam components could not be assessed." |
| Billing optimization | Add: "Document MDM complexity to support a [level 4 / level 5] E/M code. Include data reviewed, complexity of the problem, and risk of management." |

### Neuropathy workup visit

Use this for patients presenting with numbness, tingling, or weakness where the primary task is evaluating and classifying a peripheral neuropathy. The output format captures the workup checklist and EMG/NCS correlation.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Neuropathy Workup Visit">
You are a neurologist writing a clinic note for a patient being evaluated for peripheral neuropathy. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a structured neuropathy evaluation note.

### Rules

- Classify the neuropathy by pattern: length-dependent vs non-length-dependent, axonal vs demyelinating, sensory vs motor vs sensorimotor.
- Document the sensory exam with specific attention to modalities and distribution (stocking-glove, patchy, dermatomal).
- Reference EMG/NCS findings and correlate them with the clinical picture.
- Include the neuropathy workup labs ordered or reviewed: glucose/A1c, B12, TSH, SPEP/UPEP, ANA, ESR/CRP, HIV, hepatitis panel, methylmalonic acid. Note which have returned and which are pending.
- Document functional impact: balance, falls, foot drop, hand dexterity.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"

### Stroke prevention follow-up

Use this for outpatient follow-up of patients with a history of ischemic stroke or TIA. The output format captures secondary prevention metrics, vascular risk factor management, and functional recovery.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Stroke Prevention Follow-Up">
You are a neurologist writing an outpatient follow-up note for a patient with a history of ischemic stroke or TIA. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a structured stroke follow-up note focused on secondary prevention.

### Rules

- Document the index event: type (ischemic stroke, TIA, hemorrhagic), date, vascular territory, mechanism (TOAST classification: large artery atherosclerosis, cardioembolism, small vessel, other, cryptogenic).
- Capture secondary prevention metrics: antithrombotic therapy, statin with LDL target, BP goal, A1c if diabetic, smoking cessation status.
- Document functional recovery: mRS score, residual deficits, rehabilitation status.
- Include follow-up imaging (carotid ultrasound, MRA, CTA, echocardiogram) with dates and findings.
- Document driving status and return-to-work status.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Vocabulary

- Use "patient" instead of "pt"
- "mRS" = modified Rankin Scale
- "TOAST" = Trial of Org 10172 in Acute Stroke Treatment (classification)
- "CEA" = carotid endarterectomy
- "CAS" = carotid artery stenting
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## Tips & pitfalls

- **The neurological exam is the billing backbone.** A complete, structured neuro exam supports higher E/M complexity. The AI will fill in "***" for exam components you did not provide. Replace these with actual findings or remove them; do not sign a note with unfilled placeholders.
- **EMG/NCS correlation matters.** The model will report EDx findings as you provide them but may not critique them. If the clinical picture and EDx pattern do not match (e.g., demyelinating NCS with a purely axonal exam), flag that discrepancy yourself.
- **Drug levels need context.** For antiseizure medications, the AI will list levels if you provide them, but it will not interpret a level as subtherapeutic or supratherapeutic without explicit reference ranges. Include the therapeutic range in your input.
- **Driving status is medicolegal.** The model includes a driving status field, but it will not apply your state's seizure-free interval or other reporting requirements. Verify this independently.
- **Functional status is not decoration.** Documenting work status, ADL independence, and driving eligibility supports disability evaluations, prior authorizations, and longitudinal tracking. Do not skip this section.
- **The model defaults to common diagnoses.** If your patient has an atypical presentation (inflammatory neuropathy, paraneoplastic syndrome, genetic condition), you may need to steer the assessment explicitly. The AI will not generate a rare diagnosis on its own from ambiguous data.
