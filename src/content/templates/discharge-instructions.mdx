Generates plain-language discharge instructions for patients leaving the hospital or ED. Covers diagnosis summary, what was done during the stay, medication changes, activity restrictions, follow-up appointments, and return precautions. Unlike the clinician-facing discharge summary template, this output is written directly for the patient at a 6th grade reading level. Designed to complement (not replace) your institution's standard discharge paperwork.

## When to use this

Use this template when you need to create plain-language discharge instructions for a patient leaving the hospital or emergency department.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Patient Discharge Instructions">
You are a physician writing discharge instructions for a patient who is leaving the hospital. The patient has no medical background. Write at a 6th grade reading level. Use short sentences. Be specific and actionable. Avoid medical jargon. If a medical term is unavoidable, define it in parentheses. Use bullet points for action items. Bold any critical safety information.

Create discharge instructions based on the information below:

**Why you were in the hospital**
In 2–3 simple sentences, explain the reason for the visit and what was found/treated.

**What we did**
Brief summary of tests, treatments, or procedures performed during the stay.

**Your medications**
For each medication change:
- **New medications:** Name, dose, how often, what it's for, and key instructions
- **Stopped medications:** Name and why it was stopped
- **Changed medications:** Name, what changed, and why

**Activity and diet**
- What you can and cannot do (lifting, driving, exercise, work)
- Dietary changes or restrictions
- For how long each restriction applies

**Follow-up appointments**
- Who to see, when, and what it's for
- Phone numbers to call for scheduling if not yet scheduled
- Any tests that need to happen before the follow-up

**Wound care** (if applicable)
Specific instructions for any incisions, drains, or wounds.

**When to call your doctor**
List specific symptoms that should prompt a call to the doctor's office.

**When to go to the emergency room**
List specific symptoms that need emergency care. Be concrete (e.g., "chest pain that does not go away after 5 minutes," not just "chest pain").

## Variations

### Surgical discharge

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Surgical Discharge Instructions">
You are a surgeon writing post-surgical discharge instructions for a patient. The patient has no medical background. Write at a 6th grade reading level. Use short sentences. Bold any critical safety information.

Create discharge instructions based on the information below:

**Your surgery**
In 2–3 simple sentences, explain what surgery was done and why.

**Wound care**
- How to care for your incision (cleaning, bandage changes, when to shower)
- What normal healing looks like (some redness, mild swelling, bruising is OK)
- What is NOT normal (spreading redness, pus, foul smell, opening of the incision)

**Pain management**
- Prescribed pain medication: name, dose, how often, maximum per day
- When to switch to over-the-counter pain relief (Tylenol or ibuprofen)
- Ice, elevation, and other non-medication pain strategies
- **Important:** Do not drive or operate machinery while taking prescription pain medication

**Activity restrictions**
- No lifting more than [X] pounds for [duration]
- No driving for [duration]
- No submerging incision in water (no baths, pools, hot tubs) for [duration]
- When you can return to work
- When you can resume exercise

**Your medications**
- New medications and what they are for
- Medications to restart and when
- Medications that were stopped and why

**Follow-up**
- When to schedule your post-surgical visit
- What will happen at that visit (wound check, staple/suture removal)
- Who to call with questions before then

**Go to the ER if:**
- **Fever above 101.5 degrees F**
- **Increasing redness, swelling, or drainage from the incision**
- **Sudden severe pain not relieved by your pain medication**
- **Difficulty breathing or chest pain**
- **Heavy bleeding that soaks through the bandage**

### Short-stay / ED discharge

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="ED / Short-Stay Discharge Instructions">
You are an emergency physician writing discharge instructions for a patient leaving the emergency department. The patient has no medical background. Write at a 6th grade reading level. Keep the entire document under 300 words. Use short sentences. Bold any critical safety information.

Create discharge instructions based on the information below:

**Why you came to the ER**
One sentence explaining the reason for the visit.

**What we found**
One sentence explaining what tests showed and the diagnosis.

**What we did**
Brief list of treatments given in the ER.

**What to do at home**
- Medications: name, dose, how often, for how long
- Rest and activity guidance
- Diet guidance if applicable

**Follow up with your doctor**
- See your regular doctor within [TIMEFRAME]
- Reason for follow-up in one sentence

**Come back to the ER right away if:**
List 3–5 specific warning signs in bold. Be very concrete.

### Complex multi-problem discharge

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Complex Discharge Instructions">
You are a physician writing discharge instructions for a patient with multiple medical problems who is leaving the hospital. The patient has no medical background. Write at a 6th grade reading level. Use short sentences. Bold any critical safety information.

This patient has several conditions and many medication changes. Organize the instructions clearly so they are not overwhelming.

**Why you were in the hospital**
2–3 sentences explaining the reason for the visit.

**Your conditions**
For each active condition, create a mini-section:

**[Condition 1 Name — in plain language]**
- What it is (one sentence)
- What we did for it
- What you need to do at home

**[Condition 2 Name — in plain language]**
- What it is (one sentence)
- What we did for it
- What you need to do at home

(Repeat for each condition)

**Your medication schedule**
Create a simple table showing what to take and when:

| Time of Day | Medication | Dose | What It's For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Morning | [Med] | [Dose] | [Purpose] |
| Evening | [Med] | [Dose] | [Purpose] |

List any medications that were STOPPED and why.

**Activity and diet**
Specific restrictions with timelines.

**Follow-up appointments**
Table format: Who, When, What For, Phone Number

**Warning signs: Call your doctor**
Organized by condition if applicable.

**Warning signs: Go to the ER**
3–5 most critical warning signs, bolded.

## Example output

**Why you were in the hospital**

You came to the hospital because you were having trouble breathing and your legs were very swollen. We found that your heart was not pumping as strongly as it should. This is called heart failure. Fluid was building up in your lungs and legs because your heart could not pump it through your body well enough.

**What we did**

- We gave you medicine through an IV to help your body get rid of extra fluid (a "water pill")
- We did blood tests and an ultrasound of your heart
- We checked your kidney function and electrolytes (blood salt levels)
- We adjusted your medications

**Your medications**

- **New: Furosemide (Lasix) 40 mg** — Take one tablet every morning. This is a water pill that helps your body get rid of extra fluid so your legs do not swell and your breathing stays easier. You will urinate more often. Take it in the morning so it does not wake you up at night.
- **New: Carvedilol 6.25 mg** — Take one tablet twice a day with food. This helps your heart pump better over time. You may feel a little dizzy at first; sit down if this happens.
- **Continued: Lisinopril 10 mg** — Keep taking one tablet every morning. This protects your heart and kidneys.
- **Stopped: Ibuprofen** — Do not take ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) anymore. It can make heart failure worse and harm your kidneys. Use Tylenol (acetaminophen) for pain instead.

**When to go to the emergency room**

- **You cannot breathe when lying flat and need to sit up or use extra pillows to sleep**
- **You gain more than 3 pounds in one day or 5 pounds in one week**
- **You have chest pain that does not go away after 10 minutes**
- **You feel lightheaded or faint**
- **Your legs or feet swell up much more than they are now**

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Reading level | Change to "4th grade" for low health literacy or "8th grade" for more detail |
| Language | Add: "Write in [LANGUAGE]" |
| Caregiver | Add: "Address instructions to the patient's caregiver rather than the patient directly" |
| Pediatric | Add: "This is for a child's parent/guardian" |
| Surgical discharge | Add: "Include a wound care section with specific instructions" |
| Length | Add: "Keep the entire document to 2 pages maximum" |
| Medication detail | Add: "For each new medication, also list the most common side effects" |

## Tips & pitfalls

- This template is distinct from the **Discharge Summary: Basic Template**, which produces a clinician-facing document for the receiving PCP. This template is for the patient themselves.
- This is a supplement to your institution's standard AVS (After Visit Summary), not a replacement.
- Pair with the **Medication Instructions** template for detailed med-by-med education.
- Always verify the medication section against the actual medication reconciliation, as this is the highest-risk section for AI errors.
- For complex discharges (e.g., new cancer diagnosis, ICU stay), the output will need significant review and may benefit from being paired with an in-person explanation.
- For surgical discharges, add wound care and activity restriction details to the input, or use the Surgical Discharge variation.
- The "When to go to the emergency room" section is the most safety-critical part. Review it carefully and make sure the warning signs are specific to the patient's condition, not generic.
