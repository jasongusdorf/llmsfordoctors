## When to use this

Your patient is leaving the hospital and needs to understand what happened, what changed in their medications, and what should send them back to the ER. This template generates plain-language discharge instructions written directly to the patient at a 6th grade reading level. It supplements (not replaces) your institution's standard AVS paperwork.

This is the patient-facing counterpart to the clinician-facing discharge summary.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Patient Discharge Instructions">
You are a physician writing discharge instructions for a patient leaving the hospital. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to make sure they understand what happened, what to do at home, and when to come back.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Warning signs must be concrete: "you gain more than 3 pounds in one day" not "fluid retention."
- Medication instructions must include what it does, not just the name and dose.
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "water pill" alongside diuretic names
- Use "blood thinner" alongside anticoagulant names
- Use "blood pressure medicine" not "antihypertensive"
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Reading level | Change to "4th grade" for low health literacy or "8th grade" for more detail |
| Language | Add: "Write in [LANGUAGE]" |
| Caregiver | Add: "Address instructions to the patient's caregiver rather than the patient directly" |
| Pediatric | Add: "This is for a child's parent/guardian. Use 'your child' throughout." |
| Length | Add: "Keep the entire document to 2 pages maximum" |
| Medication detail | Add: "For each new medication, also list the 2 most common side effects and what to do about them" |
| Wound care | Add: "Include a wound care section with daily instructions and what normal healing looks like vs. signs of infection" |

### Surgical discharge

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Surgical Discharge Instructions">
You are a surgeon writing post-surgical discharge instructions for a patient. The patient has no medical background.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- Bold all critical safety information.
- Wound care must describe what normal healing looks like AND what is not normal.
- Pain medication instructions must include maximum daily amount and when to switch to over-the-counter.

  ─────────────────────────────
  - ***
  - ***

  ──────────────

  NOT Normal (Call Your Doctor)
  ────────────────────────────
  - Spreading redness beyond 1 inch from the incision
  - Pus or foul-smelling drainage
  - The incision opens up

───────────────
- ***: Take *** every *** hours. Do not take more
  than *** in 24 hours.
- Switch to Tylenol or ibuprofen when: ***

- Do not drive while taking prescription pain medicine.

## Example output

```
YOUR DISCHARGE INSTRUCTIONS
══════════════════════════════════════════

Dear Patient,

Why You Were in the Hospital
────────────────────────────
You came to the hospital because you were having
trouble breathing and your legs were very swollen.
We found that your heart is not pumping as strongly
as it should. This is called heart failure. Fluid was
building up in your lungs and legs.

What We Did
───────────
- Gave you medicine through an IV to help your body
  get rid of extra fluid (a "water pill")
- Did blood tests and an ultrasound of your heart
- Checked your kidney function
- Adjusted your medications

Your Medications
────────────────
| Medication            | What It's For              | How to Take It                     | New or Changed? |
|-----------------------|----------------------------|------------------------------------|-----------------|
| Furosemide (Lasix)    | Removes extra fluid        | 40 mg, one pill every morning      | NEW             |
| Carvedilol            | Helps your heart pump      | 6.25 mg, one pill twice a day      | NEW             |
| Lisinopril            | Protects heart and kidneys | 10 mg, one pill every morning      | No change       |

Medications that were STOPPED:
- Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin): makes heart failure worse
  and can harm your kidneys. Use Tylenol for pain instead.

Follow-Up Appointments
──────────────────────
| Doctor           | When              | What For                      | Phone          |
|------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|----------------|
| Heart doctor     | Within 1 week     | Check weight, labs, medicines | (555) 555-0100 |
| Regular doctor   | Within 2 weeks    | Overall check-up              | (555) 555-0200 |

Tests to complete before your follow-up:
- Blood test (BMP) in 5 days to check kidney function
  and potassium

Warning Signs
─────────────
Go to the ER if:
- You cannot breathe when lying flat
- You gain more than 3 pounds in one day or 5 pounds
  in one week
- You have chest pain lasting more than 10 minutes
- You feel lightheaded or faint

Call your doctor if:
- Your legs or feet swell more than they are now
- You feel dizzy when you stand up
- You are urinating much less than usual

Activity and Diet
─────────────────
  What You Can Do
  ───────────────
  - Walk around the house and gradually increase
  - Light housework is fine

  What to Avoid
  ─────────────
  - Heavy lifting (more than 10 pounds) for 2 weeks
  - Strenuous exercise until your heart doctor clears you

  Diet
  ────
  - Limit salt to less than 2,000 mg per day (less than
    1 teaspoon). Check labels on canned soups, frozen
    meals, and deli meat.
  - Limit fluids to 2 liters (about 8 cups) per day
  - Weigh yourself every morning. Write it down.
```

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Verify the medication section against the actual med rec.** This is the highest-risk section for AI errors and the most consequential for patient safety. Cross-check every name, dose, and frequency.
- **The "Go to the ER" section deserves line-by-line review.** Warning signs must be specific to this patient's conditions, not a generic list. A CHF patient needs weight gain thresholds. A post-surgical patient needs wound infection criteria. Different patients, different red flags.
- **This is a supplement to your AVS, not a replacement.** Your institution's After Visit Summary has legal and billing implications. This template creates a more readable companion document.
- **Pair with the Medication Instructions template for polypharmacy.** When a patient goes home on 8 medications with 3 changes, a separate med-by-med sheet helps.
- **For complex discharges, the output needs significant review.** New cancer diagnoses, ICU stays, and multi-system illness generate lengthy instructions that benefit from an in-person walkthrough with the patient and a family member.
- **Consider the caregiver.** If someone other than the patient will be managing medications and appointments at home, add that to your prompt input so the output is addressed appropriately.
- **AI tends to generate generic diet advice.** "Eat a healthy diet" is useless. Specify the restrictions in your input: low sodium with a gram target, fluid restriction with a volume, diabetic diet with carb ranges.
