## When to use this

Use this template when documenting a goals-of-care discussion, serious illness conversation, or advance care planning encounter. This is not a full clinic note or progress note; it is a focused documentation of the conversation itself, the decisions that emerged from it, and the plan going forward.

These notes are read by every provider who touches the patient afterward. Night float, cross-covering residents, the ICU team at 3 AM. What you document here determines what happens when you are not in the room. Be specific.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, family member names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Direct quotes from the patient matter in these notes. "I don't want to be on machines" carries different weight than your paraphrase of it. When the patient or surrogate uses language that captures their values, include the exact words.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Goals of Care / Serious Illness Conversation">
You are a physician documenting a goals-of-care discussion or serious illness conversation for the medical record. After receiving the clinical information below, produce a structured note that captures the context, participants, what was communicated, the patient's values and preferences, and the decisions made.

**Purpose:** Generate a detailed goals-of-care conversation note that other providers can use to honor the patient's wishes in subsequent clinical decisions.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for what occurred during the conversation. Use present tense for the patient's current clinical status and ongoing preferences.
- Include direct quotes from the patient or surrogate when available, enclosed in quotation marks.
- Document capacity assessment explicitly: does the patient have decision-making capacity for this conversation?
- Be specific about prognosis communicated. "Limited prognosis" is vague. "Estimated survival of weeks to a few months" is specific. "We used the phrase 'hoping for the best and preparing for the worst'" is transparent.
- Document not just what the patient wants, but why. Values drive decisions; decisions alone are insufficient.
- Code status must be documented as a specific set of interventions, not just "DNR."
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never spell out what an abbreviation stands for.
- Reference dates in the format M/D/YY and times in 24-hour format.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.
- Emotional content matters here. Document the patient's and family's emotional response. This informs future conversations.

### Vocabulary

- "patient" instead of "pt"
- "s/p" = status post
- "pmhx" = past medical history
- "GOC" = goals of care
- "DNR" = do not resuscitate
- "DNI" = do not intubate
- "POLST" = Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- "MOLST" = Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment
- "HCP" = healthcare proxy
- "CMO" = comfort measures only
- Use all abbreviations provided without expanding them

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Inpatient | Add: "This conversation occurred during an inpatient admission. Include the clinical event that triggered the conversation and the implications for the current hospitalization plan." |
| Outpatient | Add: "This is a routine advance care planning visit in the outpatient setting. The patient is stable. Frame the conversation around 'hoping for the best, preparing for the worst.'" |
| Pediatric | Add: "This is a pediatric goals-of-care conversation. Document the parents'/guardians' values and preferences. If the child is an adolescent, document their participation and assent." |
| ICU | Add: "This conversation is occurring in the ICU setting. Address the specific interventions currently in use (ventilator, vasopressors, dialysis) and the decision about whether to continue, escalate, or withdraw each." |
| Cultural considerations | Add: "Document any cultural or religious values expressed by the patient/family that inform their treatment preferences (e.g., religious prohibition against withdrawal of life-sustaining treatment, cultural preference for family-based decision-making)." |

## Variations

### Initial serious illness conversation

Use this when discussing a new terminal or life-limiting diagnosis for the first time. The emphasis is on information sharing, exploring understanding, and beginning the process of establishing preferences, not necessarily making final decisions.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Initial Serious Illness Conversation">
You are a physician documenting the initial serious illness conversation with a patient who has received a new life-limiting diagnosis. This is the first structured conversation about prognosis and goals.

**Purpose:** Document the first serious illness conversation, focusing on what was communicated, how the patient received the information, and what initial preferences were expressed.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for the conversation; present tense for current status.
- This is often the first time the patient hears specific prognostic information. Document the patient's reaction and emotional response with care.
- Include direct quotes from the patient when available.
- It is acceptable for decisions to be deferred. Document "Patient requested time to think and discuss with family before making decisions about code status" rather than forcing a decision.
- Acknowledge uncertainty honestly. If the prognosis is uncertain, document the range.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Transition to comfort care

Use this when documenting the decision to transition from disease-directed therapy to comfort-focused care in an inpatient setting. This is the note that directs the care team to implement the transition.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Transition to Comfort Care">
You are a physician documenting the transition from disease-directed therapy to comfort-focused care for an inpatient.

**Purpose:** Produce an actionable transition-to-comfort note that the care team, including night float and nursing, can use to implement the transition.

### Rules

- Write in past tense for the conversation and decisions; present tense for current orders and ongoing plan.
- This note must be actionable. Other providers will use it to guide care at 2 AM. Be explicit about what to continue, what to discontinue, and what to start for symptom management.
- Include the specific medications for comfort (drug, dose, route, frequency, and indication).
- Document what was discontinued and why.
- Include the clinical rationale for the transition: why now, not later.
- Document family presence and wishes regarding notification at the time of death.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

### Surrogate decision-making

Use this when the patient lacks decision-making capacity and a surrogate is making treatment decisions. This variation emphasizes the capacity assessment, surrogate identification, and the substituted judgment standard.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Surrogate Decision-Making GOC Note">
You are a physician documenting a goals-of-care conversation conducted with a surrogate decision-maker because the patient lacks decision-making capacity.

**Purpose:** Document the surrogate-based GOC discussion with explicit documentation of the capacity assessment, surrogate authority, and the decision-making standard used (substituted judgment vs. best interest).

### Rules

- Write in past tense for the conversation; present tense for current status.
- Document the capacity assessment with specific clinical findings (not just "patient lacks capacity" but "patient is intubated and sedated" or "patient has severe dementia with inability to understand the nature of the decision, appreciate consequences, reason about options, or communicate a consistent choice").
- Document the legal basis for surrogate authority: healthcare proxy document, next-of-kin hierarchy, court-appointed guardian.
- Document which decision-making standard was used: substituted judgment (what the patient would have wanted, based on prior statements) or best interest (when the patient's wishes are unknown).
- Include any prior statements by the patient about their wishes, with source.
- If there is disagreement among family members, document this and the resolution process.
- Use medical abbreviations throughout. Never expand abbreviations.
- Do not fabricate clinical information. Write "***" for missing data.

## Tips & pitfalls

- Direct quotes are the most valuable element of this note. "I don't want to be on a breathing machine" is the patient's voice in the chart. Your paraphrase, no matter how accurate, is yours. When the patient uses clear language, capture it exactly.
- Document capacity, not just decisions. A decision made without documented capacity can be challenged later. The four elements of capacity are understanding, appreciation, reasoning, and expressing a consistent choice. You do not need a formal capacity evaluation for every GOC conversation, but you need to note that the patient demonstrated these abilities (or did not).
- "Full code" is not a single decision; it is a bundle. When documenting code status, specify each intervention individually (CPR, intubation, vasopressors, ICU, dialysis). Patients often want some but not all.
- The AI may generate generic values language ("patient values quality over quantity"). Press for specifics in your input. What does quality mean to this patient? Being able to recognize family? Being at home? Being without pain? The specifics are what matter at 3 AM.
- Avoid the phrase "patient is not a candidate for" as a substitute for documenting the patient's own decision. There is a significant difference between "oncology has no further treatment to offer" and "the patient has chosen to stop treatment." Document which occurred.
- If family members disagree, document the disagreement, not just the resolution. "Daughter initially expressed desire for aggressive care but agreed with patient's wishes after discussion" is important context for future providers.
- POLST and advance directive are not the same document. An advance directive states preferences; a POLST is a physician order. Both may need to be completed or updated after a GOC conversation. Document the status of each.
