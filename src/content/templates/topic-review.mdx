## When to use this

Use this template to generate a structured, high-yield summary of any medical topic for board review. It produces the facts you need to recognize on exam day: classic presentations, buzzword associations, diagnostic criteria, treatment algorithms, and the traps examiners set.

This is a consolidation tool, not a primary learning resource. Use it to organize knowledge you have already studied, or to identify gaps worth revisiting in a textbook.

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="High-Yield Topic Review">
You are a board review course director creating a high-yield summary for exam preparation.

### Purpose
Create a structured, rapid-review summary of a medical topic covering everything a student needs for boards: definition, pathophysiology, classic presentation, diagnosis, treatment, buzzwords, and common exam traps.

### Rules
- Every claim should be a testable fact. No filler, no hedging, no "it is important to note."
- Use precise values: "serum sodium below 120 mEq/L" not "significantly low sodium."
- Buzzword associations must use the arrow format: [term] → [association].
- Common board traps must explain both what the trap is and why students fall for it.
- Keep the entire output in rapid-review format. No lengthy explanations.

### Vocabulary
- "Gold standard" for the definitive diagnostic test
- "First-line" for initial treatment
- "Best initial test" for the first diagnostic step
- Use standard board language throughout

### Output format

### Pathophysiology deep-dive

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Pathophysiology Deep Dive">
You are a pathophysiology professor explaining a disease mechanism for a medical student who wants to understand the "why," not just memorize the "what."

### Purpose
Create a detailed causal chain from normal physiology through disease mechanism to clinical manifestation. Connect every symptom back to a specific step in the pathologic cascade. Explain why each treatment works by identifying which step it interrupts.

### Rules
- Start with normal physiology as a baseline.
- Use numbered steps with arrows to show causality.
- For each clinical feature, trace it back to a specific step in the cascade.
- For each treatment, explain which step it targets.

## Example output

HIGH-YIELD TOPIC REVIEW: PULMONARY EMBOLISM
═════════════════════════════════════════════

OVERVIEW
────────
Pulmonary embolism is obstruction of a pulmonary artery by thrombus, most commonly originating from deep vein thrombosis in the lower extremities. It ranges from incidental subsegmental findings to massive PE with cardiovascular collapse and is a leading cause of preventable in-hospital death.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
───────────────
- Virchow's triad (stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability) drives thrombus formation in deep veins
- Thrombus embolizes to pulmonary vasculature, increasing pulmonary vascular resistance
- Acute right ventricular pressure overload causes RV dilation, decreased LV filling, and hypotension
- V/Q mismatch produces hypoxemia; dead-space ventilation drives tachypnea
- Pulmonary infarction occurs in a minority of cases (dual blood supply usually prevents it)

CLINICAL PRESENTATION
─────────────────────
Classic: 45-year-old woman 10 days post-knee surgery presents with sudden-onset dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, and tachycardia. SpO2 91% on room air. Unilateral calf swelling on exam.
Atypical: Syncope as the sole presentation (suggests massive PE). Isolated tachycardia without dyspnea. Gradual-onset dyspnea over days in a cancer patient (chronic thromboembolic disease).

DIAGNOSIS
─────────
Gold standard: Pulmonary angiography (rarely used in practice)
Best initial test: CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) for hemodynamically stable patients; bedside echocardiography for unstable patients who cannot go to CT
Findings: Intraluminal filling defect on CTPA. ABG shows respiratory alkalosis with hypoxemia and elevated A-a gradient. D-dimer is sensitive but not specific; useful only to rule out PE in low-risk patients (Wells score 4 or below).

TREATMENT
─────────
First-line: Anticoagulation with heparin (unfractionated or LMWH), then transition to DOAC or warfarin
Second-line: IVC filter when anticoagulation is contraindicated (active hemorrhage, recent surgery)
Contraindications: Active bleeding is an absolute contraindication to anticoagulation
Emergency: Systemic thrombolytics (alteplase) for massive PE with hemodynamic instability; catheter-directed therapy as an alternative

BOARD BUZZWORDS
───────────────
Sudden dyspnea + pleuritic chest pain + tachycardia → PE
Post-surgical immobilization → DVT/PE risk
S1Q3T3 on ECG → right heart strain (classic board answer, poor sensitivity in practice)
Hampton's hump on CXR → pulmonary infarction
Westermark sign on CXR → oligemia distal to embolism
Saddle embolus → massive PE at bifurcation of main pulmonary artery
Wedge-shaped infarct → pulmonary infarction with dual supply failure

COMMON BOARD TRAPS
──────────────────
- Trap 1: Ordering a D-dimer in a high-probability patient. D-dimer is a rule-out test for low-risk patients only. A positive D-dimer in a post-surgical patient tells you nothing; go straight to CTPA.
- Trap 2: Withholding anticoagulation while waiting for imaging. If clinical suspicion is high, start heparin before the CT. Treatment should not wait for confirmation.
- Trap 3: Choosing thrombolytics for a hemodynamically stable PE. Thrombolytics are reserved for massive PE with hypotension or cardiovascular collapse, not for submassive PE with isolated RV dilation.

QUICK-HIT FACTS
────────────────
- Wells criteria: the most commonly tested clinical prediction rule for PE
- D-dimer rules out PE only when pretest probability is low
- Heparin before imaging if clinical suspicion is high
- In hemodynamically unstable patients, bedside echo showing RV dilation supports empiric thrombolytics
- Duration of anticoagulation: 3 months for provoked PE, indefinite for unprovoked or recurrent
- Factor V Leiden is the most common inherited thrombophilia
- Pregnancy shifts the diagnostic algorithm: use compression ultrasound of the legs first to avoid radiation

## Tips & pitfalls

- Verify critical facts (drug doses, diagnostic criteria, guideline recommendations) against a trusted source. First Aid, UpToDate, and Amboss are reliable cross-references.
- The "Common Board Traps" section is often the most valuable part of the output. If you only read one section, make it that one.
- For topics you already know well, use the "Rapid review" variation instead of the full template. It saves time and avoids the false sense of productivity that comes from rereading material you have already mastered.
- Pair with the Question Generator template to test yourself after reviewing a topic. Reviewing without testing is the least efficient study strategy.
- AI output is most reliable for well-established, commonly tested concepts. It is less reliable for nuanced or recently updated guidelines (e.g., 2025 ACC/AHA updates). When in doubt, check the publication date of the guideline being referenced.
- If the output contains a fact you are unsure about, do not memorize it. Flag it and verify it. A wrong fact memorized with confidence is worse than a gap.
