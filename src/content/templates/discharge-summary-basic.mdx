## When to use this

Use this template when discharging a patient from an inpatient admission and you need a structured summary for the receiving outpatient provider.

This template produces a complete, structured discharge summary for a primary care handoff. Paste in a de-identified clinical narrative, and Claude will generate a professional summary in the standard sections expected by most receiving physicians.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, admission/discharge dates, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

---

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Discharge Summary: Basic">
You are an experienced hospitalist physician writing a discharge summary for the receiving outpatient primary care provider.

Using the clinical information provided below, write a complete and concise discharge summary with the following sections:

**1. Admission Diagnosis**
State the primary reason for admission and any secondary diagnoses addressed during the hospitalization.

**2. Hospital Course**
Provide a concise narrative (3–5 sentences) covering: key clinical events, decisions made, procedures performed, consultations obtained, and the clinical trajectory during the stay.

**3. Significant Results**
List the most clinically important labs, imaging, pathology, or procedure findings. Format as a short bulleted list.

**4. Discharge Medications**
List only medications that CHANGED during this admission:
- Started: [medication, dose, frequency, duration/indication]
- Stopped: [medication, reason if known]
- Dose-adjusted: [medication, old dose → new dose]

**5. Discharge Condition**
One sentence: stable / improved / at baseline / guarded.

**6. Follow-up Instructions**
- Scheduled appointments (specialty, timeframe)
- Pending results (what, expected timing, who reviews)
- Activity and dietary restrictions if applicable

**7. Return Precautions**
List 3–5 specific symptoms or signs that should prompt the patient to seek emergency care.

Be concise and clinically precise. Avoid filler language. Use standard medical abbreviations where appropriate.

## Variations

### Brief discharge summary for short stays

Use this for straightforward 1-2 day admissions (e.g., observation stays, uncomplicated cellulitis, simple CHF exacerbation) where a full discharge summary is unnecessarily lengthy.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Brief Discharge Summary (Short Stay)">
You are a hospitalist writing a brief discharge summary for a short (1–2 day) uncomplicated hospital stay.

Using the clinical information below, write a concise discharge summary in this abbreviated format:

**Admission Diagnosis:** One line.

**Brief Hospital Course:** 2–3 sentences maximum. What happened, what was done, why the patient is being discharged.

**Key Results:** Only results that the outpatient provider needs to know about (abnormal or pending). Omit if all results were unremarkable.

**Medication Changes:**
- New: [med, dose, frequency, duration]
- Stopped: [med, reason]
(Only list changes. If no changes, write "No medication changes.")

**Follow-up:** Appointments and pending results on one to two lines.

**Return if:** 3 specific return precautions, one line each.

Keep the entire summary under 200 words. This is for a straightforward admission; do not over-document.

### Complex multi-problem discharge

Use this for patients with multiple active diagnoses, medication changes across several drug classes, and complex follow-up needs (e.g., new cancer diagnosis, post-cardiac surgery, multi-organ failure recovery).

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Complex Discharge Summary">
You are a hospitalist writing a detailed discharge summary for a complex patient with multiple active medical problems and extensive medication changes.

Using the clinical information below, write a comprehensive discharge summary:

**1. Admission Diagnoses**
Primary and all secondary diagnoses addressed during the stay, listed.

**2. Hospital Course by Problem**
Organize the hospital course by problem rather than chronologically. For each major problem:
- What was found
- What was done (procedures, consults, treatments)
- Current status at discharge
- Outstanding issues

**3. Procedures Performed**
List all procedures with dates and key findings.

**4. Significant Results**
Organized by category: labs, imaging, pathology, microbiology. Include pending results with expected timeframes and responsible provider.

**5. Discharge Medications — Complete Reconciliation**
Full medication list organized by category:
- NEW medications: [drug, dose, route, frequency, indication, duration if applicable]
- CHANGED medications: [drug, old dose → new dose, reason]
- DISCONTINUED medications: [drug, reason]
- UNCHANGED home medications: [list for completeness]

**6. Discharge Condition & Functional Status**
Clinical condition, ambulatory status, O2 needs, weight-bearing status, diet.

**7. Follow-up Plan**
Table format: | Provider/Specialty | Timeframe | Reason |
Include all pending results with expected dates and who is responsible for follow-up.

**8. Patient/Family Education**
Key points discussed with patient/family, new diagnosis education, medication teaching.

**9. Return Precautions**
5–7 specific symptoms or signs organized by urgency (call PCP vs go to ED).

Be thorough. This patient has complex needs and the discharge summary serves as the primary communication tool for the outpatient team.

### Patient-facing discharge instructions

Use this to generate a plain-language discharge summary that can be given directly to the patient or family, written at an accessible reading level.

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Patient-Facing Discharge Instructions">
You are a physician writing discharge instructions for a patient and their family. Write at a 6th grade reading level. Avoid medical jargon; use plain language.

Using the clinical information below, write patient-friendly discharge instructions:

**Why You Were in the Hospital**
1–2 sentences in simple language explaining the diagnosis and what happened.

**What We Did**
Brief, plain-language summary of treatments, procedures, and tests.

**Your Medicines**
For each new or changed medication:
- Name of medicine
- What it's for (in plain language)
- How to take it (dose, time of day, with/without food)
- How long to take it
- Side effects to watch for

**What to Do at Home**
- Activity level (what you can and can't do)
- Diet instructions
- Wound care or other self-care tasks

**Your Follow-up Appointments**
- Who to see, when, and why (in plain terms)
- Any tests that are still pending and what will happen with results

**When to Call Your Doctor**
List 3–4 warning signs that mean "call your doctor's office."

**When to Go to the Emergency Room**
List 3–4 warning signs that mean "go to the ER right away."

Use short sentences. Use "you" and "your." Avoid abbreviations.

## Example output

Here is what this template produces when given realistic (fictional) clinical input:

**Input provided:**
A 74-year-old female admitted for 4 days with acute decompensated heart failure (EF 35%), triggered by dietary indiscretion and medication nonadherence. Treated with IV furosemide, diuresed 4.5L over admission. Echo showed EF 35% (prior 40%), moderate MR, no WMA. BNP 1840 on admission, down to 620 at discharge. Cr stable at 1.2. Started on entresto 24/12.5mg BID (replacing lisinopril 10mg), increased furosemide from 20mg to 40mg daily. K+ supplemented. Cardiology consult recommended ICD evaluation as outpatient. Discharged on room air, weight 78kg (dry weight ~76kg).

**LLM output:**

**1. Admission Diagnosis**
Primary: Acute decompensated heart failure (HFrEF, EF 35%)
Secondary: Medication nonadherence, dietary indiscretion

**2. Hospital Course**
Patient presented with 3 days of progressive dyspnea, orthopnea, and lower extremity edema in the setting of dietary indiscretion and missed doses of lisinopril. She was admitted and started on IV furosemide with a net diuresis of 4.5L over 4 days. Echocardiogram showed a decline in EF from 40% to 35% with moderate mitral regurgitation. Cardiology was consulted and recommended transition to sacubitril-valsartan and outpatient ICD evaluation. She was transitioned to oral diuretics with stable renal function and discharged on room air.

**3. Significant Results**
- BNP: 1840 pg/mL (admission) → 620 pg/mL (discharge)
- TTE: EF 35% (prior 40%), moderate MR, no wall motion abnormalities
- Cr: Stable at 1.2 mg/dL throughout admission
- K+: 3.4 on admission, supplemented, 4.1 at discharge

**4. Discharge Medications**
- Started: Sacubitril-valsartan (Entresto) 24/12.5 mg PO BID (replacing lisinopril for HFrEF)
- Stopped: Lisinopril 10 mg daily (switched to Entresto)
- Dose-adjusted: Furosemide 20 mg → 40 mg PO daily

**5. Discharge Condition**
Improved; ambulating independently on room air with residual 2 kg above dry weight.

**6. Follow-up Instructions**
- Cardiology: 2 weeks for ICD evaluation and Entresto uptitration
- PCP: 1 week for weight check, BMP, and volume reassessment
- Pending: None
- Restrictions: 2g sodium diet, daily weight monitoring, fluid restriction 1.5L/day

**7. Return Precautions**
- Weight gain >3 lbs in 24 hours or >5 lbs in one week
- Worsening shortness of breath at rest or with minimal activity
- New or worsening lower extremity swelling
- Chest pain or palpitations
- Dizziness or near-syncope (may indicate hypotension from new medication)

## Tips & pitfalls

- This template is optimized for **general medicine** admissions. See specialty-specific templates for cardiology, oncology, and surgical discharges.
- For complex discharges (e.g., new cancer diagnosis, major surgery), the AI output will require more editing; use the complex variation above or treat the output as a structural skeleton rather than a final draft.
- Always verify the medication section against the actual medication reconciliation before signing.
- **Common AI failure mode:** The model may omit medications that were continued unchanged but are clinically important (e.g., anticoagulants, immunosuppressants). Always cross-reference the full med list.
- **Pending results:** AI often writes "no pending results" even when there are outstanding cultures, pathology, or outpatient labs. Double-check this section.
- **Date sensitivity:** The model has no access to real dates. Verify all follow-up timeframes against actual scheduled appointments.
- **Medication interactions:** The model will not reliably flag new drug interactions introduced by medication changes. Use your pharmacy system for interaction checking.
