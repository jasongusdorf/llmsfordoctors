## When to use this

A patient just heard a diagnosis for the first time and is staring at you with the particular blankness that means they stopped processing two sentences ago. This template generates a plain-language handout that covers what they have, why they have it, what happens next, and when to worry. It works for any condition, from new-onset diabetes to a first seizure.

Hand over the output as a take-home sheet to reinforce your conversation, not replace it.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, dates of service, provider names, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Condition Explainer">
You are a physician writing a patient education handout. Your goal is to explain a medical condition so clearly that a patient with no medical background walks away understanding what they have, what to do about it, and when to worry.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Never use a medical term without defining it in parentheses the first time.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Use an everyday analogy to explain the core mechanism if one fits naturally.
- Warning signs must be concrete and specific (e.g., "chest pain lasting more than 10 minutes"), never vague ("worsening symptoms").
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "blood sugar" not "glucose"
- Use "blood pressure" not "BP"
- Use "heart attack" not "MI"
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Reading level | Change "6th grade" to "4th grade" for low health literacy or "8th grade" for more detail |
| Condition type | Add: "This is a chronic condition; emphasize long-term management and that this is treatable, not curable" |
| Specialty | Add: "This is a cardiology diagnosis. Include heart-specific lifestyle guidance." |
| Caregiver audience | Add: "This handout is for the patient's family caregiver, not the patient directly" |
| Length | Add: "Keep the entire handout under 300 words" |
| Language | Add: "Write the explanation in [LANGUAGE]" |
| Emotional weight | Add: "This is a frightening diagnosis. Use a warm, reassuring tone without minimizing the condition." |

## Example output

```
ABOUT YOUR CONDITION
══════════════════════════════════════════

What You Have
──────────────
You have type 2 diabetes. This means your body has
trouble using sugar from the food you eat. Think of
insulin as a key that opens your cells to let sugar
in for energy. In diabetes, that key does not work
well. Sugar builds up in your blood instead.

What Causes It
──────────────
- Your body does not respond to insulin the way it
  used to (called insulin resistance)
- Extra weight around the belly makes insulin work
  less well
- It runs in families
- Not moving enough and eating a lot of sugar or
  processed foods can make it worse

What to Expect
──────────────
You may not feel different right now. Many people
with type 2 diabetes feel fine at first. Over time,
if blood sugar stays too high, you might feel more
thirsty than usual. You might need to use the
bathroom more often. You might feel tired. The good
news: with treatment and changes to your daily
habits, most people keep their blood sugar under
control and feel well.

Your Treatment Plan
───────────────────
  Medications
  ───────────
  - Metformin 500 mg: helps your body use insulin
    better. Take one pill twice a day with meals. You
    may feel queasy at first. This usually gets better
    after a week or two.

  Lifestyle Changes
  ─────────────────
  1. Fill half your plate with vegetables at each meal.
     Cut back on sugary drinks, white bread, and sweets.
  2. Walk for 20 to 30 minutes a day, at least 5 days
     a week. Walking after meals helps your blood sugar
     the most.
  3. Check your blood sugar at home. Your doctor will
     show you how.

  Tests or Follow-Up
  ───────────────────
  - A1c blood test in 3 months to see how your blood
    sugar has been doing overall
  - Eye exam within the next year
  - Foot check at every visit

When to Get Help Right Away
───────────────────────────
Go to the ER if:
- Your blood sugar is above 300 and you feel sick,
  confused, or very thirsty
- You feel shaky, sweaty, or dizzy and your blood
  sugar is below 70
- You have chest pain or trouble breathing

Call your doctor if:
- A cut or sore on your foot is not healing after
  a few days
- You have blurry vision that comes on suddenly
- Your blood sugar stays above 250 for more than
  two days in a row

Questions for Your Doctor
─────────────────────────
1. What should my blood sugar numbers be when I check
   at home?
2. Will I need to take insulin shots, or will the pill
   be enough?
3. Can I still eat my favorite foods sometimes?
4. How often do I need to come back for check-ups?
5. Should I see a diabetes educator or dietitian?
```

## Tips & pitfalls

- **One diagnosis per handout.** For patients with multiple new diagnoses, generate separate handouts. A combined sheet overwhelms rather than informs.
- **The "When to Get Help" section is the most safety-critical output.** Read it line by line. Make sure each warning sign is specific to this patient's condition, not a generic list.
- **AI-generated analogies can mislead.** The "lock and key" analogy for insulin works. Analogies for cancer or autoimmune disease often oversimplify in harmful ways. Review every analogy before handing the sheet to a patient.
- **Pair with a conversation, not a cold handoff.** Patients retain about 40-80% of what they hear in a visit, and nearly half of what they do retain is wrong. The handout reinforces your verbal explanation.
- **Sensitive diagnoses need human tone-checking.** Cancer, chronic illness, and terminal diagnoses carry emotional weight that AI handles unevenly. Review for sensitivity before printing.
- **Consider printing in large font for elderly patients.** The elderly variation is written for this, but any version benefits from 14-point type or larger.
- **Include the patient's own questions.** If they asked "Will I go blind?" during the visit, add that to the Patient Context section so the handout addresses it directly.
