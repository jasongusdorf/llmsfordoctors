## When to use this

You just prescribed a new medication and the patient needs more than the pharmacy printout that nobody reads. This template generates a single-medication deep dive: what it does, how to take it, side effects ranked by likelihood, dangerous reactions, food and drug interactions, and what happens if they miss a dose. It fills the gap between "take this twice a day" and the 14-page pharmacy insert. For patients going home on multiple new medications at once, use the Medication Instructions template instead.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, and any other direct identifiers. Medication names and doses alone are generally not identifiable.
</Callout>

<Callout type="pitfall">
  AI can generate plausible but incorrect medication information: wrong doses, missed interactions, outdated warnings. Always verify the output against a trusted drug reference (Lexicomp, Micromedex, UpToDate) before giving it to the patient.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="New Medication Guide">
You are a physician creating a medication guide for a patient who has just been prescribed a new medication. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to make sure they understand this medication well enough to take it safely and recognize problems early.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Use an everyday analogy to explain what the medication does if one fits naturally.
- Side effects: list only the 3-5 most common and the 2-3 most dangerous. Describe what the patient will feel, not medical terminology.
- Missed dose instructions must be specific to this medication, not generic.
- Warning signs must be concrete: "your skin or the whites of your eyes turn yellow" not "hepatotoxicity."
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "blood thinner" alongside anticoagulant names
- Use "blood sugar medicine" alongside metformin, etc.
- Use "blood pressure medicine" alongside ACE inhibitor names, etc.
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Reading level | Change to "4th grade" for low health literacy or "8th grade" for more detail |
| Multiple meds | Add: "Create a separate guide for each medication listed" |
| Pediatric | Add: "This medication is for a child. Write instructions for the parent. Include liquid dose in mL with syringe markings." |
| Antibiotic | Add: "Emphasize completing the full course even if the patient feels better after a few days" |
| Language | Add: "Write in [LANGUAGE]" |
| Monitoring required | Add: "This medication requires regular blood test monitoring. Explain what is being monitored and how often." |

### Controlled substance guide

- Include maximum daily amount.

## Example output

```
YOUR NEW MEDICATION
══════════════════════════════════════════

Metformin (Glucophage) 500 mg
────────────────────────────────────────

What This Medication Does
─────────────────────────
Metformin is a blood sugar medicine. It helps your
body use insulin (a hormone that controls blood
sugar) better. Think of insulin as a key that opens
your cells to let sugar in for energy. Metformin
makes that key work more smoothly, so less sugar
stays in your blood.

How to Take It
──────────────
- Dose: 1 tablet (500 mg)
- How often: Twice a day
- With food: Always take it with a meal. This
  reduces stomach upset.
- Best time of day: With breakfast and with dinner
- Special instructions: Swallow the tablet whole
  with a full glass of water. Do not crush or chew.
- How long you will take it: This is a long-term
  medication. You will likely take it for years.
  Do not stop without talking to your doctor.

Common Side Effects
───────────────────
These are normal and usually get better after the
first 1-2 weeks:

- Upset stomach or nausea: Take with food. Eat
  smaller, more frequent meals. This usually goes
  away on its own.
- Diarrhea: Drink plenty of water. Eat bland foods.
  Tell your doctor if it does not improve after 2
  weeks.
- Metallic taste in your mouth: This usually fades
  within a few weeks. Chewing sugar-free gum can
  help.

Dangerous Side Effects
──────────────────────
Go to the ER if:
- You feel very cold, dizzy, or have trouble
  breathing. This could be a rare but serious
  reaction called lactic acidosis (a buildup of
  acid in the blood).
- Your blood sugar drops very low: shaking,
  sweating, confusion. Eat or drink something
  with sugar and call 911 if you do not feel
  better in 15 minutes.

Call your doctor right away if:
- Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea that does not go
  away after 2 weeks
- You feel very tired or weak for no clear reason
- Unusual muscle pain or cramping

Interactions to Avoid
─────────────────────
  Foods
  ─────
  - Limit alcohol. Drinking too much alcohol while
    on metformin can cause a dangerous drop in
    blood sugar.

  Medications
  ───────────
  - Tell your doctor before having any scan that
    uses contrast dye (the kind where they inject
    something into your vein). You may need to stop
    metformin for a few days before and after.
  - Tell your doctor about all other medications,
    vitamins, and supplements you take.

  Other
  ─────
  - No specific activity restrictions.

If You Miss a Dose
──────────────────
Take it as soon as you remember, as long as it is
still close to a mealtime. If it is almost time for
your next dose, skip the missed one. Do not take
two tablets at once to make up for a missed dose.

Storage
───────
- Keep at room temperature.
- Keep the bottle closed and away from moisture.

Important
─────────
- Do not stop taking metformin without talking to
  your doctor, even if you feel fine.
- Keep out of reach of children.
- Bring this guide to your next doctor visit.
```

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Verify everything against a drug reference before printing.** AI can hallucinate plausible-sounding drug interactions, invent dose recommendations, and list outdated warnings. Lexicomp, Micromedex, or UpToDate should be your backstop for every output.
- **The side effect list needs trimming.** AI defaults to completeness. Patients need relevance. Keep the 3-5 most common side effects they are likely to experience and the 2-3 most dangerous ones they need to recognize. Fifteen side effects is not helpful; it is terrifying.
- **The "Interactions to Avoid" section is the most actionable part for patients.** It is also among the most error-prone for AI. Drug-food interactions and OTC medication conflicts are where patients make real mistakes. Double-check this section.
- **For monitored medications (warfarin, lithium, methotrexate), use the monitoring variation.** These medications require different emphasis: target ranges, blood test schedules, and consequences of levels being too high or too low.
- **This template goes deep on one medication.** For patients starting 3 or more medications at once, use the Medication Instructions template for a batch overview, then generate a New Medication Guide only for the highest-risk or most complex medication.
- **AI may be overly reassuring about side effects.** "This usually goes away on its own" is not always true. Review each side effect note for clinical accuracy.
