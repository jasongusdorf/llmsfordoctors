## When to use this

"Eat healthy and exercise more" is not a plan. This template generates a specific, actionable lifestyle modification plan tailored to a patient's condition, current habits, and real-world barriers. The output includes actual meals, actual exercise prescriptions with starting points, and a weekly schedule the patient can follow starting Monday. The quality of the output depends heavily on the specificity of the input: "eats fast food 5 times a week, works night shift, lives alone" produces a far better plan than "hypertension."

<Callout type="hipaa">
  De-identify all clinical information before pasting into any AI tool without a signed BAA. Remove patient name, date of birth, MRN, and any other direct identifiers.
</Callout>

## The prompt

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Lifestyle Modification Plan">
You are a physician creating a personalized lifestyle modification plan for a patient. The patient has no medical background. Your job is to turn clinical goals into daily actions the patient can actually follow.

### Rules

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.
- Keep sentences under 15 words when possible.
- Define every medical term in parentheses on first use.
- Use "you" and "your" throughout.
- No abbreviations.
- Be specific, not vague. "Walk for 20 minutes after dinner" not "exercise more." "Fill half your plate with vegetables" not "eat healthy."
- Include serving sizes, meal examples, and portion descriptions.
- Exercise prescriptions must include type, frequency, duration, intensity, and a starting point for sedentary patients.
- Acknowledge barriers mentioned in the patient context.
- Do not invent clinical details not provided below.

### Vocabulary

- Use "blood sugar" not "glucose"
- Use "blood pressure" not "BP"
- Use "heart rate" not "HR"
- Use the simplest accurate word available

## How to customize

| Element | How to Adjust |
|---------|---------------|
| Specific diet | Add: "Focus on [Mediterranean / DASH / low-carb / renal] diet recommendations" |
| Heart failure | Add: "Include fluid restriction and daily weight monitoring. Sodium limit: *** mg/day." |
| Budget constraints | Add: "The patient has a limited food budget. Recommend affordable, accessible foods. Include frozen and canned options." |
| Cultural foods | Add: "Incorporate [CULTURE]-appropriate food examples and dietary patterns" |
| Elderly / mobility | Add: "Account for limited mobility. Focus on seated exercises, fall prevention, and balance." |
| Motivational framing | Add: "Use motivational interviewing language. Emphasize autonomy and patient choice." |
| Comorbidities | Add: "Patient also has [COMORBIDITIES]. Ensure recommendations are safe for all conditions." |
| Shift work | Add: "Patient works [SHIFT SCHEDULE]. Adapt meal timing and exercise schedule to their work hours." |

- Write at a 6th grade reading level.

══════════════════════════════════════════

Why These Changes Matter for You
────────────────────────────────
You had a heart attack 3 weeks ago. Making changes
to your diet and activity protects your heart from
another event. These changes can lower your blood
pressure, bring down your cholesterol, and help
you feel stronger. Even small improvements, like
cutting back on salt and walking more, make a real
difference.

Diet Plan
─────────
  Foods to Eat More Of
  ────────────────────
  - Fruits and vegetables: at least 5 servings a day
    (1 serving = 1 cup raw vegetables or 1 medium
    piece of fruit)
  - Whole grains: oatmeal, brown rice, whole wheat
    bread instead of white
  - Lean protein: chicken, fish, beans, lentils
  - Healthy fats: olive oil, avocado, a small handful
    of nuts

  Foods to Eat Less Of
  ────────────────────
  - Salt: less than 2,000 mg per day (about 1 teaspoon).
    Check labels on canned soups, deli meat, and frozen
    meals.
  - Sugary drinks: soda, sweet tea, juice. Switch to
    water, unsweetened tea, or water with lemon.
  - Fried foods and fast food: once a week or less.
  - Red and processed meat: limit to 2 times a week.

  Sample Day of Meals
  ───────────────────
  Breakfast: Oatmeal with blueberries and a small
    handful of walnuts
  Lunch: Turkey sandwich on whole wheat with lettuce,
    tomato, mustard, and an apple
  Dinner: Baked salmon, steamed broccoli, and brown rice
  Snack: Baby carrots with hummus

  Practical Tips
  ──────────────
  - Cook extra on Sunday. Use leftovers for lunches.
  - When eating out, ask for dressing on the side and
    grilled instead of fried.
  - Keep cut vegetables in the fridge for easy snacking.

Exercise Plan
─────────────
  What to Do
  ──────────
  Type: Walking
  How often: 5 days per week
  How long: Build to 30 minutes per session
  How hard: You should be able to talk but not sing

  If You Are Starting from Zero
  ─────────────────────────────
  Week 1-2: Walk 10 minutes, twice a day, on flat ground
  Week 3-4: Walk 15 minutes, once a day
  Week 5+: Walk 20-30 minutes, once a day. Add gentle
    hills when comfortable.

  Safety
  ──────
  Stop and sit down if: You feel chest pain, pressure,
    dizziness, unusual shortness of breath, or nausea.
  Call your doctor if: Symptoms do not go away within
    5 minutes of stopping, or if they happen often.

Other Changes
─────────────
  Sleep
  ─────
  - Aim for 7-8 hours per night.
  - Go to bed and wake up at the same time each day.

  Stress
  ──────

    for 5 minutes.
  - Talk to your doctor if you feel anxious, sad, or
    overwhelmed. This is common after a heart attack.

  ───────

    thing you can do for your heart. Talk to your
    doctor about nicotine patches, gum, or medication
    that can help.

|-----------|---------------------------------|-----------------------|-----------------|

## Tips & pitfalls

- **Input quality determines output quality.** "Patient has hypertension" produces generic advice. "52-year-old man, eats fast food 5 times a week, works night shift, does not exercise, smokes half a pack daily, lives alone, budget is tight" produces a plan the patient might actually follow.
- **Review diet recommendations for safety.** Patients with diabetes, kidney disease, food allergies, or eating disorders need diet plans reviewed by a clinician who knows their full clinical picture. AI does not reliably catch renal potassium restrictions or carb-insulin interactions.
- **Avoid overly ambitious plans.** Patients are more likely to follow a plan with 2-3 small changes than one that overhauls their entire life on Monday. The weekly starter plan should reflect that.
- **Cultural dietary preferences matter.** A plan full of foods the patient does not eat will not be followed. Include the patient's typical cuisine in the prompt input.
- **Weight-related content requires sensitivity.** Focus on health behaviors and outcomes, not numbers on the scale. Frame changes as building healthy habits, not punishing bad ones.
- **Pair with the Follow-Up Visit Prep template.** The follow-up visit is where the patient reports how the plan is going, what worked, and what did not. The two templates function as a set.
- **Medication and lifestyle are not either/or.** Make sure the plan states clearly that lifestyle changes work alongside medications, not instead of them.
