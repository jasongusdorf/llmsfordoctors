In four endoscopy centers in Poland, gastroenterologists who had each performed thousands of colonoscopies got measurably worse at their job. Not because they aged, or tired, or stopped caring. Because they were given help.

The centers had joined a trial of AI-assisted colonoscopy, software that flags polyps on the screen in real time. Cases were randomized to AI or no AI. When researchers looked at the colonoscopies the same physicians performed *without* the AI, before the tool arrived and after, they found something nobody had measured in a clinic before. The rate at which these endoscopists detected adenomas on their own had fallen from 28.4% to 22.4%. A six-point absolute drop, about twenty percent of their unassisted skill, gone in roughly a year.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The study, published in *The Lancet Gastroenterology & Hepatology* in August 2025, is the first real-world clinical evidence of deskilling from medical AI. Adenoma detection rate is not an academic metric. Every 1% increase is associated with a measurable reduction in subsequent colorectal cancer. These physicians became worse at finding the lesions that kill patients.
</Callout>

The easy lesson is that AI is dangerous and we should be careful with it. That lesson is wrong, or at least so incomplete that it points us in exactly the wrong direction.

## The Tool Did Not Do This

Nothing about polyp-detection software erodes a human skill. The erosion came from what surrounded the software, which was nothing. The endoscopists were handed a second set of eyes and given no instruction on how to keep using their own. So they did what any of us would do when a confident assistant takes over a task: they relaxed into it. Attention drifted to the screen's green box. The habit of systematically interrogating every fold of mucosa, built over a career, quietly loosened, because for months nothing punished its absence.

This is not a story about machines replacing judgment. It is a story about judgment going unexercised because no one designed the work to keep exercising it. The deskilling was not a property of the AI. It was a property of the rollout: a powerful tool dropped into a clinical workflow with no thought given to what the human half of the partnership was supposed to be doing.

We have done this before, with less at stake. A generation of physicians can no longer estimate a corrected calcium or a Wells score without a phone, and mostly that is fine, because the phone is always there and the arithmetic was never the skill that mattered. Clinical reasoning is not arithmetic. The physician who cannot work through a differential without a model is not mildly inconvenienced when the model is wrong, or down, or confidently hallucinating. She is unable to catch it.

## The Same Tool, Pointed the Other Way

Now the other half of the evidence, which almost never gets told in the same breath, and should.

Take the [randomized trial of AI literacy](/trials/diagnostic-rct-ai-literacy-2026) conducted with 60 physicians. After a 20-hour curriculum on how to actually use a large language model for clinical reasoning, the physicians who then worked with an LLM improved their diagnostic reasoning scores from 42.6% to 71.4%. Not a modest bump. A near-doubling of measured reasoning quality, from the same underlying tool that deskilled the endoscopists.

The trial also produced the finding that should be taped to every program director's door. In one arm, the LLM answering cases *alone* outperformed the physician-plus-LLM teams across most cases. The untrained human in the loop made the output worse about two-thirds of the time. Access to the tool was not the intervention. Teaching people to use it was the intervention. Without the teaching, the human was a liability; with it, reasoning nearly doubled.

<Callout type="evidence">
  Put the two studies side by side. Same class of technology. One setting produced a 6-point loss of unassisted skill; the other produced a 29-point gain in reasoning. The difference between them was not the model. It was whether anyone taught the humans what to do.
</Callout>

So the question that dominates faculty meetings, "is AI going to deskill our trainees," is the wrong question. It has no answer, because it treats the outcome as a property of the technology. The honest question is the uncomfortable one: are we going to teach this, or are we going to let the curriculum write itself in the call room at 2 a.m.?

## We Do Not Leave Other Skills to Osmosis

Consider how we treat every other high-stakes clinical skill. We do not hand a resident a central line kit and a vein and wish her luck. There is a module, a simulation, a checklist, a supervised first attempt, a sign-off. We are exhaustive about procedures precisely because the cost of doing them badly is borne by patients.

The large language model is now the most powerful cognitive instrument to enter medicine in a generation, and our default posture toward it is the one thing we would never tolerate for a chest tube: we are letting people figure it out alone. Most medical students have already used these tools for coursework. Most of their schools have no formal curriculum for it. The adoption is universal and the instruction is absent, which is the precise recipe that deskilled the endoscopists, now running at the scale of an entire profession.

My colleague's earlier piece on this site, [The Best Tutor and the Worst Crib Sheet](/editorials/llms-in-medical-education), made the case at the level of the individual learner: the same model is a brilliant Socratic tutor or a hollowing-out crib sheet depending on the student's relationship to difficulty. That is true. But we cannot keep locating the entire burden in the eighteen-year-old's character. A learner's relationship to difficulty is not fixed at birth. It is shaped, or left unshaped, by the people who train her. That shaping is the job. It is ours.

## What Teaching It Actually Means

The good news is that the curriculum is not mysterious, and it is not twenty new lectures. It is a small number of habits, taught early and assessed seriously.

Teach trainees to produce their own answer before they ask the model, so the tool grades their reasoning instead of replacing it. Teach them to make the model argue against them rather than for them. Teach them to chase mechanism, not conclusions, to ask "why" until the model reaches the edge of what it actually knows. Teach them, above all, the difference between using an LLM to think harder and using it to avoid thinking, because the two look almost identical from the outside and produce opposite physicians. The companion to this piece, our guide on [learning with an LLM without deskilling yourself](/guides/learning-with-llms), lays out the specific techniques.

<Callout type="tip">
  The single most useful instruction we can give a trainee fits in one sentence: make the model work you, not work for you. Answer first, then let it attack your answer. A student who internalizes that one habit is upskilled by the same tool that deskills the student who does not.
</Callout>

And we have to change what we reward. As long as the assessment is a written note or a multiple-choice exam, the AI-polished answer and the hard-won one are indistinguishable, and we are quietly training people to produce the former. The fix is old and unglamorous: ask the resident to reason out loud, on the spot, laptop closed. Oral examination, bedside Socratic questioning, the demand to defend a plan in real time. These were never about cruelty. They were always the only assessments that can tell whether the understanding is in the trainee or in the tool.

## The Choice

The endoscopists in Poland did not choose to get worse. The system around them chose for them, by treating a profound change in how the work gets done as an IT deployment rather than an educational event. That is the mistake we are on track to repeat, at scale, with the physicians we are training right now.

Deskilling is not what the machine does to us. It is what happens when we let the machine change the work and decline to change the teaching. That part was always within our control. The tool will do whatever the curriculum tells it to. The only question is whether we write one.

*Jason G. Gusdorf, MD*
