## Study Design

A committee of researchers and clinicians at the Symposium on AI for Learning Health Systems (SAIL) 2025 reviewed clinical AI developments from 2024 to 2025 across six themes: clinical documentation, clinical decision support, insurance denials, clinician-AI interaction, AI regulation, and healthcare economics.

---

## Key Findings

- **Ambient documentation** is the most widely adopted clinical AI application, with universal adoption activities reported across 43 surveyed health systems
- An AI ECG alert system demonstrated a **17% reduction in 90-day mortality** in hospitalized patients (3.6% vs 4.3%, HR 0.83)
- Real-time wearable sensor data led to **38% fewer heart failure rehospitalizations** in 90 days
- A multicenter AI intracranial hemorrhage triage trial showed **no improvement** in sensitivity, specificity, or turnaround times
- AI-assisted mammography screening found **17.6% more breast cancers** without raising false recalls (PRAIM study)
- Health insurers are **using AI to autodeny claims** with minimal human review, prompting lawsuits
- Burnout dropped from **77% to 51%** in one multicenter cohort after AI scribe implementation
- A large longitudinal study showed **no significant reduction in documentation time** from ambient AI despite user satisfaction

<Callout type="evidence">
  2024-2025 was the year AI moved from research to implementation at scale. Some applications deliver clear patient benefits (ECG mortality reduction). Others show no improvement (hemorrhage triage). The evidence demands nuance, not enthusiasm.
</Callout>

---

## Clinical Implications

1. **Documentation AI is adopted but unproven.** Near-universal adoption based on user satisfaction, despite inconsistent evidence of actual time savings or efficiency gains. Institutions are buying enthusiasm, not evidence.

2. **Decision support has breakthrough results, sometimes.** The ECG mortality reduction and heart failure rehospitalization results change practice. The hemorrhage triage failure shows that retrospective accuracy doesn't guarantee real-world impact.

3. **AI in insurance is a growing crisis.** AI-driven claim denials, patients flagged by AI but denied coverage, and the emerging "financial toxicity" of AI-enabled diagnosis are issues the clinical community needs to confront.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  The review highlights that AI-generated documentation hallucinations are a real safety concern, and that clinician automation bias (uncritically accepting AI output) compounds the risk. As AI notes become the norm, the medical record may become less trustworthy.
</Callout>

4. **Clinician-AI collaboration depends on context.** AI-assisted mammography shows clear synergy; AI-assisted diagnostic reasoning shows uncertain benefit. The "doctor plus AI" equation is task-dependent, not universally positive.

<Callout type="tip" title="What to do with this">
  **If your institution is adopting ambient AI:** Ask for time-savings data, not just satisfaction scores. The evidence shows clinicians like these tools but may not actually document faster. Demand before-and-after metrics on documentation time, note turnaround, and pajama time.

  **If you're evaluating clinical decision support tools:** Prioritize tools with prospective trial evidence (like the ECG alert study) over retrospective accuracy claims (like the hemorrhage triage tool that failed in practice).

  **If patients ask about AI-denied insurance claims:** Know that this is an active area of litigation. Document your clinical reasoning thoroughly; AI denial systems are pattern-matchers that struggle with atypical presentations.
</Callout>

---

## My Takeaway

The tools that work (ECG alerts, structured screening) are specific, well-defined, and integrated into clear clinical workflows. The tools that struggle (documentation time savings, hemorrhage triage) are broader, more ambiguous, or deployed without supporting workflow changes. Evaluate AI tools by rigorous outcomes, not adoption rates or user satisfaction.
