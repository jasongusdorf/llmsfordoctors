Somewhere in the spring of 2024, an internist in Boston typed a differential diagnosis into ChatGPT during a night shift, checked it against her own reasoning, and decided not to tell anyone. By early 2026, there are randomized controlled trials, large-scale EHR analyses, deployment safety audits, and real-world cohort studies tracking exactly what she did and whether it helped. The field went from anecdote to evidence in under two years, which is fast enough to make you wonder whether the evidence can keep up with the adoption.

The trajectory is worth mapping. Early 2025 was dominated by foundation-level medical QA benchmarks and clinician-in-the-loop RCTs. By late 2025, the field shifted to large-scale EHR cohort extraction and workflow automation. Early 2026 brought deployment safety audits, AI-literacy as a measurable intervention, and governance frameworks. The questions got harder. That is, on the whole, a good sign.

This review covers peer-reviewed studies from Nature Medicine, NEJM AI, JAMA, and their companion journals. It excludes medical education, consumer chatbots, and administrative applications. Citation counts appear as a rough proxy for influence, not quality: a highly cited study may be controversial rather than correct, but citation counts tell you which studies are shaping the conversation.

## Theme 1: Clinician Performance in Controlled Trials

The fundamental question has always been simple. Does giving a physician access to an LLM make them better at their job? For a long time, the honest answer was "probably, but we're guessing." Now there is trial data. The answer is yes, with caveats that deserve their own paragraphs.

### The benchmark that started the modern era

Med-PaLM 2, published in Nature Medicine in 2025, scored 86.5% on MedQA and established the template every subsequent study has followed: a standardized benchmark score, a clinician rubric evaluation, and a grounding strategy to reduce hallucination. Physicians preferred Med-PaLM 2's answers over physician-generated answers on 8 of 9 quality axes, which is the kind of finding that makes you read it twice.

<Callout type="evidence">
  Med-PaLM 2 has been cited over 700 times. Its significance isn't the score alone; it's the evaluation framework. Before this study, there was no consensus on how to assess clinical LLM output. The combination of automated benchmarks and structured physician review became the standard approach.
</Callout>

A benchmark score, though, is an audition, not a performance. Med-PaLM 2 proved that LLMs could pass a very particular kind of test. The studies that followed asked the question that actually matters: what happens when these tools meet the mess of real clinical reasoning?

### Why the framework matters more than the number

What Med-PaLM 2 gave the field was a common language. Its three-layer evaluation framework (automated benchmark, clinician rubric, grounding strategy) meant that, for the first time, different research groups could compare results without arguing about methodology.

The benchmark layer (MedQA, MedMCQA, PubMedQA) tests factual medical knowledge in a controlled, reproducible format. The clinician rubric layer asks whether real physicians find the output useful, accurate, and safe. The grounding strategy asks how the model handles uncertainty: does it cite sources, flag low-confidence answers, or hallucinate with the confidence of a third-year resident presenting on rounds?

Every major study since has used some version of this framework. When you read a new clinical AI paper, look for all three layers. A study reporting only benchmark scores is incomplete. A study reporting only physician preference without benchmarks is anecdotal. You need both, plus an explicit assessment of failure modes, and if any layer is missing, the paper has a hole in it.

### The first large management reasoning RCT

Goh et al. (2025, Nature Medicine) randomized 92 physicians to manage clinical cases with or without GPT-4 access. The LLM-assisted group scored 6.5 percentage points higher on management reasoning, a statistically significant and clinically meaningful improvement.

The catch: they also took approximately 119 seconds longer per case.

The study design matters more than the headline. Physicians weren't handed a pre-generated AI answer to rubber-stamp. They had access to GPT-4 as a consultation tool while working through clinical vignettes: asking questions, requesting differentials, checking management guidelines, or ignoring the model entirely. This is closer to how clinical AI will actually be used. Not a replacement for reasoning, but an on-demand second opinion that never gets annoyed at being asked.

<Callout type="evidence">
  This is the first large RCT demonstrating measurable improvement in physician management reasoning with LLM assistance. The effect size is roughly equivalent to moving from an average performer to a good performer, meaningful in aggregate across thousands of clinical decisions.
</Callout>

Those two extra minutes need context. In a 15-minute primary care visit, 119 seconds is a 13% productivity hit. In an ED running at capacity, it might not be feasible at all. The question is not just "does it help?" but "does it help enough to justify the workflow cost?" The answer depends heavily on whether you're sitting in a quiet outpatient office or standing in a trauma bay.

There is a deeper question the study can only gesture at. Does the time cost decrease with experience? Goh et al. measured physicians on their first use of LLM assistance. It is plausible that experienced users learn to query more efficiently, evaluate output more quickly, and integrate AI into their workflow with less friction. But there is no longitudinal data yet, and that gap matters. (For more detail on the Goh et al. RCT, see our [trial summary](/trials/gpt4-physician-management-rct-2025).)

<Callout type="tip">
  When interpreting the time-cost findings from AI-assisted reasoning studies, remember that these are typically first-exposure measurements. Experienced users may be faster. But don't assume the time cost disappears; plan for it in workflow design, and measure it in your own deployment.
</Callout>

### What twenty hours of training buys you

Qazi et al. (2026, Nature Health) studied 60 physicians in a low- and middle-income country setting. The design included a 20-hour AI-literacy curriculum before the diagnostic accuracy trial, which is a detail that changes everything about the results.

The numbers tell a clean story:
- **Physician alone:** 42.6% diagnostic accuracy
- **Physician + LLM (with AI-literacy training):** 71.4% diagnostic accuracy
- **LLM alone (no physician):** higher than physician + LLM on average

<Callout type="evidence">
  The Qazi et al. trial makes two points that deserve separate consideration. First, AI-literacy training is part of the intervention, not an optional add-on. Physicians who understood how the LLM works, where it fails, and how to evaluate its output got dramatically better results. Second, the finding that LLM-alone outperformed physician+LLM on average raises uncomfortable questions about whether human-in-the-loop always adds value, or whether it sometimes introduces noise.
</Callout>

That last finding is the one people latch onto, and it needs careful handling. It does not mean physicians should step aside. The average masks the variation that matters: physicians caught errors the LLM made confidently, and made errors the LLM would not have. The combination is most valuable at the tails of the distribution, not at the mean. What it does suggest is that "just add a human" is not an automatic safety improvement. It can be. It is not always.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Do not interpret "LLM-alone outperformed physician+LLM" as evidence that physicians are unnecessary. Average performance hides the cases where physician judgment catches catastrophic AI errors. The goal is calibrated collaboration: knowing when to trust the model and when to override it. That's exactly what AI-literacy training teaches.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** Start building your own AI-literacy now. Learn the basics of how LLMs generate text, practice recognizing hallucinations, and develop a habit of verifying AI output against your own clinical reasoning. Our [Prompting 101](/guides/prompting-101) guide is a practical starting point. For a head-to-head look at how different models handle the same clinical case, see [Which LLM for Clinical Reasoning?](/editorials/llm-clinical-reasoning-comparison).
</Callout>

## Theme 2: Safety and Adoption in Real Deployments

Controlled trials are clean. Production systems are not. The question shifts from "does this work under study conditions?" to "what happens when LLMs run against real patient records, in real EHR workflows, in clinics where nobody has time to read the methods section?"

### Sixteen clinics in Kenya

Agweyu et al. (2026, Nature Health) audited an EMR-embedded clinical decision support system across 16 clinics in Kenya, reviewing 1,469 clinical records ([trial summary](/trials/emr-cds-safety-kenya-2026)). The headline numbers are reassuring: guideline alignment was 99%, and the hallucination rate was 3.4%.

Buried deeper in the data: actively harmful recommendations appeared in 7.8% of encounters.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  This is the study that crystallized a critical lesson: utility and harm don't "average out." A system can be 99% guideline-aligned and still produce dangerous output in nearly 1 in 13 patient encounters. In deployment, you need to evaluate the tails of the distribution, not just the mean.
</Callout>

Think about what 7.8% means in a clinic seeing 100 patients per day. That is roughly 8 encounters where the AI suggestion could cause harm. If the system is designed so that accepting a recommendation is easier than rejecting one (and it usually is; this is the default in most CDS interfaces), those harmful suggestions have an outsized chance of being acted upon.

This is the friction asymmetry problem. One click to accept. Active cognitive effort to reject. In a busy clinic, that asymmetry is not a design quirk. It is a safety hazard.

<Callout type="tip">
  When evaluating any AI-CDS system, ask: "What does it cost the clinician to reject a suggestion?" If the answer is "more effort than accepting it," the system has a design-level safety problem regardless of its accuracy metrics.
</Callout>

### The denominator problem

It is tempting to dismiss 7.8% as a small number. Consider the analogy: if a pharmacist told you that a medication safety system flags the right drug 99% of the time but dispenses a potentially dangerous drug 7.8% of the time, no hospital would adopt it. The high alignment rate and the harmful recommendation rate are measuring different things. One measures routine performance. The other measures tail risk. They are not interchangeable, and the second one is the one that shows up in morbidity and mortality conferences.

Safety-critical systems are judged by their worst failures, not their average successes. Aviation does not report that 99.99% of flights land safely and call it a day. It investigates every incident. Clinical AI needs the same posture.

The Kenya study also revealed where harm concentrates. Harmful recommendations were not distributed uniformly across patient types. They clustered around complex presentations, atypical symptom combinations, and cases where the AI's training data was thin. The patients most likely to receive harmful recommendations were the patients who needed the most careful clinical attention, which is, if you think about it, exactly the wrong way around.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Harmful AI recommendations are not randomly distributed. They cluster around the edge cases: complex patients, rare presentations, unusual medication combinations. These are exactly the cases where physicians are most likely to seek decision support. The tool is least reliable precisely when it would be most valuable.
</Callout>

### A pattern across deployments

The Kenya study is not an outlier. Across the 2025-2026 literature, LLM-based CDS systems perform well on average metrics while harboring failure modes that surface only under specific conditions: unusual presentations, rare medications, edge-case demographics, combinations of comorbidities underrepresented in training data. The average looks fine. The edges do not.

Several additional studies reinforce the theme:

- **ED triage evaluation (2025):** ChatGPT and Copilot were tested for emergency department triage classification. Both showed reasonable accuracy on straightforward presentations but exhibited concerning bias patterns, undertriaging certain demographic groups and overtriaging others. In triage, a single misstep cascades: a wrong triage level affects resource allocation, wait time, and the clinical team's initial framing of the case. The error does not stay at the front door.

- **Prescribing error CDS (2025):** LLMs were tested as a medication safety screening layer, checking orders for drug interactions, dosing errors, and contraindications. Performance was strong on common interactions and degraded on complex polypharmacy, which is precisely where CDS earns its keep. A particular weakness emerged: the model struggled with weight-based dosing adjustments in patients with renal or hepatic impairment, a scenario that requires integrating multiple patient parameters simultaneously.

- **Bias in retinal disease recommendations (2026):** LLM-generated treatment recommendations for retinal disease varied based on patient demographic and socioeconomic information embedded in the prompt, even when the clinical presentation was identical. When patient race, insurance status, or zip code changes the model's treatment recommendation for the same fundoscopic findings, the model has learned the healthcare system's disparities and is reproducing them at scale.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Bias in LLM clinical recommendations is not hypothetical. When demographic or socioeconomic details are included in a prompt, they can shift the model's clinical recommendations even when they shouldn't. This is a systemic problem that cannot be fixed by prompt engineering alone; it requires careful evaluation, monitoring, and audit at the deployment level.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** Before trusting any AI-CDS tool in your workflow, ask two questions: (1) What is the measured harm rate, not the accuracy rate? (2) How hard is it for a clinician to reject a suggestion versus accept one? If your vendor cannot answer the first question or the answer to the second is "harder," push back. See our [HIPAA Compliance guide](/guides/hipaa-compliance) for the broader vendor evaluation framework.
</Callout>

## Theme 3: Security and Robustness Failure Modes

Deployment safety concerns what goes wrong in normal operation. Security concerns what goes wrong when someone actively introduces bad information. The distinction matters because the failure modes are different, and so are the defenses.

### Poisoned knowledge

Alber et al. (2025, Nature Medicine) demonstrated that LLMs can ingest and reproduce false medical information planted in their training data. The attack is disarmingly simple: introduce fabricated medical claims into the pretraining corpus, and the model learns to repeat them as fact.

The finding that should keep you up at night: standard medical benchmarks did not detect the poisoned knowledge. A model could score well on MedQA while confidently providing dangerous misinformation on the specific topics that were poisoned.

<Callout type="evidence">
  With over 200 citations, Alber et al. is the study that made "benchmarks are not equal to safety" a consensus position in the field. A model that scores 90% on a medical QA benchmark may still contain pockets of confidently wrong knowledge that no benchmark tests for. This has direct implications for regulatory evaluation: passing a benchmark suite is not sufficient evidence of safety.
</Callout>

This is not an academic exercise. Medical LLMs train on data scraped from the internet: medical forums, preprint servers, social media. The barrier to injecting false medical information into these sources is low. And unlike a wrong answer the model is uncertain about (which can sometimes be caught by uncertainty quantification), poisoned knowledge produces high-confidence wrong answers. The model is not guessing. It is certain. It is also wrong.

### How the attack works

An adversary publishes a series of plausible-looking medical articles, forum posts, or preprint manuscripts containing a specific false claim. Say, that a particular drug interaction is safe when it is actually dangerous, or that a specific symptom pattern indicates a benign condition when it is an emergency.

These documents get scraped into training data. The model learns the false claim as fact. When a physician later asks about the topic, the model responds with high confidence because it has seen the claim repeated across multiple "independent" sources. The physician, seeing a confident and well-articulated response, has no easy way to distinguish this from a correct answer.

Alber et al. demonstrated this experimentally, not hypothetically. The false claims survived standard medical benchmarking because the poisoned topics were not covered by the benchmark questions. An adversary can target their poisoning to specific clinical domains that benchmarks do not test. The lock and the key do not occupy the same door.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  Data poisoning attacks have implications beyond accuracy. If a compromised model is used in clinical decision-making, and the poisoned output leads to patient harm, the liability chain (from model developer to vendor to institution to individual clinician) is untested in most jurisdictions. Institutions deploying clinical AI should consult legal counsel about liability exposure from model-level failures.
</Callout>

### What this means for how we validate

The data poisoning risk changes the validation calculus in four ways:

1. **Benchmark suites are necessary but not sufficient.** You also need adversarial testing on institution-specific clinical scenarios.
2. **Provenance matters.** Models trained on curated, source-verified medical data are more trustworthy than models trained on open web scrapes, but also more expensive to build.
3. **Continuous monitoring is required.** A model that was safe at deployment can become unsafe if fine-tuned on contaminated data.
4. **Open-source model inspection is a safety advantage.** You can audit training data and model weights for open models. You cannot do this for proprietary APIs.

<Callout type="tip">
  When evaluating medical LLM vendors, ask about training data provenance and curation. "Trained on internet data" is not a reassuring answer for clinical use. Look for models trained on curated medical corpora with documented quality control processes.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** Never use a single benchmark score as evidence that a model is safe for clinical use. When your institution evaluates an LLM tool, insist on adversarial testing against your own clinical scenarios, not just vendor-supplied benchmarks. For literature-backed questions, use a search-augmented model like [Perplexity](/tools/perplexity) to verify claims against primary sources.
</Callout>

## Theme 4: Workflow Automation

The controlled trials and safety audits generate the most discussion at conferences. The workflow applications generate the most value in clinics. This section is about the second category: using LLMs to reduce the documentation and communication burden that consumes a large fraction of physician time and produces very little of physician satisfaction.

### The blank-page problem, solved

Small et al. (2025, JAMA Network Open) compared LLM-generated hospital course summaries with physician-generated summaries. The key metric: physicians edited 31.5% of LLM-generated summaries versus 44.8% of physician-generated summaries from other physicians.

<Callout type="evidence">
  LLM-generated hospital course summaries required less editing than summaries written by other physicians. This doesn't mean the AI writes better summaries; it means the AI produces a consistent, structured starting point that's efficient to refine. The value isn't replacing the physician's judgment but eliminating the blank-page problem.
</Callout>

The pattern is elegant in its modesty. The LLM does not need to be perfect. It needs to produce a draft that is faster to edit than to write from scratch. For documentation tasks (discharge summaries, progress notes, procedure notes, referral letters), the evidence increasingly suggests it clears that bar. The AI handles structure and routine language. The physician adds judgment, corrects inaccuracies, and ensures the final product reflects what actually happened to the patient.

A subtlety: the 31.5% edit rate was measured against a specific quality standard. Physicians were actively reviewing and correcting AI output, not waving it through. This is human-in-the-loop working as intended.

The risk to watch for is what happens next. As physicians grow accustomed to AI-generated drafts, will editing vigilance decline? If the first 50 AI summaries are mostly correct, will the physician still catch the error in summary 51? This is the automation complacency problem, well-documented in aviation and manufacturing, and there is no reason to believe medicine is immune. Documentation applications should include periodic quality audits to monitor for this drift.

### The inbox, unburdened

A 2025 study evaluated LLM-assisted drafting of patient portal message responses, the inbox work that consistently ranks among physicians' top sources of burnout. The system generated draft responses for physicians to review and edit before sending.

The results showed meaningful time savings with high physician satisfaction. The crucial design decision: the system drafted responses but never sent them automatically. The physician remained the final author of every message.

This design choice (draft but never send) is a template for safe clinical AI deployment. It preserves physician authority, maintains the physician-patient relationship, and creates a natural quality checkpoint. The mechanical work of composing a well-structured response belongs to the machine. The clinical judgment of whether the content is right for this particular patient belongs to the physician.

The scale of the inbox burden is worth stating plainly. Primary care physicians routinely receive 50 to 100 or more patient portal messages per day. Many require chart review, clinical assessment, and a thoughtful response. This work is typically uncompensated and happens outside of scheduled clinic time. Even a 30% time savings on message drafting translates to a meaningful reduction in the after-hours work that drives physicians out of the profession.

<Callout type="tip">
  Patient message drafting is one of the highest-value, lowest-risk LLM applications available today. The physician reviews every response before it's sent, the task is well-defined, and the time savings are immediate. If your organization is looking for a starting point for clinical AI, this is a strong candidate.
</Callout>

### Research at a scale that was not previously possible

Pak et al. (2025, JAMA Network Open) used LLaMA 3 8B to extract sepsis symptoms from clinical notes across a cohort of 104,248 patients. The model achieved 99.3% accuracy on symptom extraction, turning unstructured clinical text into structured research data at health-system scale.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The Pak et al. study represents a use case that gets less attention than clinical decision support but may be equally transformative: LLMs as research instrumentation. Manually abstracting symptoms from 104,000 patient records would require years of human effort. An LLM did it with 99.3% accuracy. This enables cohort studies, quality improvement analyses, and epidemiological research that were previously impractical.
</Callout>

The application (LLMs as structured data extractors from unstructured notes) has several features that make it unusually well-suited to the technology:

- **The task is well-defined.** Extract specific data elements from text. This is narrower than open-ended clinical reasoning by orders of magnitude.
- **The output is verifiable.** You can audit a random sample of extractions against human review.
- **The risk profile is lower.** Errors in research data extraction are serious but do not directly affect individual patient care the way a wrong CDS recommendation does.
- **The scale advantage is enormous.** No other approach can process six-figure patient cohorts from free-text notes at this speed.

For context: traditional manual chart abstraction for research studies typically costs $15 to $50 per record and requires trained abstractors working for weeks or months. An LLM processing 104,000 records completes the extraction in hours at a fraction of the cost. Validation is still necessary (you still need human review of a sample to verify accuracy), but the economics of clinical research shift fundamentally when the bottleneck of data extraction disappears.

### Accelerating the trial pipeline

Unlu et al. (2025, JAMA) evaluated the RECTIFIER system, a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) approach to clinical trial eligibility prescreening. The AI group identified eligible patients more rapidly than the manual screening group.

Clinical trial recruitment is a bottleneck that delays medical research across every specialty. Most eligible patients are never identified because manual chart review cannot keep pace with enrollment needs. The numbers are sobering: up to 80% of clinical trials fail to meet enrollment timelines, and 30% fail entirely due to insufficient enrollment. Every month of delay costs an average of $600,000 to $8 million depending on the therapeutic area. If RAG-based prescreening can accelerate enrollment even modestly (identifying eligible patients days or weeks earlier), the downstream impact on research timelines is substantial.

RECTIFIER's architecture is worth examining. Rather than relying solely on the LLM's training data, which may be outdated or incomplete on specific trial criteria, it retrieves relevant information from the trial protocol and the patient's medical record at query time. This grounds the eligibility assessment in the actual documents rather than the model's parametric knowledge, reducing hallucination risk on a task where accuracy matters enormously.

<Callout type="tip">
  Trial eligibility prescreening is a high-value application that doesn't require the model to make clinical decisions, just to match patient characteristics against inclusion/exclusion criteria. The human investigator still makes the enrollment decision. This makes it one of the lower-risk, higher-impact applications in the current landscape.
</Callout>

### Other workflow studies worth knowing

Several other workflow automation studies from 2025 complete the picture:

- **Clinical outcome adjudication (2025):** Fu-LLM was evaluated for adjudicating clinical outcomes in trials, classifying events like myocardial infarction or stroke from clinical records. In large trials, outcome adjudication can involve thousands of medical records reviewed by clinical events committees. LLM pre-classification could reduce that workload by an order of magnitude, another trials operations bottleneck where the model accelerates human review without replacing it.

The thread running through all of these is a design pattern worth internalizing: **AI generates, human validates.** The model produces a first draft, extraction, or classification. A qualified human reviews it before it has clinical consequences. The pattern works because it plays to the strengths of both parties: the model's speed and consistency, the human's judgment and accountability. (For a detailed look at the ambient scribe evidence, see our [DAX/Nabla RCT summary](/trials/ambient-ai-scribes-dax-nabla-rct-2025).)

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** Pick one documentation task and try it this week. Discharge summaries are the best starting point: the format is predictable, the output is easy to verify, and the time savings are immediate. See our [LLMs in Clinical Care 101](/guides/llms-in-clinical-care-101) guide for the step-by-step workflow and the [Discharge Summary template](/templates/discharge-summary-basic) for a ready-to-use prompt.
</Callout>

## Theme 5: Specialty-Domain Validation

As LLMs matured, specialty-specific validation studies proliferated. The results vary by domain, and the variation tells you something.

### Radiology: translation, not diagnosis

Radiology report simplification studies in 2025 showed that LLMs could translate technical radiology reports into patient-friendly language with improved comprehension scores. This is a communication task, not a diagnostic one; the LLM is not reading images, it is translating text.

The clinical value is straightforward. Patients increasingly access their radiology reports through patient portals. A report that says "There is a 1.2 cm hypodense lesion in segment VII of the liver, likely representing a simple hepatic cyst" is meaningless to most patients, or worse, anxiety-inducing. An LLM-generated plain-language summary ("A small, likely harmless fluid-filled sac was found in your liver. This is very common and usually requires no treatment.") can reduce unnecessary worry and phone calls to the clinic.

<Callout type="tip">
  Report simplification for patients is one of the safer specialty applications because the source material (the radiologist's report) is already finalized. The LLM is performing translation, not interpretation. The original report remains the clinical document of record.
</Callout>

The key risk is oversimplification: stripping away clinical nuance in a way that is technically inaccurate. "Likely benign" is not "definitely benign," and the simplified version needs to preserve that distinction. Studies so far show that LLMs handle this reasonably well, but institution-specific validation still matters.

### Pathology: formatting is the enemy

A 2025 study tested LLM analysis of 579 pathology reports, evaluating the model's ability to extract structured data (diagnosis, grade, stage, margin status) from narrative pathology text. The model performed well on common cancer types and standard reporting formats, then degraded on unusual histologic subtypes and non-standard report structures.

This degradation pattern is consistent across specialties. LLMs work best on common, well-represented conditions and struggle on rare ones. For pathology specifically, the challenge is that report formatting varies enormously between institutions. A model trained on one institution's pathology reports may struggle with another's, even when the clinical content is equivalent. The bottleneck is not medical knowledge. It is the sheer variety of ways humans write things down.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The 579-report pathology study highlights a common theme: LLM performance on specialty tasks is highly dependent on how well the model's training data represents the specific format and terminology used at the deployment institution. Out-of-the-box performance on standardized reports doesn't predict performance on your institution's reports. Local validation is essential.
</Callout>

### Mental health: the human disappears from the loop

The GenAI mental health chatbot RCT (Heinz et al., 2025, NEJM AI) was the first RCT of a fully generative AI therapy chatbot for patients with clinical-level mental health symptoms. Not a rule-based chatbot. Not a guided self-help tool. An LLM generating therapeutic responses in real time, with no clinician between the model and the patient.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The Heinz et al. trial, with over 260 citations, represents a conceptual shift: the LLM moves from "clinical assistant" to "therapeutic intervention." This carries fundamentally different regulatory, ethical, and safety implications. An assistant that helps a physician draft a note is a productivity tool. A chatbot that delivers therapy directly to patients is a medical device, and needs to be evaluated as one.
</Callout>

The trial showed promising results on symptom reduction measures. The implications go well beyond efficacy data. If a generative AI chatbot delivers therapy, who is responsible when it says the wrong thing? How do you monitor for harmful outputs in a private conversation between a patient and a machine? What happens when a patient in crisis receives a response that is technically appropriate on average but catastrophically wrong for their specific situation?

Consider the failure mode concretely. A patient with suicidal ideation interacts with a GenAI therapy chatbot. The chatbot generates a response appropriate for mild depression but catastrophically inadequate for acute suicidal crisis. No therapist reviews the response before it reaches the patient. No clinical judgment mediates between the model's output and the patient's experience. The safety net that exists in every other clinical AI application (human review before action) is absent by design.

This does not mean GenAI therapy chatbots should never be developed. The mental health treatment gap is real, especially in underserved communities. It means the safety bar should be higher than for clinician-facing tools, not lower, and the monitoring infrastructure more robust, not less.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Direct-to-patient generative AI therapy is in a different risk category than clinician-facing tools. There is no human in the loop to catch errors. The patient is the least equipped person to evaluate whether the AI's therapeutic guidance is appropriate. Regulatory frameworks for this use case are still being developed; proceed with extreme caution.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** If your specialty is evaluating LLM tools, insist on validation with your own institution's data and formats. Out-of-the-box performance on standardized reports does not predict performance on your institution's reports. Start with low-risk communication tasks (report simplification, patient-facing summaries) rather than diagnostic applications.
</Callout>

## The Open-Source Inflection Point

For years, the assumption was that clinical AI meant sending patient data to an API owned by a company in San Francisco. That assumption is breaking.

Sandmann et al. (2025, Nature Medicine) tested DeepSeek and other open-source models against proprietary models on 125 clinical cases. The finding: open-source models performed "equally well and in some cases better" than proprietary alternatives.

<Callout type="evidence">
  Open-source parity on clinical benchmarks is not just an academic curiosity; it's a regulatory and procurement inflection point. Healthcare organizations operating under data sovereignty requirements, HIPAA constraints, or institutional policies that prohibit sending patient data to external APIs now have a viable path to clinical AI adoption using locally hosted models.
</Callout>

The implications are practical, not theoretical:

1. **Data stays local.** No patient data leaves the institution's infrastructure. This eliminates an entire category of privacy and compliance risk.
2. **Auditability.** You can inspect model weights, training data documentation, and behavior under adversarial conditions. With a proprietary API, you are trusting the vendor's claims.
3. **Cost predictability.** API pricing can change without notice. A locally hosted model has fixed infrastructure costs.
4. **Customization.** Open models can be fine-tuned on institutional data: your own clinical notes, your own guidelines, your own formulary.

The trade-offs are real. Local hosting requires infrastructure and ML engineering expertise. Models may lag behind proprietary frontier models on the hardest reasoning tasks. The institution takes on maintenance responsibility. But for many clinical applications (documentation, extraction, CDS with human review), the open-source path is now viable. The door that was locked a year ago is open.

### What local hosting actually requires

For institutions considering this path, some practical realities:

**Hardware.** Running a model like LLaMA 3 8B locally requires GPU infrastructure, typically one or more NVIDIA A100 or H100 GPUs for inference at clinical-grade latency. This is a significant but manageable capital expense for a health system, and it is a one-time infrastructure investment rather than an ongoing per-query API cost.

**Model selection.** Not all open-source models are equivalent. The Sandmann et al. benchmarks provide a starting point, but institutions should validate on their own clinical scenarios. Performance on 125 standardized cases does not guarantee performance on your specific patient population and clinical workflows.

**Maintenance.** When you host your own model, you own updates, security patches, and performance monitoring. With a proprietary API, the vendor handles this. Some institutions will prefer the control. Others will prefer the convenience. Both are valid, with different risk profiles.

**Regulatory uncertainty.** Using a locally hosted open-source model may simplify some regulatory questions (data stays on-premises) while complicating others (who is the "manufacturer" of an open-source model for FDA purposes?). This is evolving territory.

<Callout type="tip">
  If your institution has been waiting for clinical AI because of data privacy concerns about proprietary APIs, the 2025 open-source benchmarks are worth a close look. Models like LLaMA 3 and DeepSeek can be hosted on institutional infrastructure with no external data transfer.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** If data privacy has been your institution's reason for not adopting clinical AI, bring the Sandmann et al. benchmarks to your next IT governance meeting. Open-source models hosted on local infrastructure eliminate the external data transfer problem entirely. For a practical overview of which models perform best on which clinical tasks (including open-source options), see [Which LLM for Clinical Reasoning?](/editorials/llm-clinical-reasoning-comparison). For the compliance framework that governs any deployment, see the [HIPAA Compliance Guide](/guides/hipaa-compliance).

## Cross-Cutting Lessons

Five lessons cut across the entire 2025-2026 evidence base. They are worth stating together because they reinforce each other.

### Benchmarks are not safety

A model can score 90% on MedQA and still:
- Reproduce poisoned medical knowledge with high confidence (Alber et al.)
- Generate harmful recommendations in 7.8% of real clinical encounters (Agweyu et al.)
- Exhibit demographic bias in treatment recommendations (retinal disease study)

Benchmarks measure average-case performance on a fixed set of questions. Safety requires evaluating worst-case performance on the cases your patients will actually present with. These are fundamentally different evaluation targets, and conflating them is the most common mistake in clinical AI procurement.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Never use benchmark scores alone as evidence that a medical LLM is safe for clinical use. Require adversarial testing, bias audits, deployment monitoring, and incident reporting. "It scored well on MedQA" is the beginning of the safety evaluation, not the end.
</Callout>

### Training the user is training the tool

Qazi et al. did not just give physicians LLM access. They gave them 20 hours of training on how LLMs work, where they fail, and how to evaluate their output. The improvement in diagnostic accuracy was a product of both the tool and the training. Separate them, and you do not know which one you are measuring.

The implications for implementation are direct:
- Rolling out clinical AI without AI-literacy training is like giving someone a scalpel without surgical training.
- Training should cover: how LLMs generate text, common failure modes (hallucination, anchoring, demographic bias), how to verify AI output against clinical evidence, and when to override AI suggestions.
- Training is not a one-time event. As models change and new failure modes are discovered, training needs updating.
- The investment pays for itself. Qazi et al. showed a nearly 30-percentage-point improvement in diagnostic accuracy. No model upgrade alone delivers that kind of gain. The capable tool needs the capable user.

We do not hand a new resident an ultrasound machine and say "figure it out." We provide structured training on image acquisition, interpretation, common pitfalls, and when the findings need to be confirmed by a specialist. Clinical AI tools deserve the same onboarding.

<Callout type="tip">
  If your institution is deploying clinical AI tools, budget for AI-literacy training as a core component of the rollout, not as an optional add-on. The evidence suggests that the training may matter as much as the tool itself.
</Callout>

### The path of least resistance leads to the wrong place

Multiple studies point to the same dynamic: when accepting an AI suggestion requires less effort than rejecting it, harmful suggestions are more likely to be acted upon. This is not a model quality problem. It is an interface design problem.

In most EHR-embedded CDS implementations, the default action is to accept. Rejecting a suggestion requires clicking a button, selecting a reason, sometimes documenting an alternative. In a busy clinic, the path of least resistance is acceptance.

Safe clinical AI interfaces need to be designed so that:
- Acceptance and rejection require comparable effort
- High-risk suggestions (medication changes, diagnosis shifts) require active confirmation rather than passive acceptance
- The system surfaces its uncertainty level to the clinician
- Override patterns are logged and reviewable
- Periodic "challenge" prompts require the clinician to articulate their own assessment before seeing the AI's suggestion

The friction asymmetry problem is not unique to clinical AI. It is the same dynamic that makes "accept all cookies" buttons effective on websites. In clinical medicine, the stakes are incomparably higher. Interface designers building clinical AI tools should be required to demonstrate that their system does not favor acceptance over rejection. This should be a regulatory requirement, not a feature request.

### The time cost may be the point

Goh et al. found that LLM-assisted physicians took approximately 119 seconds longer per case. This is not necessarily a flaw. It may reflect the time needed to evaluate AI output, integrate it with clinical reasoning, and make a considered decision. If that extra two minutes produces better clinical decisions, it is time well spent.

What it does mean: "LLM access makes physicians faster" is not supported by the current evidence for reasoning tasks. Workflow applications (documentation, messaging, data extraction) do save time. Clinical reasoning applications improve quality but may cost time. These are different value propositions, and organizations need to plan for both.

There is also a dose-response question. How much time with the AI produces the optimal outcome? Is the full 119 seconds necessary, or could a more streamlined interface deliver most of the benefit in less time? Does the time cost vary by clinical complexity? These are open questions the current evidence does not answer.

### Automation complacency is predictable and measurable

Multiple studies across domains (aviation, manufacturing, and now healthcare) show that when humans supervise automated systems, their vigilance degrades over time. The first 100 AI-generated drafts get careful review. By draft 500, the physician is skimming. By draft 1,000, approval may be near-automatic.

This matters because the error rate in LLM output is not zero. If a system makes errors 3% of the time and the physician catches 90% of those errors initially but only 50% after six months of routine use, the effective error rate has doubled. Not because the model got worse, but because the human safety net got thinner.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Automation complacency is not a character flaw; it's a predictable human response to monitoring a mostly-reliable system. Institutional safeguards should include periodic quality audits, random spot-checks of AI-assisted clinical outputs, and structured review processes that keep physician engagement high over time.
</Callout>

### Where to start: the answer is clear

Across the entire evidence landscape, the applications with the strongest combination of demonstrated benefit, manageable risk, and institutional readiness are:

1. **Documentation assistance**: hospital course summaries, progress notes, discharge summaries
2. **Patient message drafting**: inbox management with physician review
3. **Data extraction from clinical notes**: research, quality improvement, cohort identification
4. **Trial eligibility prescreening**: matching patients to clinical trial criteria

These share common features: the task is well-defined, the output is reviewed by a human before action is taken, the risk of undetected harm is lower than in clinical decision support, and the time savings are measurable and immediate.

<Callout type="tip">
  If you're advising your organization on where to start with clinical AI, lead with workflow automation. It has the strongest evidence, the most manageable risk profile, and the most immediate ROI. Use the early wins to build institutional trust and infrastructure for higher-stakes applications later.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** Use these five cross-cutting lessons as a checklist when your institution evaluates any clinical AI tool. (1) Demand adversarial testing, not just benchmark scores. (2) Budget for AI-literacy training as part of every rollout. (3) Audit the interface for friction asymmetry: is it easier to accept suggestions than to reject them? (4) Plan for the time cost of AI-assisted reasoning; do not assume it will make physicians faster. (5) Build in periodic quality audits to guard against automation complacency. For the practical compliance framework, see our [HIPAA Compliance Guide](/guides/hipaa-compliance).
</Callout>

## What's Coming Next

Several areas worth watching, in roughly the order they are likely to arrive.

**Governance and controls.** The early deployment studies have made clear that technical accuracy alone is insufficient. Expect a wave of research on governance frameworks: who approves model updates, how adverse events are reported and investigated, what monitoring is required in production, and how institutions audit AI-assisted clinical decisions. Several academic medical centers have already established AI governance committees. The lessons from their early experiences will shape best practices for everyone else.

**Agentic AI systems.** The next generation of clinical AI tools will not just answer questions; they will take actions. Agentic systems that can query databases, place orders, send messages, and coordinate workflows are already in development. These raise fundamentally new safety questions. If an AI agent places a medication order that harms a patient, is that a different kind of failure than an AI that suggests a wrong medication that a physician then orders? The liability, monitoring, and safety frameworks for agentic clinical AI are entirely undeveloped. The tools are arriving before the guardrails.

**Multi-modal models in specialty care.** The current evidence base is heavily text-focused. Models that combine text, imaging, and structured data are emerging and will need their own validation studies. Radiology and pathology are the obvious first movers, but any specialty that relies on visual data (dermatology, ophthalmology, cardiology) will follow.

**Long-term outcome studies.** Nearly all current evidence measures short-term process metrics: diagnostic accuracy, management scores, documentation quality, time savings. There is almost no data on whether LLM-assisted care improves patient outcomes over months and years. These studies are underway, but they take time. The field is building the plane while flying it.

**Regulatory frameworks catching up to deployment.** The FDA, EMA, and other regulators are actively developing frameworks for LLM-based clinical tools. The current gap between deployment pace and regulatory clarity is a source of institutional risk that nobody wants to be the test case for.

**Equity and access.** The Qazi et al. study hints at a transformative possibility: LLMs could dramatically improve diagnostic accuracy in resource-limited settings where specialist access is scarce. This requires models that work well in diverse clinical contexts, languages, and healthcare systems, not just academic medical centers in high-income countries. Whether the current generation of models can deliver on this promise, or whether they will replicate the biases in their training data, is an open and urgent question.

**Composite AI systems.** The standalone LLM is already giving way to composite systems: RAG architectures grounded in institutional knowledge bases, agent frameworks that query EHRs and order systems, multi-model pipelines using different models for different tasks. These systems are more capable and harder to evaluate, audit, and regulate. Expect the evidence base to shift from "how does this model perform?" to "how does this system perform?" The unit of analysis is changing.

<Callout type="tip">
**What to do with this:** You do not need to track every emerging area. Focus on the two that will affect your practice soonest: governance (ask whether your institution has an AI governance committee; if not, advocate for one) and regulatory clarity (before adopting any new AI tool, ask your compliance team whether the FDA classification has been evaluated). For the compliance questions specifically, see the [HIPAA Compliance Guide](/guides/hipaa-compliance).
</Callout>

## How to Read This Literature

A few practical notes for clinicians evaluating AI studies, offered in the spirit of saving you time.

**Look at the task, not the model.** "GPT-4 for medicine" is too broad a claim. GPT-4 for medication interaction checking is a specific, evaluable application. The evidence is strong for some tasks and weak for others, even within the same model.

**Check the evaluation setting.** A model that performs well on board-style multiple-choice questions may not perform well on open-ended clinical scenarios with ambiguous information. Benchmark studies and deployment studies measure different things.

**Ask about the failure modes.** Every study should characterize not just average performance but the distribution of errors. What does the model get wrong? How wrong does it get? Are the errors random or systematic? Are they detectable by a clinician in real time?

**Consider the workflow integration.** A technically accurate model that does not fit into existing clinical workflows will not be used. The best AI tool is the one that physicians actually adopt, and that requires thoughtful integration, not just high accuracy.

**Distinguish the model from the system.** Most deployment studies evaluate a complete system: model plus interface plus workflow integration plus human review process. A great model in a bad interface will underperform. A mediocre model in a well-designed workflow may outperform. When a study reports that "AI improved outcomes," ask how much of that improvement came from the model versus the workflow redesign that accompanied it.

**Watch the denominator.** Studies that report accuracy on "cases where the model provided an answer" may exclude cases where the model declined to answer, errored out, or produced unusable output. The real accuracy includes those failures. A model that is 95% accurate on the 80% of cases it handles, and useless on the other 20%, has an effective accuracy of 76%.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The 2025-2026 evidence base contains over a dozen RCTs, multiple large-cohort EHR studies, deployment audits across high- and low-resource settings, and security analyses. This is no longer a field running on demos and anecdotes. The evidence is real, and so are the risks it has uncovered. The institutions that will benefit most from clinical AI are the ones that take both the promise and the evidence of harm seriously.
</Callout>

## Applying the Evidence: Two Worked Examples

### Example 1: Evaluating a vendor's accuracy claims

**The scenario:** Your department chair forwards you a vendor pitch deck for an AI-CDS tool. The deck claims "97% accuracy on clinical decision tasks" and cites a single benchmark study.

**How to apply the evidence from this guide:**

1. **Ask about the harm rate, not just the accuracy rate.** The Kenya CDS study showed 99% guideline alignment with 7.8% actively harmful recommendations. A 97% accuracy rate tells you nothing about the tail risk. Request the vendor's data on harmful or dangerous outputs specifically.
2. **Check the evaluation setting.** Was the 97% measured on standardized board-style questions or on real clinical encounters? Benchmark performance does not equal deployment safety (Alber et al.).
3. **Ask about bias audits.** Has the tool been tested for differential performance across patient demographics? The retinal disease study showed that demographic details in prompts shifted recommendations even when clinical presentation was identical.
4. **Insist on local validation.** Specialty-specific studies consistently show that performance degrades on your institution's data compared to standardized datasets. Request a pilot with your own clinical scenarios before procurement.

### Example 2: Choosing the right LLM task for your workflow

**The scenario:** You are a hospitalist who wants to start using LLMs but are not sure where to begin. You have 15 minutes to spend on this decision.

**How to apply the evidence from this guide:**

1. **Start with documentation, not clinical reasoning.** The workflow automation evidence (Theme 4) has the strongest combination of benefit, manageable risk, and institutional readiness. Small et al. showed LLM-generated hospital course summaries required less editing than physician-written ones.
2. **Use the de-identify-prompt-review workflow.** This pattern is described in detail in the [LLMs in Clinical Care 101](/guides/llms-in-clinical-care-101) guide. It takes 30 seconds to learn and eliminates HIPAA risk for consumer tools.
3. **Pick one template and use it for a week.** The [Discharge Summary template](/templates/discharge-summary-basic) is the best starting point. Track how much time it saves you and how much editing the output requires.
4. **Do not use LLMs for clinical decision support without understanding the risks.** The controlled trial evidence (Theme 1) shows that LLM-assisted reasoning improves quality but requires AI-literacy training and takes extra time. This is a second-phase adoption, not a starting point.

## Bottom Line

The evidence landscape for LLMs in medicine as of early 2026 can be stated in five findings:

1. **LLMs measurably improve physician performance in controlled settings**, but the improvement comes with a time cost and depends heavily on AI-literacy training.

2. **Deployment safety is not guaranteed by benchmark performance.** Real-world audits consistently find harmful outputs at rates that matter clinically, even when average accuracy is high.

3. **Security failure modes are real and underappreciated.** Data poisoning can introduce confidently wrong medical knowledge that standard evaluations will not catch.

4. **Workflow automation (documentation, messaging, data extraction) has the strongest evidence and the most favorable risk profile** for near-term clinical adoption.

5. **Open-source models reaching clinical parity** opens a path for regulated, privacy-preserving deployment that was not available a year ago.

The field has moved past "can LLMs pass a medical exam?" The question now is how to deploy them safely, train clinicians to use them, and monitor them in production.

The evidence supports using LLMs in clinical medicine. It also specifies the conditions: AI-literacy training for clinicians, interface design that does not make acceptance the path of least resistance, continuous monitoring in production, bias audits, incident reporting. And a healthy skepticism of any vendor who tells you their benchmark scores are all you need to know.

This evidence landscape will look different a year from now. Whether that is because institutions deployed wisely or because they rushed to adopt without the safeguards the evidence says are necessary remains, for the moment, an open question.
