The model returns a ranked differential diagnosis with probability estimates, supporting evidence for each, a prioritized workup, and a paragraph on the cognitive biases that might lead a clinician astray. She has just done something that could be described, depending on your perspective, as either the most efficient studying in the history of medical education or no studying at all.

## The Experiment That's Already Running

The question of whether LLMs belong in medical education is moot. Medical students are using them. Surveys from 2024 and 2025 found that most medical students had used ChatGPT or a similar tool for coursework. They use it to study for exams, generate practice questions, explain concepts they didn't understand in lecture, summarize research papers, and draft patient notes. Some programs have formal AI curricula. Most do not. The adoption happened anyway.

This puts medical educators in a familiar position: running behind a technology that their students adopted before anyone wrote a policy. The last time this happened with anything approaching the same speed was probably the smartphone, which medical schools spent years banning from clinical settings before accepting that residents were looking up drug dosages on UpToDate between patients, and that this was, on balance, fine.

LLMs are not UpToDate. A drug reference tool answers a specific factual question. An LLM can simulate the entire reasoning process that a physician uses to arrive at a clinical decision. That difference matters enormously, and it cuts in both directions.

## The Case for Deskilling

Clinical reasoning is not a body of knowledge. It is a skill, and like most skills, it develops through struggle. The student who sits with a clinical vignette for fifteen minutes, considers three possible diagnoses, eliminates two based on the history, and arrives at the third through a process of effortful retrieval has done something that her neurons will remember. The student who types the vignette into ChatGPT and reads the answer has not.

Learning scientists call this "desirable difficulty." The effort of retrieval, the discomfort of uncertainty, the productive failure of a wrong first guess: these are not obstacles to learning. They are the mechanism of learning. Remove them and you get a student who can recognize the right answer when she sees it but cannot generate it on her own.

The GPS analogy is instructive, if imperfect. People who rely on GPS navigation lose the ability to navigate without it. Spatial cognition measurably declines. For driving, this tradeoff is acceptable; the GPS is always available, and the cost of getting lost is low. For clinical reasoning, the calculus is different. The physician who cannot reason through a differential without AI assistance is not just inconvenienced when the tool is unavailable. She is dangerous.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  The risk is not that students will use AI. It is that they will use it in the way that requires the least effort: as an answer key rather than a thinking partner. The path of least resistance in medical education has always been memorization over understanding. LLMs make that path wider and faster.
</Callout>

There is a second deskilling concern, subtler than the first. Clinical reasoning is not purely analytical. It involves pattern recognition, intuition built from thousands of patient encounters, and a tolerance for ambiguity that develops only through sustained exposure to uncertainty. An AI tutor that provides clean, confident, well-structured answers to messy clinical questions may teach students to expect a clarity that real medicine does not offer. The patient in the vignette has a ranked differential. The patient in the exam room has a story that doesn't quite fit any of them.

## The Case for Discovery

Now consider a different student, same vignette, same tool, different approach.

She reads the clinical scenario. Before typing anything, she generates her own differential. She writes it down: PE, ACS, pneumothorax. She's not sure about the troponin. She types the vignette into ChatGPT but adds: "I think this is PE. Challenge my reasoning and tell me what I might be missing."

The model pushes back. It asks about the troponin: could this be RV strain rather than primary ischemia? It suggests she consider pericarditis. It points out that she didn't mention aortic dissection and explains why the hypertension and chest pain should keep it on the list. She argues. The model responds. The conversation takes eight minutes instead of four, and she emerges from it having stress-tested her reasoning against an interlocutor that never gets tired, never gets impatient, and has read more clinical literature than any human attending.

This student has used the same tool to do something that no textbook, no lecture, and no study group can replicate at 2 AM: she has engaged in Socratic dialogue on demand.

The educational potential here is genuine and, in some ways, unprecedented. Traditional medical education offers limited opportunities for one-on-one clinical reasoning practice. Attending physicians are busy. Standardized patients are expensive and scheduled weeks in advance. Study groups help but operate at the level of the weakest reasoner in the room. An LLM can simulate a tireless clinical tutor that is available at any hour, can adjust its complexity to the learner's level, and can generate an unlimited number of clinical scenarios targeting specific knowledge gaps.

<Callout type="evidence">
  In an RCT of 247 medical students, those who trained with GPT-4-powered simulated patient encounters scored higher on faculty-graded OSCEs (median 11.4 vs. 10.7 out of 20), reported lower stress, and showed exam absenteeism of 0.8% versus 4.9% in controls. A dose-response pattern emerged: students who completed more simulations scored higher.
</Callout>

For students who lack confidence, the effect may be even more important than the knowledge gain. The OSCE trial found that 44% of students in the AI group reported feeling emotionally ready for the exam, compared to 10% of controls. The near-elimination of absenteeism, from 4.9% to 0.8%, suggests that the tool addresses something deeper than test preparation. Students who feel underprepared avoid exams. Give them a low-stakes rehearsal space and they show up.

Seventy-four percent of students reported that the AI-simulated encounters felt like real patient interactions. Sixty-seven percent felt they were being evaluated by a human teacher, not a machine. The simulation was credible enough to generate the emotional and cognitive engagement that learning requires. Whether that engagement transfers to actual patient encounters remains an open question, but the finding is striking on its own terms.

## What the Trials Show

The evidence is still early, but two randomized trials stand out for what they reveal about the conditions under which AI helps learners.

The [OSCE simulation trial](/trials/ai-simulated-osce-training-rct-2025) tested whether AI-powered practice encounters improve performance on real clinical exams. The answer was yes, modestly: less than one point on a 20-point scale, but statistically significant and specific to the skills the platform trained. The effect was absent on the control station (otoscopy, a physical exam skill the platform could not teach), which strengthens the causal inference. The behavioral effects (reduced absenteeism, lower stress, higher confidence) may matter more than the score improvement itself.

The [AI-literacy trial](/trials/diagnostic-rct-ai-literacy-2026), conducted with 60 physicians in a low-and-middle-income country setting, revealed something more dramatic. After a 20-hour AI-literacy curriculum, physicians with LLM access improved their diagnostic reasoning scores from 42.6% to 71.4%, a near-30-percentage-point jump. Physicians who prompted more actively and asked more follow-up questions scored higher, suggesting that interaction quality, not just access, drives the benefit.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The 20-hour AI-literacy curriculum was treated as part of the intervention, not a separate variable. This design choice matters: it suggests that "giving clinicians access to ChatGPT" is not the intervention. Teaching them to use it well is.
</Callout>

The same trial produced an uncomfortable finding. In an exploratory arm, the LLM answering vignettes alone outperformed the physician-plus-LLM teams on average. Physicians added value in about a third of cases, likely the ones involving ambiguity, atypical presentations, or contextual factors the model couldn't assess. For the other two-thirds, the human in the loop made the output worse.

This finding does not mean physicians are unnecessary. It means we have not yet learned how to team with AI effectively. The 20-hour curriculum improved performance enormously, but it was a first attempt. The optimal interaction design, the pedagogical framework that teaches learners when to trust the model, when to override it, and when to use it as a sparring partner rather than an oracle, does not yet exist.

## The Same Tool, Two Students

Go back to the study room at 2 AM. Two students, same vignette, same model.

One types the question, reads the answer, moves on. She will perform well on tomorrow's exam. She may perform well for years, as long as the tool is available. The day it is not, or the day a patient presents with something the vignettes never covered, she will reach for a reasoning muscle that has atrophied from disuse.

The other types the question and then argues with the answer. She challenges the model's differential. She asks it to explain why it ranked one diagnosis above another. She poses a variation: what if the troponin were normal? What if the patient were 25 instead of 55? She is using the model not to avoid thinking but to think harder, and in more directions, than she could alone.

Medical education has always produced both kinds of learners. Before LLMs, the first student highlighted her Pathoma transcript and the second one drew concept maps and taught the material to her study group. The tool changes. The variable that determines the educational outcome does not: it is the learner's relationship to difficulty. Whether she seeks it or avoids it.

<Callout type="tip">
  The evidence suggests a practical framework. Use LLMs to generate questions, not answers. Use them to challenge your differential, not to produce it. Use them to explain the reasoning behind a conclusion you've already reached, not to reach the conclusion for you. The tool is most valuable when it makes studying harder, not easier.
</Callout>

The deskilling concern is real. It is also, in a sense, a problem that predates LLMs by decades. Medical education has struggled with the tension between efficiency and depth since the invention of the multiple-choice exam. The question was always whether the student understood the material or had merely learned to recognize the right answer. LLMs amplify that tension because they make the shortcut faster and the shortcut's output more convincing. But they also amplify the alternative: the student who uses AI to explore, to probe, to test her understanding against an interlocutor that knows more than she does and can respond in real time.

The tools are here. The students are using them. The question that remains is not whether to allow it but whether medical schools will design curricula that channel the technology toward discovery rather than dependence. That requires deliberate pedagogical work: AI-literacy training, structured prompting exercises, assessment methods that reward reasoning over recall, and a willingness to rethink what clinical competence means when a reasoning engine is always available.

The [AI-literacy trial](/trials/diagnostic-rct-ai-literacy-2026) offers a proof of concept. Twenty hours of structured training turned an AI tool from a modest convenience into a transformative educational intervention. Without the training, the same tool was less useful than the model alone. With it, diagnostic reasoning scores nearly doubled.

Twenty hours is not trivial. But it is not impossible. And the alternative, letting students figure out AI on their own at 2 AM, is an experiment that is already running, in every medical school in the country, without a protocol, without oversight, and without any plan for what happens when the results come in.
