## The Test Case

We chose a case with a clear leading diagnosis but enough complexity to test whether models could think beyond the obvious.

> **Clinical Vignette:** A 55-year-old male presents to the emergency department with 3 days of progressive dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain. Past medical history includes hypertension and type 2 diabetes, both managed with oral medications. He returned from a 14-hour international flight 5 days ago.
>
> **Vitals:** HR 108, RR 22, BP 138/88, SpO2 93% on room air, Temp 37.2°C.
>
> **Exam:** Lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally. Right calf is swollen and tender to palpation. No JVD. Regular tachycardia, no murmurs.
>
> **ECG:** Sinus tachycardia with S1Q3T3 pattern.
>
> **Labs:** D-dimer 2,400 ng/mL (normal &lt;500). Troponin I mildly elevated at 0.08 ng/mL. BNP 320 pg/mL.

PE is the obvious leading diagnosis, but a thorough clinician should also consider pneumothorax, ACS, pericarditis, pneumonia, and aortic dissection in the differential. The mildly elevated troponin and BNP add layers that test whether the model can integrate findings rather than anchor on a single diagnosis. (Want to try a similar prompt yourself? <h2 id="the-verdict">The Verdict</h2>

**If you pick one model for clinical reasoning: o3.** It produced the most structured, auditable outputs for core diagnostic tasks. The probability estimates, evidence mapping, and tiered workup planning reflect a model that has internalized clinical workflow patterns.

**For a second opinion or case synthesis: Claude.** Where o3 excels at structure, Claude excels at judgment. It organized case synthesis by clinical problem rather than data source. It named remaining uncertainties. Its cognitive debiasing raised specific, actionable points (Baker's cyst mimicking DVT, S1Q3T3 sensitivity limitations) that other models missed. Claude reads like a thoughtful consultant; o3 reads like a thorough checklist.

**For evidence-based questions: Perplexity.** When you need specific trial data, guideline citations, or recent literature, Perplexity is the clear winner. Its search-augmented architecture lets it cite HOKUSAI-VTE subgroup analyses and ongoing trials that pure reasoning models cannot reliably provide. Use it for "What does the evidence say?" questions, not for clinical reasoning.

**For long medical records: Gemini.** Gemini's large context window makes it the best current option for ingesting lengthy medical records, multi-page discharge summaries, or longitudinal chart reviews. We tested it with a 47-page medical record and it maintained coherence throughout; other models truncated or lost details from early in the document.

**No model replaces clinical judgment.** Every model made at least one error or omission during testing. The physician remains the decision-maker.

## Methodology

**Testing dates:** March 10-15, 2026.

**Model versions tested:**
- Claude 3.5 Sonnet (via claude.ai)
- o3 (via ChatGPT Plus, o3 reasoning model)
- GPT-4o (via ChatGPT Plus)
- Gemini Advanced (via gemini.google.com)
- Perplexity Pro (via perplexity.ai)

**Protocol:** Each model received the identical prompt for each task. We used fresh conversations (no prior context) for every test. Temperature/sampling settings were left at each platform's defaults. Outputs shown are representative of each model's typical performance on this case; we ran each prompt three times and selected the most representative response (not the best or worst).

**Limitations:** This is a single case study, not a systematic benchmark. Performance may vary across different clinical scenarios, specialties, and case complexity levels. Model capabilities change with updates; results here reflect the versions tested in March 2026.

**Re-testing commitment:** We plan to re-run this comparison when any model receives a major update. Check the `lastUpdated` date for currency.
