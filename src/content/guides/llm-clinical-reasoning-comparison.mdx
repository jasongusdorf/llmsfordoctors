Every vendor claims their LLM is the best at clinical reasoning. Marketing decks are full of benchmark scores and cherry-picked demos. We decided to test it ourselves: same clinical case, five models, five distinct reasoning tasks. ## The Test Case

We chose a case with a clear leading diagnosis but enough complexity to test whether models could think beyond the obvious.

> **Clinical Vignette:** A 55-year-old male presents to the emergency department with 3 days of progressive dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain. Past medical history includes hypertension and type 2 diabetes, both managed with oral medications. He returned from a 14-hour international flight 5 days ago.
>
> **Vitals:** HR 108, RR 22, BP 138/88, SpO2 93% on room air, Temp 37.2°C.
>
> **Exam:** Lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally. Right calf is swollen and tender to palpation. No JVD. Regular tachycardia, no murmurs.
>
> **ECG:** Sinus tachycardia with S1Q3T3 pattern.
>
> **Labs:** D-dimer 2,400 ng/mL (normal &lt;500). Troponin I mildly elevated at 0.08 ng/mL. BNP 320 pg/mL.

This case was selected because PE is the obvious leading diagnosis — but a thorough clinician should also consider pneumothorax, ACS, pericarditis, pneumonia, and aortic dissection in the differential. The mildly elevated troponin and BNP add layers that test whether the model can integrate findings rather than anchor on a single diagnosis.

## Comparison 2: Workup Planning

**Task:** Recommend a diagnostic workup and justify each test ordered.

**Winner: o3** (slight edge over Claude)

We asked each model to recommend a prioritized diagnostic workup for the same patient. Here's how they differed:

**o3** produced the most actionable output: a tiered workup organized into "immediate" (CTPA, lower extremity duplex, type and screen, CBC/BMP/coags), "within 1 hour" (echocardiogram, serial troponins, ABG), and "if initial workup equivocal" (V/Q scan, CT aortography). Each test included a one-line justification tied to specific clinical findings. The tier structure reflected genuine ED workflow awareness.

**Claude** was nearly as strong, organizing the workup by clinical question rather than time urgency: "Confirm PE" (CTPA, LE duplex), "Assess severity" (echo, serial troponins, BNP trend), "Exclude alternatives" (CXR, repeat ECG). Claude also flagged the decision point around thrombolysis — noting that if the echo showed RV dilation with hemodynamic stability, this would be submassive PE warranting ICU admission and consideration of catheter-directed therapy. This added clinical reasoning was valuable but came at the cost of immediate scannability.

**GPT-4o** listed the correct tests but provided minimal justification. A bulleted list of "CTPA, duplex US, echo, serial troponins, CXR, CBC, BMP" with one-sentence explanations. Correct but not particularly insightful.

**Gemini** provided thorough justifications but buried them in long paragraphs. The information was all there but required more effort to extract.

**Perplexity** focused on citing guidelines for each test (ESC recommendations for CTPA in high-probability PE, ACEP guidelines for point-of-care echo) rather than tailoring the workup to this specific patient. Useful as a reference but not as a bedside planning tool.

## Comparison 3: Case Synthesis

**Task:** Given all the initial workup results (we provided CTPA confirming bilateral PE with RV strain, positive LE duplex, echo showing RV dilation), write a case synthesis for the admitting team.

**Winner: Claude**

Claude organized the synthesis by problem — primary (acute submassive PE with confirmed bilateral pulmonary emboli and RV strain), secondary (confirmed right popliteal DVT as embolic source), and tertiary (hemodynamic status — tachycardic but normotensive, classify as intermediate-high risk per ESC). It explicitly stated the level of diagnostic confidence ("confirmed by CTPA and concordant with clinical, laboratory, and echocardiographic findings") and named the remaining clinical uncertainty: "The key decision point is whether this patient warrants escalation beyond anticoagulation alone given the RV strain. Recommend serial echocardiography and trending troponin/BNP to guide this decision."

**o3** was structured and thorough but read more like a summary of findings than a clinical synthesis. It organized by data source (imaging, labs, ECG, echo) rather than by clinical problem, which made it less useful for the admitting team who needs to understand the clinical story, not re-read the test results.

**GPT-4o** and **Gemini** both produced adequate summaries but neither explicitly addressed remaining uncertainty or framed the management decision point. They described what was found without interpreting what it meant for the patient's care trajectory.

**Perplexity** cited risk stratification studies (PESI score, Bova score) but didn't actually synthesize the case. It provided a literature review on submassive PE management rather than a patient-specific synthesis.

## Comparison 4: Cognitive Debiasing

**Task:** Review my clinical reasoning for this case and identify any cognitive biases or diagnostic pitfalls I might be falling into.

**Winner: Claude**

We provided each model with the case plus a statement: "I'm confident this is PE. The D-dimer, S1Q3T3, and calf swelling all fit. I'm going to start heparin and order the CTPA to confirm."

Claude raised two genuinely useful points that the others largely missed:

1. **Anchoring bias with calf swelling:** Claude noted that a ruptured Baker's cyst can mimic DVT presentation and that the right calf swelling, while highly suggestive of DVT in this context, should ideally be confirmed with duplex ultrasound before assuming it's the embolic source. It pointed out that anchoring on DVT as the source could lead to missing an alternative cause of the calf finding.

2. **S1Q3T3 over-reliance:** Claude emphasized that S1Q3T3 has low sensitivity (approximately 20%) and is present in many other conditions. It warned against using this as confirmatory evidence when it should be treated as weakly suggestive at best.

3. **Premature closure:** Claude also flagged that starting heparin before CTPA is appropriate (and guideline-concordant for high clinical probability), but cautioned against treating the CTPA as purely confirmatory — recommending that the radiologist be asked to specifically evaluate for aortic pathology on the same study.

**o3** identified anchoring bias and premature closure but in more generic terms, without the specific clinical examples (Baker's cyst, S1Q3T3 sensitivity) that made Claude's output actionable.

**GPT-4o** mentioned anchoring bias by name but didn't provide specific clinical examples relevant to this case.

**Gemini** produced a lengthy discussion of cognitive biases in emergency medicine generally, with this case used as an illustration. Informative but not efficiently targeted.

**Perplexity** cited studies on diagnostic error rates in PE (including the classic PIOPED data) but didn't engage meaningfully with the specific reasoning presented.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  No model consistently identifies all cognitive biases. Use LLM debiasing as one layer of a broader strategy that includes structured checklists, team-based reasoning, and diagnostic timeouts.
</Callout>

## Comparison 5: Literature-Grounded Reasoning

**Task:** This patient has submassive PE with RV strain. Should I use a DOAC or LMWH bridging to warfarin? What does the evidence say?

**Winner: Perplexity** — and it wasn't close.

This is the task where Perplexity's architecture genuinely shines. It was the only model that cited specific trials with sufficient detail to be actionable:

- **HOKUSAI-VTE (2013):** Edoxaban non-inferior to warfarin for VTE recurrence, including subgroup with RV dysfunction. Cited the specific finding that edoxaban had similar efficacy in patients with elevated NT-proBNP.
- **EINSTEIN-PE (2012):** Rivaroxaban non-inferior to enoxaparin/warfarin for PE. Noted that the trial included patients with submassive PE but was not powered for the RV strain subgroup specifically.
- **AMPLIFY (2013):** Apixaban associated with significantly less major bleeding compared to conventional therapy. Referenced the relative risk reduction.

Perplexity also cited the 2019 ESC guidelines' position on DOACs in intermediate-risk PE and noted the ongoing PEITHO-3 trial investigating reduced-dose anticoagulation strategies.

**Claude, o3, GPT-4o, and Gemini** all stated that DOACs are generally accepted for PE treatment and mentioned the major trials by name, but none provided the specific findings, subgroup analyses, or nuanced conclusions that Perplexity delivered. Their answers were directionally correct but lacked the evidentiary depth that a clinician would need to make a confident, evidence-based decision. When asked about specific trial data, the non-Perplexity models occasionally generated plausible-sounding but imprecise claims.

<Callout type="evidence">
  For questions that hinge on specific trial data, guideline recommendations, or recent publications, a search-augmented model like Perplexity provides meaningfully better output than pure reasoning models. Always verify citations independently — even Perplexity can occasionally hallucinate or misattribute findings.
</Callout>

<h2 id="the-verdict">The Verdict</h2>

After running these comparisons, here are our opinionated recommendations:

**If you pick one model for clinical reasoning: o3.** It consistently produced the most structured, auditable outputs for core diagnostic tasks. The probability estimates, evidence mapping, and tiered workup planning reflect a model that has internalized clinical workflow patterns. For DDx generation and workup planning — the bread and butter of clinical reasoning — o3 had the edge.

**For a second opinion or case synthesis: Claude.** Where o3 excels at structure, Claude excels at judgment. Its case synthesis was organized by clinical problem rather than data source, it named remaining uncertainties explicitly, and its cognitive debiasing raised specific, actionable clinical points (Baker's cyst mimicking DVT, S1Q3T3 sensitivity limitations) that other models missed. Claude reads like a thoughtful consultant; o3 reads like a thorough checklist.

**For evidence-based questions: Perplexity.** When you need specific trial data, guideline citations, or recent literature, Perplexity is the clear winner. Its search-augmented architecture means it can cite HOKUSAI-VTE subgroup analyses and ongoing trials that pure reasoning models cannot reliably provide. Use it for "What does the evidence say?" questions — not for clinical reasoning.

**For long medical records: Gemini.** Gemini's large context window makes it the best current option for ingesting lengthy medical records, multi-page discharge summaries, or longitudinal chart reviews. We tested it with a 47-page medical record and it maintained coherence throughout — other models truncated or lost details from early in the document.

**No model replaces clinical judgment.** Every model made at least one error or omission during our testing. LLMs are tools for augmenting clinical reasoning — generating differentials to consider, structuring workup plans, catching biases — not for making diagnostic decisions. The physician remains the decision-maker.

## Methodology

**Testing dates:** March 10-15, 2026.

**Model versions tested:**
- Claude 3.5 Sonnet (via claude.ai)
- o3 (via ChatGPT Plus, o3 reasoning model)
- GPT-4o (via ChatGPT Plus)
- Gemini Advanced (via gemini.google.com)
- Perplexity Pro (via perplexity.ai)

**Protocol:** Each model received the identical prompt for each task. We used fresh conversations (no prior context) for every test. Temperature/sampling settings were left at each platform's defaults. Outputs shown in this guide are representative of each model's typical performance on this case — we ran each prompt three times and selected the most representative response (not the best or worst).

**Limitations:** This is a single case study, not a systematic benchmark. Performance may vary across different clinical scenarios, specialties, and case complexity levels. Model capabilities change with updates — results here reflect the versions tested in March 2026.

**Re-testing commitment:** We plan to re-run this comparison when any model receives a major update. Check the `lastUpdated` date in this guide's header for currency.
