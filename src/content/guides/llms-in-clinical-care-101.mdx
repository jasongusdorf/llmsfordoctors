You want to use AI to make your clinical documentation faster. Good: it can cut note-writing time in half. But there is a right way and a wrong way to do it. The wrong way puts patient data at risk. The right way takes 30 extra seconds and protects you, your institution, and your patients.

This guide teaches you the practical, day-to-day workflow for using an LLM in clinical care. It is the workflow. If you read one guide on this site, make it this one.

---

## The Core Workflow

Every clinical LLM interaction follows the same three steps:

1. **De-identify**: strip patient identifiers from your clinical text
2. **Prompt**: paste the cleaned text into your LLM with a saved template
3. **Review**: read the output critically before using it

That's it. Three steps. The rest of this guide walks through each one in detail.

---

## Step 1: De-identify the Note

This is the most important step. Skip it and you've sent PHI to a third-party server. Do it and you can use almost any LLM safely, even consumer tools without a BAA.

### The Find-and-Replace Method

This is the fastest de-identification method for daily use. It takes under 30 seconds once you've done it a few times.

1. **Copy the note from Epic** (or your EHR) into a plain text editor: Word, TextEdit, Notepad, whatever is on the computer in front of you.

2. **Find and replace the patient's name with "Patient."** Use Ctrl+H (or Cmd+H on Mac). Replace all instances (first name, last name, and any combined forms). If the note says "Mr. Johnson" and "David Johnson" and "Johnson," replace all of them.

3. **Find and replace the date of birth with nothing.** Just delete it. The LLM does not need the patient's birthday to write a discharge summary. If you need age-based context, type "72-year-old male" separately; that is not an identifier.

4. **Scan for other identifiers.** Quickly check for MRNs, account numbers, addresses, phone numbers, or specific dates of service. Remove them. This takes 5 seconds with practice.

5. **Copy the cleaned text.** You're ready for step 2.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  HIPAA defines 18 categories of identifiers. For daily clinical use, the three you'll encounter most are **name**, **date of birth**, and **MRN**. Removing these three covers 95% of the risk in a typical progress note or discharge summary. See the full list in the <a href="/guides/hipaa-compliance">HIPAA Compliance Guide</a>.
</Callout>

<Callout type="tip">
  Build this into muscle memory. After a few days, the find-and-replace step becomes automatic, like washing your hands between patients. It adds almost no time to your workflow and eliminates the biggest risk of using AI in clinical care.
</Callout>

### Why a Text Editor and Not Directly in the LLM?

You want a buffer between your EHR and the LLM. If you copy from Epic and paste directly into ChatGPT, you will forget to de-identify. It will happen (not today, not tomorrow, but eventually). The text editor is your scrub sink. You stop there, clean the data, and then move on.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Do not rely on "I'll just be careful." You are a busy clinician seeing 15–25 patients a day. You will forget. The intermediate step (copy into a text editor first, de-identify, then paste into the LLM) is a system-level safeguard. Systems beat intentions.
</Callout>

## Step 2: Prompt with a Saved Template

You should not be writing your prompt from scratch every time. That's slow and inconsistent. Instead, save 2–3 prompt templates for the tasks you do most often and reuse them.

### How to Set Up Templates

Most LLMs let you save prompts or create reusable conversations:

- **Claude**: Use Projects. Create a project called "Clinical Templates" and add your prompt templates as project instructions. Every new conversation in that project starts with your template already loaded.
- **ChatGPT**: Use Custom Instructions or GPTs. Set your system prompt once and it persists.
- **Any LLM**: At minimum, keep a text file on your desktop with your templates. Copy-paste the template, then paste your de-identified note below it.

### Example: Discharge Summary Template

Here's a template you can save and reuse every time you write a discharge summary:

<PromptPlayground tool="Claude" title="Discharge Summary Template">
You are an experienced hospitalist physician. Using the clinical information I paste below, draft a concise discharge summary for the outpatient primary care provider.

Format with these sections:
1. Admission Diagnosis
2. Brief Hospital Course (paragraph form, 3–5 sentences)
3. Significant Results (labs, imaging, procedures; only clinically relevant findings)
4. Discharge Medications (changes only: new, stopped, dose-changed)
5. Discharge Condition
6. Follow-up Instructions
7. Return Precautions (plain language, suitable for patient handout)

Be concise and clinically precise. No filler phrases. Use standard medical abbreviations where appropriate.

---

[Paste de-identified clinical note here]
</PromptPlayground>

### Other Common Templates

You'll likely want templates for:

- **Patient education handouts**: "Explain [condition] and [treatment plan] in plain language at an 8th grade reading level"
- **Prior authorization letters**: "Write a medical necessity letter for [treatment] citing relevant clinical guidelines"
- **Differential diagnosis reasoning**: "Given the following presentation, generate a ranked differential with supporting and opposing evidence for each"
- **Handoff summaries**: "Summarize this patient's current status for a cross-cover handoff in 5 bullet points"

Each template saves you from thinking about prompt structure so you can focus on the clinical content.

<Callout type="tip">
  Start with one template. The discharge summary is the best starting point: it's the most time-consuming documentation task, the format is predictable, and the output is easy to verify. Once you're comfortable with one, add more.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Review the Output

AI is not a physician. It is a writing tool. You are still the clinician responsible for everything in the note.

**What to check:**

- **Accuracy**: Does the output match the clinical facts you provided? AI will occasionally invent details that sound plausible. Look for medications, dosages, or findings that weren't in your input.
- **Completeness**: Did it include everything important? Follow-up appointments, pending results, and return precautions are commonly omitted.
- **Tone and audience**: Is the language appropriate for the intended reader? A PCP handoff should be clinical. A patient handout should be plain language.
- **Hallucinations**: AI can fabricate lab values, medication names, or clinical events. If something in the output wasn't in your input, delete it or verify it.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Never sign a note you haven't read. The AI draft is a first draft; treat it like a resident's note. Read it, edit it, own it. If it's wrong and you signed it, it's your error, not the AI's.
</Callout>

## The Complete Workflow in Practice

Here's what this looks like in real time, start to finish:

| Step | Action | Time |
|------|--------|------|
| 1 | Copy progress note from Epic into TextEdit | 5 sec |
| 2 | Cmd+H → replace "Johnson" with "Patient" → Replace All | 5 sec |
| 3 | Cmd+H → replace "04/15/1952" with "" → Replace All | 5 sec |
| 4 | Quick scan for MRN, remove if present | 5 sec |
| 5 | Copy cleaned text | 2 sec |
| 6 | Open Claude project with saved discharge summary template | 5 sec |
| 7 | Paste de-identified note, hit Enter | 3 sec |
| 8 | Review AI output, edit as needed | 2–3 min |
| 9 | Copy final version back into Epic | 5 sec |
| **Total** | | **~3–4 min** |

Compare this to writing a discharge summary from scratch: 15–20 minutes.

## What About HIPAA-Compliant LLMs?

Some LLMs are available under a Business Associate Agreement (BAA), which means your institution has a contract allowing PHI to be processed by that vendor. If your institution has set this up, you may be able to skip the de-identification step for that specific tool.

**But here's the thing:** the de-identification workflow is still good practice, even with a BAA in place. Why?

- BAAs can be revoked or changed. If you build a habit of always de-identifying, you're never caught off guard.
- Not every tool you'll use will have a BAA. The habit transfers across tools.
- De-identification reduces your institutional risk footprint regardless of the contract.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  A BAA does not make a tool "safe." It makes it "legally compliant under specific conditions." You should still follow your institution's specific policies, which may impose additional restrictions beyond what the BAA covers.
</Callout>

## Getting Started Today

1. **Pick one task.** Discharge summaries are the best starting point.
2. **Save one template.** Use the discharge summary template above or adapt it to your style.
3. **Practice the de-identification step** three times. By the fourth patient, it will be automatic.
4. **Review every output.** This is non-negotiable. You are the physician of record.

You don't need to overhaul your practice. You need one workflow, done consistently. Start there. The rest will follow.
