You want to use AI to make clinical documentation faster, and it can: a good prompt turns twenty minutes of discharge writing into three. The instinct, though, is to copy the whole note out of the chart, scrub a few names, and paste it into ChatGPT. That instinct is the problem. It is the slowest path to the most risk and, often, the worst output.

There is a better default, and it fits on one line.

<Callout type="tip">
  **The short version.** Do not paste the chart. Paste your clinical judgment. For most tasks the model needs a short, clinician-curated summary, not the raw record. Match the tool and the input to the risk of the task, and read every output before you use it.
</Callout>

The model rarely needs the full medical record. It needs the relevant diagnoses, the key events, the active decision, and the specific output you want. Writing that summary takes thirty seconds, and it is faster, safer, and usually better than copying raw EHR text. If you read one guide on this site, make it this one.

## Match the Tool to the Risk

Clinical AI tasks fall into three tiers. The right tool and the right input depend on which tier you are in.

### Tier 1: Generic tasks, no patient data

Many of the most useful LLM tasks contain no patient information at all. These are the easiest and lowest risk uses, and any general-purpose model handles them well.

- "Explain SGLT2 inhibitors to a patient with heart failure at an 8th-grade reading level."
- "Generate a generic discharge instruction template for pneumonia."
- "Draft a prior authorization letter template for a GLP-1 medication."
- "Make this generic patient handout more readable."
- "Give me a checklist for the outpatient workup of hyponatremia."

Because none of these prompts carry patient-specific information, they are appropriate for any consumer LLM, as long as you still review the output for accuracy.

### Tier 2: Abstracted patient tasks, the everyday sweet spot

Most patient-specific work does not require the chart. It requires a short summary you write yourself: the minimum clinical facts the task needs, in your own words.

<PromptPlayground tool="Any LLM" title="Curated summary instead of a pasted note">
Older adult with HFrEF and CKD3 admitted for acute decompensated heart failure. Improved with IV diuresis, going home on increased torsemide. Creatinine rose mildly but stabilized before discharge.

Draft plain-language discharge instructions focused on daily weights, low-salt diet adherence, follow-up labs, and return precautions.
</PromptPlayground>

This beats pasting a discharge summary for two reasons. It forces you to decide what matters before the model gets involved, which is the part only a clinician can do. And it keeps stale, copied-forward, contradictory, and irrelevant chart data out of the prompt, so the model can't mistake clutter for signal.

The curated-summary approach covers most of what clinicians actually want from an LLM:

- Patient instructions and after-visit summaries
- Handoff and sign-out summaries
- Discharge counseling language
- Differential diagnosis brainstorming
- Patient-friendly explanations
- Prior authorization drafts
- Message replies and clinic note phrasing

The goal is not to have the model read the chart for you. It is to have the model turn your clinical understanding into a cleaner written product.

<Callout type="tip">
  Save your prompts so you are not rewriting them every time. In Claude, create a Project called "Clinical Templates" and put your prompts in the project instructions. In ChatGPT, use a custom GPT or saved instructions. At minimum, keep a text file on your desktop. Then each task is just: write the two-line summary, drop it under the saved prompt, go. Browse ready-made versions in the <a href="/templates">templates library</a>.
</Callout>

### Tier 3: Raw chart text and PHI, institution-approved tools only

Some tasks genuinely need the source material: a full note, a discharge summary, a lab table, a consult, a medication list. When the task requires copying PHI-rich text, use a tool your institution has approved for that purpose, under appropriate privacy, security, and contractual safeguards.

Do not paste raw or near-raw EHR text into a consumer LLM unless your institution has explicitly approved that tool for that use, or the text has been properly de-identified. The next two sections cover what "approved" and "de-identified" actually mean, because both are easy to get wrong.

## Why Not Just Paste the Whole Note?

Copying a block of chart text is tempting, and it creates two distinct problems.

The first is privacy. EHR notes are dense with identifiers that are easy to miss in a quick scan: names, dates, MRNs, phone numbers, addresses, locations, ordering and consulting providers, even family members. Miss one and you have sent PHI to a third-party server.

The second is clinical quality, and it is the one clinicians underestimate. Notes are noisy. They carry copied-forward problems, outdated medication lists, conflicting histories, irrelevant labs, boilerplate, and plans that changed three days later. Paste all of that in and the model may summarize the wrong information with complete confidence. The clinical risk is concrete: an after-visit summary built on last week's medication list, or a handoff that repeats a problem you resolved on day two. A curated summary gives the model the facts that matter and leaves out the rest.

## The Institution-Approved Path: Enterprise LLMs and BAAs

The honest answer to "how do I safely use the chart itself" is not a clever scrubbing trick. It is that your hospital should hold a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with a high-quality LLM vendor, and route clinical use through that approved product.

A BAA is the contract that lets a vendor handle PHI on a covered entity's behalf. The important and widely misunderstood point: the BAA-eligible product is a different product from the app you use at home. ChatGPT Plus is not ChatGPT Enterprise. The free claude.ai is not Claude's HIPAA-ready Enterprise plan. The model may be the same; the data handling, retention, and contractual terms are not.

As of mid-2026, the major frontier vendors offer covered paths, and both launched dedicated healthcare tiers in early 2026:

- **OpenAI.** A BAA is available for ChatGPT Enterprise (sales-managed accounts) and for the API. OpenAI launched ChatGPT for Healthcare in January 2026 for regulated clinical environments; PHI entered through these products is not used to train the models. The consumer tiers (Free, Plus, Pro, Team) and ChatGPT Business are not covered.
- **Anthropic.** Claude offers HIPAA-ready Enterprise plans where an admin opts in and the BAA is click-to-accept, plus API BAAs. Claude can also be used under a BAA through Amazon Bedrock or Google Vertex AI, where the cloud provider's agreement covers the inference path. Free, Pro, Max, Team, and the Console are not covered.

<Callout type="hipaa">
  A signed BAA covers the vendor's obligations, not yours. It does not make every use appropriate. The tool still has to be approved for the specific workflow, configured correctly, and used according to institutional policy. What your staff type into the prompt, and which tier they use, sits entirely outside the BAA. Vendor terms change; confirm the current arrangement with your institution before using any tool with PHI.
</Callout>

## De-identification: Useful, But Not Magic

When you do not have an approved tool and need to lower the risk of a piece of text, de-identification helps. It is a risk-reduction technique, not a permission slip, and the distinction matters.

HIPAA de-identification is a specific legal standard. The Safe Harbor method requires removing 18 categories of identifiers: names, all dates except year, geographic units smaller than a state, phone and fax numbers, email addresses, MRNs, account numbers, URLs, IP addresses, biometric identifiers, full-face photographs, and any other unique identifying detail.

In day-to-day practice, names, dates of birth, MRNs, and dates of service are the high-risk identifiers you will meet most often. A fast find-and-replace in a plain text editor handles them: copy the text out of the chart, replace the name with "Patient," delete the date of birth (the model does not need a birthday; "72-year-old man" is not an identifier), and scan for an MRN or address. Thirty seconds with practice.

But removing those four does not make the remaining text de-identified. A rare diagnosis, an unusual mechanism of injury, a specific location, or an exact timeline can still single out a patient. Treat manual de-identification as turning a high-risk input into a lower-risk one, not as a green light to use any tool for anything.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Do not rely on "I'll just be careful." Seeing 15 to 25 patients a day, you will eventually paste something you meant to scrub. The fix is structural: if a task needs the chart, route it through an approved tool. If it doesn't, write the curated summary instead. Systems beat intentions.
</Callout>

## The Minimum-Necessary Prompt

Before any clinical prompt, ask one question: what is the least information the model needs to do this well?

Leave out the full chart if the model only needs the diagnosis and the plan. Leave out exact dates if relative timing ("on day three") is enough. Leave out demographics unless they change the clinical answer. Leave out names, locations, room numbers, and family details unless there is a specific, approved reason to include them.

Good LLM use starts before the prompt, with the decision about what belongs in it.

## A Practical Workflow

For everyday clinical use:

1. Decide which tier the task is in: generic, abstracted patient-specific, or PHI-rich.
2. Use no patient information when you can.
3. When you need patient context, write a brief clinician-curated summary instead of pasting the chart.
4. Include only the minimum necessary.
5. Use an institution-approved tool for raw notes, copied chart text, or other PHI-rich work.
6. Read and edit every output before it touches clinical care.

The model is not the physician. It is not the chart reviewer. It is not responsible for the final note, message, or plan. You are.

---

## What to Check Before You Use the Output

Every LLM-generated clinical output gets the same read-through:

- **Accuracy.** Does it match the facts you provided?
- **Completeness.** Did it keep the important follow-up, pending results, medication changes, and return precautions?
- **Hallucinations.** Did it invent a medication, lab value, diagnosis, or event? If it wasn't in your input, verify it or cut it.
- **Tone.** Is the wording right for the audience? A handoff is clinical; a patient handout is plain language.
- **Clinical judgment.** Is the plan medically appropriate?
- **Privacy.** Did the output reintroduce any identifying detail?

Never sign, send, or copy forward LLM-generated content without reading and editing it. Treat the draft like a resident's note: read it, fix it, own it. If it is wrong and you signed it, it is your error, not the model's.

---

## Bottom Line

The safest practical approach is not "paste the note after removing a few identifiers."

It is this: use generic prompts when no patient data is needed, use a short clinician-curated summary for everyday patient-specific tasks, route raw chart text through an institution-approved tool under a BAA, and review every output. This protects patients, lowers legal and institutional risk, and usually produces better clinical writing, because the model receives the facts that matter instead of a noisy block of copied chart text.
