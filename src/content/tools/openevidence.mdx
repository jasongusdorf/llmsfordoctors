## Overview

OpenEvidence is an AI-powered clinical search and evidence retrieval platform designed for physicians. It surfaces point-of-care summaries drawn from peer-reviewed literature, aiming to close the gap between a clinical question and the best available evidence.

The platform maintains a trust center indicating a HIPAA compliance program and SOC 2 Type 2 certification. It claims encryption-in-transit for data moving between your browser and their servers. However, the availability of a Business Associate Agreement for individual clinicians versus institutional customers remains unclear from public documentation alone.

For straightforward clinical evidence queries ("What is the latest data on SGLT2 inhibitors in HFpEF?") OpenEvidence performs well. The risk enters when clinicians begin pasting patient-specific details into prompts without confirming their organization has a signed BAA in place.

OpenEvidence occupies an interesting niche: it is more clinician-focused than general-purpose LLMs, but its compliance posture is less clearly documented than enterprise ambient documentation tools. That makes it useful for evidence lookup but risky for anything involving identifiable patient information.

If your workflow involves answering clinical questions at the point of care (whether during rounds, in the emergency department, or before a procedure) OpenEvidence can serve as a fast literature-backed reference. Just keep patient-identifiable data out of the search box until your BAA situation is resolved.

## Clinical Strengths

OpenEvidence is worth evaluating for these use cases:

- **Clinician-focused medical search**: built specifically for physician queries, not repurposed from a general-purpose chatbot
- **Evidence-based summaries**: returns structured answers grounded in published literature with citations
- **Formal trust portal**: maintains a dedicated security and compliance page describing their HIPAA program and SOC 2 posture
- **Low barrier to entry**: free tier available, making it accessible for individual clinicians to trial
- **Point-of-care utility**: designed for the kinds of quick-turnaround questions that arise during clinical encounters
- **Differential support**: useful for generating differential diagnoses based on clinical findings when framed as general medical questions

<Callout type="tip">
  OpenEvidence works best as a rapid evidence lookup tool. Think of it as a smarter PubMed search, not a replacement for your clinical reasoning. Frame queries as clinical questions rather than pasting chart data.
</Callout>

## Weaknesses

Keep these limitations in mind:

- **BAA availability is ambiguous**: public documentation describes a HIPAA program, but whether individual clinicians can obtain a BAA (versus institutional customers) is not clearly stated
- **Some institutions explicitly prohibit PHI entry**: multiple academic medical centers have warned staff not to enter patient data absent an organizational BAA with OpenEvidence
- **Click-through agreements may not suffice**: a terms-of-service acceptance is not the same as a signed BAA that satisfies your employer's compliance requirements
- **Audit and logging details are unspecified**: public documentation does not describe what access logs are maintained or how they can be reviewed
- **Citation verification still necessary**: like any AI-powered search tool, references should be spot-checked against source material
- **Not a replacement for dedicated resources**: OpenEvidence supplements but does not replace established point-of-care tools like UpToDate or DynaMed that have editorial oversight and structured drug information

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Do not assume that a platform's general HIPAA compliance program means you personally have BAA coverage. Many platforms maintain HIPAA programs for enterprise customers while offering no BAA to individual users on free tiers.
</Callout>

## HIPAA & Privacy

<Callout type="hipaa">
  OpenEvidence states that PHI is handled under standard BAA and customer agreements, and their trust center describes a HIPAA compliance program with SOC 2 Type 2 certification. However, actionable BAA coverage depends entirely on your organization's agreement with the vendor. Do NOT assume that a personal account or click-through agreement satisfies institutional HIPAA requirements. Confirm with your compliance office before entering any patient-identifiable information.
</Callout>

What to verify before using OpenEvidence with patient data:

- **Does your organization have a signed BAA with OpenEvidence?** This is the threshold question.
- **Which features are covered under that BAA?** Not all product tiers or features may carry the same compliance posture.
- **What are the data retention terms?** Understand how long your queries and any associated data are stored.
- **What audit capabilities exist?** Confirm whether access logs are available for your compliance team.

For evidence-based queries that contain no patient-identifiable information, OpenEvidence is a useful clinical tool regardless of BAA status. The issue arises only when PHI enters the equation.

The practical rule: use OpenEvidence the same way you would use a medical textbook search: with clinical questions, not with patient charts. If you find yourself pasting anything from a patient's record, stop and confirm your BAA status first.

When used within its intended scope (evidence retrieval without PHI) OpenEvidence is a valuable addition to a clinician's toolkit. The compliance question only becomes a barrier when patient-specific data enters the workflow.
