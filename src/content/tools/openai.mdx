## Overview

OpenAI offers a family of models that spans from the fast, general-purpose GPT-4o to the deliberate, chain-of-thought-driven o-series (o1, o3). For clinical users, the distinction matters enormously: GPT-4o is a capable workhorse for routine tasks, while the o-series models represent a genuine leap forward for structured diagnostic reasoning.

The o3 model in particular applies extended chain-of-thought computation before producing an answer. In practice, this means it will walk through a differential systematically, weigh competing diagnoses against each other, and surface considerations that faster models skip, behavior that closely mirrors how a careful clinician thinks.

OpenAI makes a Business Associate Agreement available at the enterprise tier, enabling HIPAA-compliant use of patient data for healthcare organizations.

---

## Clinical Strengths

- **Differential diagnosis generation**: o1 and o3's chain-of-thought reasoning produces structured, ordered differentials with explicit reasoning steps
- **Complex clinical reasoning**: excels at multi-step problems: drug-drug interactions, atypical presentations, rare disease workups
- **Note writing with GPT-4o**: fast enough for real-time documentation assistance; produces well-formatted SOAP notes and discharge summaries
- **Instruction following**: GPT-4o reliably produces output in specified formats (tables, structured templates, numbered lists)
- **Broad general knowledge**: strong performance across specialties, including areas with sparse training data

## Which Model Should I Use?

| Clinical Task | Best Model | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Differential diagnosis (complex case) | o3 | Deep chain-of-thought reasoning; systematic |
| Differential diagnosis (routine) | o1 or GPT-4o | Faster; sufficient for most presentations |
| SOAP note / discharge summary | GPT-4o | Speed matters; output quality is excellent |
| Drug interaction check | o1 | Reasoning depth catches subtle interactions |
| Patient education letter | GPT-4o | Fast; excellent at tone and reading level |
| Literature question ("Is there evidence for X?") | GPT-4o + web browsing | Built-in search in ChatGPT |
| Rare disease workup | o3 | Extended reasoning handles sparse evidence well |
| Board-style question analysis | o1 or o3 | Step-by-step reasoning matches vignette format |

## Weaknesses

- **Confusing model lineup**: GPT-4o, o1, o1-mini, o1-pro, o3, o3-mini: the naming is opaque and the differences are not intuitive for non-technical users
- **o-series is slow**: o3 in particular takes significantly longer to respond than GPT-4o; not suitable for real-time note dictation
- **Consumer tiers are not HIPAA-compliant**: the most accessible tiers (Free, Plus, Pro) cannot be used with patient data; enterprise setup adds friction
- **Hallucination risk**: like all LLMs, the o-series can produce confident but incorrect clinical details; extended reasoning reduces but does not eliminate errors
- **No persistent memory by default**: each session starts fresh; you must re-establish patient context manually

OpenAI's Enterprise tier includes:
- Business Associate Agreement (BAA)
- Data not used for model training
- SOC 2 Type II compliance
- Admin controls and audit logging
- SSO / SCIM provisioning

Individual physicians without enterprise access should use only de-identified data. When in doubt, remove all 18 HIPAA identifiers before pasting into any consumer AI product.

## Bottom Line

If structured diagnostic reasoning is your primary use case, the o-series models are the best available tools as of early 2026. The chain-of-thought approach genuinely mirrors careful clinical thinking in a way that faster models do not. For routine note-writing and documentation tasks, GPT-4o is fast and reliable.

The practical barrier is HIPAA compliance: the consumer tiers that most physicians use daily do not include a BAA. Organizations willing to set up enterprise agreements gain access to a genuinely powerful clinical reasoning tool.
