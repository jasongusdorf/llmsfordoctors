## Study Design

Most evaluations of medical LLMs are static and single-turn: a question goes in, an answer comes out, and the answer is graded. Real consultations are nothing like that. They unfold over multiple turns, the patient's story is incomplete and sometimes contradictory, and the clinician has to decide what to ask next. This preprint proposes AIPatient Arena, a framework that tries to grade the whole workflow rather than the final answer.

The system turns real electronic health record (EHR) data into patient-specific knowledge graphs, which then drive a simulated patient the model can interview across multiple turns. Each consultation is scored on eight dimensions of clinical competence, spanning how the model gathers information, reasons about it, and communicates. The authors ran it on a primary cohort of 437 patients and two out-of-distribution external validation cohorts (119 and 67 patients) to check that the patterns held outside the development set.

## Key Findings

Scores are mean values on a 1 to 5 scale, reported as ranges across the models tested.

- **Strong where the stakes are lower.** Models did well on medical interview questioning skills (4.43 to 4.99), ethical and professional conduct (4.38 to 4.93), and clarity of clinical explanations (3.80 to 4.72). They ask reasonable questions and explain themselves clearly.
- **Middling on synthesis and safety.** Information integration (3.19 to 4.21) and medication safety and justification (3.13 to 3.78) landed in the moderate band.
- **Weak exactly where it matters.** The lowest scores were in the most clinically consequential dimensions: handling ambiguous patient responses (2.57 to 3.32), information coverage (2.08 to 3.02), and diagnostic accuracy and reasoning (2.63 to 3.55).
- **The process failures were specific and recurrent.** Looking at the consultations themselves, the authors found repetitive questioning, omission of past medical history, inadequate handling of uncertainty, and limited empathy.
- **More context helped diagnosis, not treatment.** Giving the model richer conversational history improved its diagnostic reasoning but produced limited gains in treatment planning.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The core argument is methodological: a model can score well on final-answer benchmarks while failing the process. Here the same models that interviewed competently still missed information and reached weak diagnoses, and only a turn-by-turn evaluation made that visible. That is the contribution worth taking seriously, more than any single dimension score.
</Callout>

## Clinical Implications

1. **A smooth interview is not a safe one.** These models are good at sounding like a competent clinician, asking sensible questions, and explaining clearly, while being weak at covering everything that matters and landing the right diagnosis. The fluent surface is exactly what makes the gaps easy to miss.

2. **Watch the parts that do not show up in a transcript.** The recurring failures (skipping past medical history, repeating questions, not chasing an ambiguous answer) are process errors. A reviewer reading only the model's final summary would not catch them. If you are piloting a consultation tool, evaluate the conversation, not just the output.

3. **This grades models, not products, and on simulated patients.** The patients here are EHR-derived knowledge-graph simulations, not real people, and the cohorts are specific. The numbers describe how these models behave in this arena, not how a given commercial tool will perform in your clinic.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  This is a preprint and has not been peer-reviewed. It also relies on a simulated patient built from EHR data and on LLM-assisted scoring of clinical competence, so some of the evaluation machinery shares the same failure modes it is measuring. Treat the specific numbers as directional and the ranking of strengths and weaknesses as the durable takeaway.
</Callout>

## My Takeaway

The shape of the result is more telling than any single score. On a 1 to 5 scale these models looked strong on the visible craft of a consultation, questioning skills 4.43 to 4.99, ethical conduct 4.38 to 4.93, explanation clarity 3.80 to 4.72, and weak exactly where it counts: handling ambiguous patient responses 2.57 to 3.32, information coverage 2.08 to 3.02, and diagnostic accuracy 2.63 to 3.55. A model that interviews like a 4.7 and diagnoses like a 2.9 is a specific and uncomfortable profile, because the strengths are what a casual observer notices and the weaknesses are what harm a patient.

Methodologically the design is the contribution. It is EHR-grounded, multi-turn, and validated on two out-of-distribution external cohorts (119 and 67 patients) beyond the 437-patient primary set, and the process-based scoring surfaced concrete failures (repetitive questioning, omitted past medical history, poor handling of uncertainty) that a final-answer benchmark would hide. The limits are equally clear: it is a preprint with no resolvable DOI yet, the patients are EHR-derived knowledge-graph simulations rather than real people, and the competence scoring leans on LLM assistance, so part of the evaluation shares the weaknesses it measures. The numbers are best read as a competence profile, not a precise grade.

The practical implication is a testing discipline. If you are piloting a consultation or intake tool, judge the whole conversation, not the tidy summary it produces at the end, because that is where the missed history and the unchased ambiguous answer live. Fluency is not safety, and this is direct evidence that the two can come apart in the same model.
