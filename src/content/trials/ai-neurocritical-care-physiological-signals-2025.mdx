## Study Design

Park et al. review emerging applications of AI and machine learning in neurocritical care, focusing on how continuous physiological data streams (electroencephalography (EEG), intracranial pressure (ICP), and cerebral blood flow) can be used to detect subclinical deterioration and guide early treatment. The review examines three illustrative clinical scenarios: delayed cerebral ischemia detection, real-time seizure detection, and continuous physiological "nowcasting" for prognostication.

---

## Key Findings

- **Neurocritical care is uniquely suited for AI**: rich continuous data streams, narrow therapeutic windows, and clinical decisions that depend on detecting subtle changes in brain physiology
- Current clinical practice relies on **intermittent neurological examination**, which misses subclinical deterioration in sedated or intubated patients
- AI systems can perform **continuous "nowcasting"** (real-time estimation of brain state from physiological signals) to detect deterioration before it becomes clinically apparent
- **Delayed cerebral ischemia** (a major cause of morbidity after subarachnoid hemorrhage) can potentially be predicted from EEG and cerebral blood flow patterns before symptoms manifest
- **Real-time seizure detection** from continuous EEG could eliminate the current delay between seizure onset and treatment, which is critical because seizure duration directly correlates with brain damage
- Most AI tools in this domain **remain in developmental or validation stages**; clinical translation requires rigorous testing, workflow integration, and regulatory consideration

<Callout type="evidence">
  The neurocritical care unit is one of the most data-rich environments in medicine: continuous EEG, ICP monitoring, cerebral blood flow measurements, and multimodal physiological streams. AI's ability to synthesize these signals in real time addresses a genuine clinical gap: human clinicians cannot continuously monitor all these data streams simultaneously.
</Callout>

---

## Clinical Implications

1. **The therapeutic window problem.** In neurocritical care, the window for effective intervention is often hours or less. AI systems that provide minutes or hours of advance warning before clinical deterioration could fundamentally change outcomes.

2. **Sedated patients are the biggest unmet need.** When patients are sedated and intubated, the neurological exam is severely limited. Continuous AI-driven physiological monitoring could provide the "examination" that sedation makes impossible.

3. **Integration is the challenge, not algorithms.** The mathematical models exist. The barriers are workflow integration, alarm fatigue, clinician trust, and regulatory pathways for real-time clinical decision support.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  Most of the AI systems reviewed are in validation stages with retrospective data. The gap between detecting a pattern in retrospective data and providing actionable, reliable alerts in real time (without overwhelming clinicians with false alarms) is substantial and largely unaddressed.
</Callout>

---

## My Takeaway

This review maps an important future for clinical AI: continuous physiological monitoring with machine learning interpretation in settings where timing is everything. The neurocritical care unit is the right proving ground: the data is rich, the stakes are high, and the current approach (intermittent manual assessment) is clearly inadequate for detecting subclinical changes. The gap between the promise and reality is that most of these systems are still in research. The field needs fewer retrospective validation studies and more prospective clinical trials with patient-centered outcomes to move these tools from bench to bedside.
