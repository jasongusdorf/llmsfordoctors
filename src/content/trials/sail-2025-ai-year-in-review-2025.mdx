## Study Design

This perspective from the Symposium on AI for Learning Health Systems (SAIL) 2025 synthesizes the key developments in clinical AI from 2024 to 2025, curated by a committee of researchers and clinicians through an extensive literature review, industry reports, and news analysis. The review covers six themes: clinical documentation, clinical decision support, insurance denials, clinician-AI interaction, AI regulation, and healthcare economics.

---

## Key Findings

- **Ambient documentation** is the most widely adopted clinical AI application, with universal adoption activities reported across 43 surveyed health systems
- An AI ECG alert system demonstrated a **17% reduction in 90-day mortality** in hospitalized patients (3.6% vs 4.3%, HR 0.83)
- Real-time wearable sensor data led to **38% fewer heart failure rehospitalizations** in 90 days
- A multicenter AI intracranial hemorrhage triage trial showed **no improvement** in sensitivity, specificity, or turnaround times, proving not all AI tools help in practice
- AI-assisted mammography screening found **17.6% more breast cancers** without raising false recalls (PRAIM study)
- Health insurers are **using AI to autodeny claims** with minimal human review, prompting lawsuits
- Burnout dropped from **77% to 51%** in one multicenter cohort after AI scribe implementation
- A large longitudinal study showed **no significant reduction in documentation time** from ambient AI despite user satisfaction

<Callout type="evidence">
  This review captures a pivotal moment: 2024–2025 was the year AI moved from research to implementation at scale. The evidence is mixed: some applications deliver clear patient benefits (ECG mortality reduction), while others show no improvement (hemorrhage triage). The field is maturing beyond hype into nuanced evaluation.
</Callout>

---

## Clinical Implications

1. **Documentation AI is adopted but unproven.** Near-universal adoption based on user satisfaction, despite inconsistent evidence of actual time savings or efficiency gains. Institutions are buying enthusiasm, not evidence.

2. **Decision support has breakthrough results, sometimes.** The ECG mortality reduction and heart failure rehospitalization results are practice-changing. But the hemorrhage triage failure shows that retrospective accuracy doesn't guarantee real-world impact.

3. **AI in insurance is a growing crisis.** AI-driven claim denials, patients flagged by AI but denied coverage, and the emerging "financial toxicity" of AI-enabled diagnosis are issues the clinical community needs to confront.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  The review highlights that AI-generated documentation hallucinations are a real safety concern, and that clinician automation bias (uncritically accepting AI output) compounds the risk. As AI notes become the norm, the medical record may become less trustworthy.
</Callout>

4. **Clinician-AI collaboration depends on context.** AI-assisted mammography shows clear synergy; AI-assisted diagnostic reasoning shows uncertain benefit. The "doctor plus AI" equation is task-dependent, not universally positive.

---

## My Takeaway

This is the definitive snapshot of where clinical AI stands right now. The headline is that AI has arrived in clinical practice, not as a future promise but as a present reality. The substance behind the headline is more complex: the tools that work best (ECG alerts, structured screening) are specific, well-defined, and integrated into clear clinical workflows. The tools that struggle (documentation time savings, hemorrhage triage) are broader, more ambiguous, or deployed without supporting workflow changes. For clinicians and administrators, the message is clear: evaluate AI tools by rigorous outcomes, not adoption rates or user satisfaction.
