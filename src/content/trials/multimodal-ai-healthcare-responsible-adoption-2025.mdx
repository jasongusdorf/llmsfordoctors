## Study Design

Azarfar and colleagues reviewed the state of multimodal AI in health care on behalf of the Alliance of Centers of Artificial Intelligence in Medicine. The paper synthesizes findings from a 2024 symposium at the University of Toronto (T-CAIREM) and a structured PubMed search of 597 records published between January 2015 and May 2024. It is not a systematic review with pooled effect sizes. It is a narrative review organized around five questions: what multimodal AI is, why it matters in health care, how to build a framework for it, what challenges remain, and where the field is headed. The authors draw on applications in sepsis diagnosis, cardiology, oncology, neonatal care, and paediatric asthma to illustrate both the potential and the failure modes.

---

## Key Findings

- **Early fusion multimodal models outperformed single-modality counterparts by 6 to 33%** across various health care applications, using the Holistic AI in Medicine framework that integrates tabular, time-series, textual, and image data

- **Three fusion architectures define current approaches.** Early fusion combines modalities after individual encoding but may miss subtle cross-modal relationships. Joint fusion encodes modalities iteratively with feedback linked to output loss, improving feature optimization. Late fusion trains separate models per modality and aggregates their outputs, but risks losing inter-modal correlations.

- **No standardized benchmark exists for evaluating multimodal AI models in health care.** The authors recommend starting with single-modality baselines, then comparing fusion techniques to identify the best approach for a given clinical task.

- **Cross-modal transfer learning reduces the need for labelled data.** CT2US, a kidney ultrasound segmentation model, trained on synthetic ultrasound images derived from CT scans and generalized effectively to real ultrasound data despite limited labelled training sets.

- **Data availability varies by modality and timing.** Demographics and vital signs are captured at check-in. Labs and imaging arrive hours to days later. Wearable sensor and omics data are collected in research settings only. This staggered availability means a multimodal model may operate on partial information for long stretches of a clinical encounter.

- **Different explanation techniques for the same AI decision can yield contradictory results.** Shapley Additive Explanations and Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations sometimes generate varying attributions for identical predictions, complicating clinician trust and regulatory oversight.

- **Anonymised multimodal data can be re-identified.** Despite differential privacy and anonymization, the combination of modalities creates a richer signal for re-identification attacks, particularly when models trained on private hospital records face adversarial prompts.

- **Paediatric populations face compounding disadvantages.** Scarce paediatric-specific training data, slower regulatory approval pathways, and smaller commercial markets reduce the likelihood that children will benefit from multimodal AI at the same pace as adults.

<Callout type="evidence">
  The 6 to 33% performance gain from multimodal fusion is drawn from the Holistic AI in Medicine framework, which used early fusion with interpretable models like XGBoost. That range is wide enough to suggest the benefit depends heavily on the clinical task and the quality of individual modalities. Single-modality baselines should be established before any multimodal deployment to confirm the added complexity is justified.
</Callout>

## Clinical Implications

1. **Multimodal AI does not eliminate the need for clinical judgment; it adds to the cognitive load of evaluating it.** A model that integrates labs, imaging, notes, and vital signs produces predictions that are harder to audit than a model that reads one chest X-ray. Clinicians should ask how each modality contributed to a given recommendation before acting on it.

2. **Sepsis is a natural proving ground, and the gaps are visible.** The review opens with a clinical vignette of a 57-year-old immunocompromised transplant patient whose sepsis diagnosis required synthesizing history, exam, labs, imaging, and paracentesis. No single data source was sufficient. Multimodal AI could, in theory, integrate these streams in real time, but no validated system does so yet.

3. **Liability for multimodal AI errors remains undefined.** When a prediction draws on five data sources and three fusion layers, assigning fault among the physician, the health system, the AI developer, and the software manufacturer is an open legal question. The authors argue that liability should be shared, but no jurisdiction has codified this.

4. **Validation must account for missing modalities, not just complete datasets.** In practice, data arrives at different times during a patient encounter. A model validated only on complete multimodal inputs may perform unpredictably when, say, imaging results are unavailable for the first six hours. The authors recommend testing models under realistic missingness conditions.

5. **Federated learning and homomorphic encryption are proposed solutions for privacy, but neither is production-ready in health care.** Blockchain-based decentralization, federated model training, and computation on encrypted data each address parts of the privacy problem. All require further development before clinical deployment.

<Callout type="pitfall">
  A multimodal AI model validated on complete datasets may degrade when modalities are missing, which is the norm in clinical practice. Lab results arrive hours after vital signs. Imaging may not be ordered at all. Before adopting any multimodal tool, ask the vendor how it performs when one or more data sources are unavailable, and whether that scenario was included in validation.
</Callout>

## My Takeaway

The core tension in this review is familiar: technical capability outpaces clinical validation. Multimodal AI can combine data sources that clinicians already synthesize mentally, and early fusion models show measurable gains over single-modality approaches. The problem is that none of the safeguards required for responsible deployment exist yet. There is no standard benchmark. Liability frameworks are absent. Bias can compound across modalities in ways that are harder to detect than in single-source systems. The review is honest about this gap, which makes it more useful than most. For clinicians, the practical question is not whether multimodal AI will arrive, but whether the governance, validation, and interpretability infrastructure will be in place when it does.
