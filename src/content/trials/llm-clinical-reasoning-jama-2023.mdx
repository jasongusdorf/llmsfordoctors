## Study Design

This study evaluated a large language model on a series of NEJM Clinical Problem-Solving cases, one of the most demanding diagnostic reasoning formats in published medicine. These cases are intentionally complex, multi-step, and designed to challenge even experienced clinicians. They require synthesizing history, exam findings, laboratory data, and imaging in sequence, without presenting the final diagnosis until the end.

The LLM was scored on both final diagnosis accuracy and the quality of intermediate reasoning steps. Physician performance on the same cases served as the benchmark comparator. Cases spanned a wide range of specialties and included a deliberate mix of rare diseases, common diseases with atypical presentations, and diagnostic red herrings.

---

## Key Findings

- The LLM achieved diagnostic accuracy **comparable to or exceeding attending physicians** across the NEJM case series
- Error patterns were **systematically different** between AI and physicians:
  - AI performed well on **rare diseases**, unconstrained by availability bias, it considers low-probability diagnoses that physicians may not surface
  - AI performed poorly on **atypical presentations of common diseases**; it pattern-matches to typical presentations and fails when familiar diseases behave unusually
  - Physicians showed the inverse: stronger on atypical presentations of common conditions, weaker on rare disease recognition
- LLM errors tended to be **confident**; the model expressed high certainty even when wrong, with little calibrated uncertainty
- Performance was notably stronger when the model was given the full case narrative upfront versus a stepwise presentation

---

## Clinical Implications

The headline finding (comparable accuracy) is real, but the more important finding is the complementary error structure. AI and physicians are not failing in the same ways. That asymmetry is clinically significant.

1. **Availability bias is the physician's vulnerability.** Physicians over-index on diagnoses they have recently seen or commonly encounter. LLMs have no recency bias; they draw from the full training distribution equally.

2. **Pattern matching is the AI's vulnerability.** LLMs are extraordinarily good at matching presentations to prototypical disease templates. When a patient's presentation deviates from the prototype, the model may not recognize the deviation as meaningful.

3. **Confidence without calibration is dangerous.** An LLM that says "this is almost certainly X" when it is actually wrong provides false reassurance. Physicians should treat LLM certainty expressions with skepticism.

4. **The workflow implication is specific.** The value of LLMs is highest in situations where you want to pressure-test your differential, particularly for rare disease consideration and cognitive debiasing against anchoring errors.

<Callout type="evidence">
  This study provides the strongest published evidence that LLMs function as effective cognitive complements to physician reasoning. The finding is not that AI is as good as doctors; it is that AI and doctors are wrong about different things, which makes them genuinely useful in combination.
</Callout>

## My Takeaway

The real finding here is simple: **AI and physicians are wrong about different things.**

That sentence should change how you think about LLMs in clinical practice. The question is not "can AI replace me?" but "can AI catch what I am likely to miss?" Based on this study, the answer is yes, in a specific and predictable way: LLMs are strongest where physicians are most vulnerable (rare diseases, availability bias) and weakest where physicians are strongest (atypical presentations, clinical gestalt).

This is the empirical basis for using LLMs as a **second opinion** rather than a primary reasoner. You bring clinical judgment, physical exam interpretation, and pattern recognition for common-disease variants. The LLM brings breadth, no recency bias, and structured consideration of rare diagnoses you might not have surfaced.

## Have these findings been replicated?

Yes. The core finding that LLMs perform at or near physician level on diagnostic reasoning has been replicated and extended in subsequent studies. Most notably, a [2024 NEJM study](/trials/llm-diagnostic-nejm-2024) evaluated LLMs on diagnostic reasoning with a larger, more rigorous design and confirmed that LLMs achieve physician-comparable diagnostic accuracy on structured clinical cases. The complementary error pattern identified here (AI stronger on rare diseases, physicians stronger on atypical presentations of common diseases) has also been observed in subsequent evaluations.

However, important caveats remain. All replication studies, including the NEJM 2024 study, have used vignette-based designs rather than real-world clinical encounters. The finding that LLMs match physicians on standardized cases is now well-established; the question of whether this translates to clinical value at the bedside remains open. Additionally, the models tested have evolved substantially since 2023, and current-generation LLMs likely outperform the model evaluated in this study on benchmark tasks, though the fundamental error patterns (overconfidence, weakness on atypical presentations) persist across model generations.

This study remains important as the foundational evidence that LLMs and physicians have complementary error structures, a finding that has held up across multiple subsequent evaluations and forms the conceptual basis for using LLMs as cognitive aids rather than diagnostic replacements.
